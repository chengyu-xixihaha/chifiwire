If you own a pair of Sony headphones or earbuds, you may want to check the Sony Headphones Connect app before your next update. According to a report from SoundGuys published June 11, 2026, Sony is in the process of sunsetting certain features for older devices — meaning some of the functionality you've been using could quietly disappear after an app or firmware update.

## What's Being Removed

Sony has not published a sweeping all-at-once removal, but the pattern is clear: features that once required software infrastructure to support — things like advanced equalizer customization options, specific 360 Reality Audio settings, personalized noise canceling calibration tools, and Speak-to-Chat configuration — are being scaled back or removed entirely for older device generations.

The affected products are expected to include older models in the WH-1000XM line (think XM3 and XM4 era), certain LinkBuds configurations, and select WF-series true wireless earbuds. Newer flagship models are unlikely to be impacted in the near term, since Sony's development resources will naturally concentrate on supporting current hardware.

It's worth being clear: the headphones themselves won't stop working. You can still use them as Bluetooth headphones. What's being removed are software-side features that lived in the companion app and depended on Sony's backend infrastructure to function. When Sony pulls the plug on those backend services, the app features that rely on them go with it.

## Why Companies Do This

This kind of feature sunset is uncomfortable to accept, but the reasoning from a business perspective is straightforward.

Every app feature that touches a cloud backend costs money to maintain — servers, security updates, API upkeep. When a device is several years old, the number of active users still relying on those features shrinks. At some point, Sony's engineers make a call: the cost of keeping the backend alive for a shrinking user base no longer pencils out.

There's also an unspoken commercial incentive at work. Removing premium features from older hardware makes newer models more attractive by comparison. If your WH-1000XM4 suddenly loses a feature you actually use, the upgrade path to the XM6 starts looking more appealing. Whether Sony is being purely pragmatic or nudging users toward a purchase is impossible to say from the outside — probably some of both.

## This Is Bigger Than Sony

It would be easy to call this out as a Sony-specific problem, but that would miss the bigger picture. Feature sunsetting in audio hardware is becoming an industry-wide pattern.

Bose has removed features from older QuietComfort and Noise Cancelling Headphones models through app updates. JBL has similarly scaled back software support for aging earbuds. The underlying issue is structural: when audio companies shifted from purely hardware products to "hardware + app ecosystem" products over the past decade, they took on ongoing software liabilities that they're now quietly unwinding for older devices.

The uncomfortable truth is that when you buy a modern pair of wireless headphones, you're not just buying hardware — you're buying into a software platform that the manufacturer controls and can change at any time. The headphones may last a decade physically. The software promises are shorter.

## What You Should Do

**Check Sony's official communications first.** Sony should be sending notices to affected users and posting details on their support pages. Before assuming the worst, verify exactly which features are being removed from your specific model.

**Decide whether the affected features matter to you.** If you set your EQ once two years ago and never touched it again, a removal of advanced EQ options may not affect your day-to-day experience at all. Triage what you actually use before treating this as a crisis.

**Export or document your current settings.** If you have a heavily customized EQ profile or personalized sound settings, note them down or screenshot them before any update locks you out. Some app features allow settings export — check if yours does.

**Think carefully before updating immediately.** If you're on a firmware version that still supports the features you care about, you don't have to rush to update. That said, holding back on updates indefinitely creates its own security and compatibility risks over time.

**Consider whether this changes your next purchase.** Some audio enthusiasts are increasingly factoring in "software longevity" when choosing headphones — looking at companies with a track record of long-term support, or opting for simpler devices with fewer cloud dependencies. Sony's premium build quality and ANC performance remain excellent; this is a software support question, not a hardware quality one.

## The Bottom Line

Sony making these changes doesn't mean your headphones are broken. It does mean the feature set you signed up for at purchase may shrink over the device's lifetime — and that's worth knowing about.

The broader lesson: in a world where "smart" devices depend on software ecosystems to unlock their full potential, the fine print around long-term support matters more than it used to. Check what you're losing, protect the settings you care about, and factor software support longevity into your next audio purchase.
