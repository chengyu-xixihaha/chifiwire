7Hz has announced the Legato, a new single dynamic driver IEM priced at $139 that sits above the Salnotes Zero 2 in the company's lineup. The defining hardware choice is a 12mm beryllium-coated diaphragm — an unusual material at this price point — which 7Hz says delivers faster transient response and extended high-frequency extension compared to the polyurethane drivers typically found in budget single-DD IEMs.

## What Beryllium Coating Means at $139

True beryllium diaphragms are expensive to produce and mostly appear in IEMs priced $500 and up (Campfire Audio, Final Audio's A series). Beryllium-coated diaphragms — a thin beryllium layer deposited over a polymer base — carry many of the acoustic benefits at a fraction of the cost.

The key property is stiffness-to-weight ratio. A stiffer diaphragm can move faster and stop more accurately, which translates to improved transient attack (how quickly a drum hit starts and stops) and reduced distortion in the upper frequencies. Whether that improvement is audible at $139 depends on implementation, but beryllium coating is not marketing fluff — it's a genuine material science choice with measurable acoustic consequences.

The 12mm driver size is on the larger end for a single DD, which typically means stronger bass authority and more physical slam. 7Hz has tuned the Legato toward a W-shaped response: elevated sub-bass, slightly recessed upper midrange, and airy high-frequency extension. This is a crowd-friendly signature rather than a reference or neutral one.

## Design and Build

The Legato uses a machined aluminum shell with a faceplated resin accent. At first glance it resembles the Salnotes Zero 2's understated aesthetic, but the Legato is larger and heavier — partly due to the aluminum construction, partly due to the larger driver housing.

Cable is a 6-strand silver-plated copper terminated in 3.5mm single-ended, with a 0.78mm 2-pin connector. 4.4mm balanced version is available as a separate purchase for $18 more. 7Hz uses its standard semi-rigid ear hook design that has proven popular for fit stability, particularly during movement.

Three colorways at launch: Midnight Black, Glacier Blue, and a limited Translucent (which shows the driver housing through the faceplate).

## How It Fits the 7Hz Lineup

7Hz now has a cleaner tiered structure:
- **Salnotes Zero 2** (~$30): entry-level, warm W-shape, polycarbonate shell
- **Legato** ($139): mid-tier, beryllium DD, aluminum shell, airier treble
- **Timeless AE** (~$220): planar magnetic, technical flagship

The Legato fills a gap the company hasn't addressed since the original Salnotes Dioko. In the $100-$150 single-DD segment, it competes directly with the Moondrop Aria 2 ($89), Simgot EA500 ($99), and Tanchjim Origin ($129).

## Early Impressions

Pre-release review units have gone to several community reviewers. The consistent report is that the sub-bass and treble air are the highlights — the Legato has noticeable physical slam and a sense of openness in the upper frequencies that most competitors at this price don't match. The midrange is described as slightly recessed, which makes vocals step back in the mix relative to the bass and treble response.

For genres like EDM, hip-hop, and cinematic scores, that tuning is likely to land well. For vocal-forward music or acoustic jazz, the Aria 2 or Simgot EA500 may be a better fit.

## Availability

The Legato is available now through Linsoul, HifiGo, and 7Hz's own store at $139 for the 3.5mm version and $157 for the 4.4mm balanced variant. Shipping begins June 20.
