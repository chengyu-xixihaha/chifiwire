Moondrop has officially launched the **Armature Art 24** — a 24-driver-per-side balanced armature flagship IEM at **¥6,999 (≈ $1,030)**. The Armature Art 24 is one of the densest BA configurations in any production IEM and marks Moondrop's most ambitious technical exercise to date.

## Driver layout

Each side packs **24 balanced armatures** split as:

- **16 BAs for bass**
- **4 BAs for mid-high**
- **4 BAs for ultra-high**

This is unusual for two reasons:
1. Most flagship IEMs use **hybrid configurations** (dynamic + BA) because a single dynamic typically delivers better sub-bass than BAs. 16 BAs for bass is Moondrop committing to a pure-BA approach at full bandwidth.
2. The driver layout is **single-sided acoustic design** — all 24 BAs fire through a single shared acoustic path, rather than splitting drivers into multiple bores.

## Where it slots in

At $1,030, the Armature Art 24 is **competing with the established multi-BA flagships**: 64 Audio Trio, Vision Ears VE8, Empire Ears Legend X. Moondrop is the most affordable of this group — many BA-flagship competitors run $1,500-3,000.

The closest in-house comparison: Moondrop's **Variations** ($520) — a 1DD + 2EST + 2BA tribrid that's been the brand's reference IEM for the last two years. The Armature Art 24 is the next tier up, both in price and in driver count.

## What we'd want to know

Moondrop's official announcement is light on what's audibly different. Key questions:

- **Sub-bass implementation**: how does 16-BA bass actually perform vs a single 10mm dynamic on extension and slam?
- **Coherence**: 24 drivers per side require precise crossover work. Are the handoffs audible?
- **Driver coloration**: BA tonality has a known "plasticky" character at high driver counts. Has Moondrop solved this with the AA24?
- **Source requirements**: 24-BA flagships typically need real desktop amps. Does the AA24 follow this pattern or is it phone-driveable?
