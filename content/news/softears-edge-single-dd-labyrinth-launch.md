Softears launched the Edge on June 1, 2026, priced at $499. It's the first model in the company's new "Lifestyle Listen (LS)" series, and its central argument is straightforward: one well-engineered driver beats a stack of mediocre ones.

## What Is Softears, and Why Does It Matter

Softears spun out from Moondrop, one of the most influential tuning houses in the ChiFi space. That lineage matters. Moondrop's house sound — neutral-bright, technically resolving, built on rigorous acoustic research — defined a generation of IEMs. Softears carries that engineering DNA while positioning itself as a premium, more design-forward offshoot. The Edge is its clearest statement yet of what that means at flagship pricing.

## The Labyrinth Chamber, Explained

The headline engineering feature is a labyrinth-structured internal acoustic chamber machined into the titanium alloy CNC housing. The term sounds abstract, so here's what it actually does.

A dynamic driver moves air. When it does, pressure waves bounce around inside the housing — some constructively, most destructively. Those reflected waves arrive back at the driver at irregular intervals, creating standing waves: resonant frequencies that color the sound and blur transient detail.

A labyrinth chamber solves this by replacing a simple hollow cavity with a folded, maze-like internal path. Air has to travel through a longer, more controlled route before it dissipates. This does two things: it regulates airflow so the driver's back pressure stays consistent across the frequency range, and it breaks up the reflections before they can build into standing waves. The result, in theory, is a cleaner, more controlled low end and better-defined transients — without adding drivers or crossover complexity.

It's the same principle used in high-end speaker enclosures, applied at IEM scale. Getting the geometry right inside a housing small enough to sit in your ear canal is genuinely difficult manufacturing work.

## Driver and Specs

The Edge uses an 11mm dynamic driver — large by IEM standards, which helps with air displacement and bass extension. The titanium alloy housing is CNC-machined, keeping resonance low and providing a consistent acoustic environment for the labyrinth geometry to function as designed.

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| Driver | 11mm dynamic driver |
| Housing | Titanium alloy CNC |
| Impedance | 16Ω |
| Sensitivity | 128 dB/Vrms |
| THD+N | <1% |
| Cable | 7N OCC copper silver-plated, 4.4mm balanced |
| Price | $499 |

The 16Ω impedance and 128dB sensitivity make it easy to drive from a dongle DAC or portable source — no desktop stack required.

## The Case for One Driver

At $499, the Edge sits in a segment crowded with tribrid and multi-BA designs that compete on driver count. Softears is making a deliberate counter-argument: a single DD with correct acoustic design can outperform more complex topologies on coherence, timbre, and dynamic range, because there are no crossover phase issues and no driver mismatch artifacts to manage.

Whether the labyrinth chamber delivers enough technical resolution to justify that position against the competition is a listening question. The engineering rationale, at least, is sound.

The Softears Edge is available now at $499.
