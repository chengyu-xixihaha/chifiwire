The original Moondrop Aria didn't just sell well — it set the expectation for what a $79 IEM should sound like. Four years and several variants later (Snow Edition, Aria SE), Moondrop has rolled out the proper sequel: the **Aria 2**, $89.99, single dynamic driver, new diaphragm, modular cable. The question isn't whether it's good — Moondrop knows how to tune at this price. The question is whether the refinements are enough to justify the buy if you already own the original, or whether someone coming fresh into the hobby should pick this over a Truthear Hexa or a Letshuoer S08.

Short answer: **the Aria 2 is the best single-DD IEM Moondrop has shipped under $100**, but it's not a slam-dunk over its tribrid competition. Read on for why.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm dynamic, TiN ceramic-coated spherical dome composite diaphragm |
| **Impedance** | 33 Ω ± 15% @ 1kHz |
| **Sensitivity** | 122 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency Response** | 16 Hz – 22 kHz (IEC61094) / 20 Hz – 20 kHz (-3 dB, IEC60318-4) |
| **THD** | ≤ 0.05% @ 1kHz |
| **Shell** | CNC-milled zinc alloy cavity, replaceable brass nozzle |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin detachable, **modular plug** — 3.5mm single-ended **and** 4.4mm balanced included |
| **Target curve** | Moondrop VDSF (their Harman-derived in-house target) |
| **In the box** | IEMs, cable, 3.5mm + 4.4mm plugs, eartips, hard case, manual |
| **MSRP** | $89.99 |

The hardware story is the upgrade: **the diaphragm and the modular cable**. The original Aria used a beryllium-coated dome; the Aria 2 switches to TiN (titanium nitride) ceramic-coated composite — Moondrop's argument is lower distortion and better transient response. Empirically, that holds up. The other change you actually notice on day one is the cable: **3.5mm and 4.4mm interchangeable plugs in the box**, no upgrade required. At $90, that used to be a $30 add-on; now it's stock.

## Who it's for

- **First-time audiophile** who's outgrown $20 IEMs and wants something that doesn't need explanation. The Aria 2 sounds "right" out of the box on any source.
- **Someone who liked the original Aria** but found it slightly bright or thin. The Aria 2's mid-bass and treble both got the rough edges sanded off.
- **Balanced-output owners** who don't want to pay extra for a 4.4mm cable. The modular plug alone is worth the $10 premium over the original Aria.

Skip it if you already own a Truthear Hexa or a Moondrop Blessing 3 — the Aria 2 is a side-grade, not an upgrade.

## Build, fit, and accessories

The Aria 2 keeps the matte gray-titanium finish of the original — that's deliberate, since the look became part of the brand. CNC-milled zinc alloy gives them real heft (these aren't featherweight plastic), but the ergonomic curve is unchanged, which means **if the original Aria fit you, the Aria 2 will**. The replaceable brass nozzle is more about durability than tuning — you can swap it if it gets clogged with wax, not for sound changes.

The included silicone tips are adequate but not great. Reviewers consistently flag this: the original Aria SE came with Moondrop's Spring Tips (genuinely excellent), and the Aria 2 inexplicably dropped those for cheaper stock tips. **Budget another $10 for Spinfit CP145 or Moondrop Spring Tips**; the sealing improvement directly translates to better bass response.

The cable itself is well-braided, low microphonics, and the modular connection feels solid (it's a screw-on collar, not a press-fit). Pin orientation is standard 0.78mm 2-pin, so any aftermarket cable will work.

## Sound

The Aria 2 follows Moondrop's **VDSF target curve** — their in-house derivative of the Harman target, biased slightly warmer in the mid-bass. For sub-$100 IEMs that lean reference, this is the textbook tuning.

### Bass

The biggest audible change from the original. Mid-bass is **meatier and punchier**, giving kick drums and bass guitars real weight without bleeding into the lower mids. Sub-bass extension is competent but slightly recessed compared to the mid-bass hump — if you listen primarily to electronic, hip-hop, or anything that needs deep sub-bass rumble, you'll find the Aria 2 polite where you want it grandstanding. For rock, pop, vocals, and acoustic genres, the bass tuning is genuinely well-judged.

### Mids

Where the Aria 2 quietly excels. Female vocals get an airy texture without the brittle peakiness that the original Aria sometimes showed. Male vocals have appropriate chest resonance. There's no upper-mid shout, no plasticky timbre — instruments sound like the instruments they're supposed to be. **This is the best Moondrop mid-range tuning under $200.**

### Treble

Tuned **comfortably, not aggressively**. The Aria 2 won't fatigue you, won't sibilate on harsh tracks, and won't bury cymbals. The trade-off: **hi-hats and crash cymbals lack the last bit of sparkle**. If you're a treble-head who wants every brushed snare to glisten, look at the Truthear Hexa or Tanchjim Origin instead. If you've ever winced at a bright IEM, the Aria 2 is the safe pick.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Surprisingly good for the price.** The stage isn't huge (these aren't open-backs), but it has real depth — vocals sit in front of you, drums are clearly behind, and there's lateral space for guitar/keys to spread without piling up. Imaging is accurate enough for casual gaming, though competitive FPS players will want something more focused.

### Technical performance

THD is genuinely low — Moondrop's spec sheet claims ≤0.05% at 1kHz, and you don't hear distortion artifacts even at higher volumes. Dynamic range and detail retrieval are class-appropriate but not exceptional; the Aria 2 won't reveal hidden layers in your library the way a $300 IEM will.

## Compared to

### vs. Truthear Hexa ($80)

The Hexa is 1DD + 3BA tribrid, brighter, more analytical, more "studio reference." The Aria 2 is 1DD, warmer in the mid-bass, smoother in the treble, more "musical reference."

- **Aria 2 wins** for: vocals (especially female), warmth, longer listening sessions, balanced cable in the box
- **Hexa wins** for: detail retrieval, treble extension, classical/instrumental, value-per-driver

If you're not sure, the Hexa is the more "impressive on first listen" option; the Aria 2 is the one you'll still be using six months later.

### vs. Moondrop Chu 2 ($20)

The Chu 2 punches absurdly above its price, sharing the VDSF tuning. The Aria 2 isn't 4x better — but the bass is tighter, the soundstage is wider, the build is leagues ahead, and the modular cable alone justifies a chunk of the price difference. If you're starting fresh and have $90 to spend, get the Aria 2. If you have $20, get the Chu 2 and don't apologize.

### vs. Letshuoer S08 ($85)

The S08 is also single DD with a Harman-derived tuning. Letshuoer leans **slightly more sub-bass-forward** and slightly more vivid in the upper treble. The Aria 2 is more polite and balanced overall. Cable accessories are roughly even (S08 also includes interchangeable plugs). Toss-up; pick by signature preference.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ The most refined VDSF tuning yet under $100
- ✅ Modular 3.5/4.4mm cable in the box — no upsell
- ✅ Excellent mid-range and vocal performance
- ✅ Smooth, non-fatiguing treble
- ✅ Build quality genuinely better than the original

**Cons**
- ❌ Sub-bass is polite, not authoritative — bass-heads should look elsewhere
- ❌ Stock eartips are a downgrade from the Aria SE's Spring Tips
- ❌ Treble lacks the last 10% of sparkle for cymbal-heavy genres
- ❌ Not enough of an upgrade to replace an original Aria if it's still working

**Buy if:** you want one IEM that handles vocals, pop, rock, and acoustic music well, sounds polished on any source, and includes everything you need (including balanced cable) in the box.

**Skip if:** you already own the original Aria and are happy, or if you prioritize sub-bass slam and treble sparkle over balance.

## Frequently asked questions

**What are the Moondrop Aria 2's specs — impedance and sensitivity?**
The Aria 2 uses a single 10mm dynamic driver with a TiN ceramic-coated composite diaphragm. Impedance is **33 Ω ± 15% @ 1kHz**, sensitivity is **122 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz**, frequency response is 16 Hz – 22 kHz, and THD is ≤ 0.05% @ 1kHz. The shell is CNC-milled zinc alloy with a replaceable brass nozzle, and the 0.78mm 2-pin cable ships with both 3.5mm single-ended and 4.4mm balanced plugs.

**Does the Moondrop Aria 2 need an amp?**
No. At 33 Ω and 122 dB/Vrms the Aria 2 is highly efficient — a phone dongle, laptop headphone jack, or any basic USB-C DAC will drive it to loud, clean volume with headroom to spare. A dedicated amp won't change the tuning or "unlock" the sound; spend that money on better eartips instead.

**When was the Moondrop Aria 2 released and how much does it cost?**
Moondrop released the Aria 2 in 2026 at an MSRP of **$89.99**, succeeding the original 2021 Aria. It's stocked at Moondrop's official store, Linsoul, HiFiGo, Apos Audio, and Bloom Audio — see the links below.

**Moondrop Aria 2 vs Truthear Hexa vs Truthear Nova — which should I buy?**

**Does the Aria 2 come with a balanced cable?**
Yes. Unlike the original Aria, the Aria 2 includes **modular 3.5mm and 4.4mm plugs in the box** — no separate balanced-cable purchase needed.

**Should I upgrade from the original Aria to the Aria 2?**
