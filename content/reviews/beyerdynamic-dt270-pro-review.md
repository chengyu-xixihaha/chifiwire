Beyerdynamic's closed-back lineup has a reputation problem. The **DT 770 Pro** is a studio institution — durable, widely used, cheap to find used — but its treble has a 6–8 kHz spike that splits opinion hard. Bright enough to be fatiguing over a long session, with a midrange some engineers describe as slightly disconnected from the rest of the frequency range. The **DT 700 Pro X** cleaned that up but pushed the price north of $200. The gap at the affordable end stayed open.

The **DT 270 Pro** is Beyerdynamic's answer: a new closed-back that trades the DT 770's divisive tuning for something more cohesive, comes in at **around $150**, folds flat for portability, and ships with a coiled cable plus a USB-C to 3.5mm adapter. Headphones.com called it "their BEST closed back headphone." That's worth testing.

First, a quick note for anyone new to the term: a *studio monitoring headphone* is tuned to reproduce audio as accurately as possible — not to flatter music, but to reveal what's actually in a recording. Engineers use them to catch mix problems, check vocals, and monitor takes during recording. That honesty is the whole point. Whether the DT 270 Pro is truly honest is the question this review sits with.

## Build and design

The DT 270 Pro is visually understated — matte black plastic chassis, metal headband adjustments, nothing flashy. At **194 g without the cable**, it is noticeably light. The cups swivel a full 135 degrees and fold flat, which is genuinely useful if the headphone lives in a backpack between sessions or moves between a home setup and a recording booth.

The coiled cable (1.3m extending to 3m) terminates in a 3.5mm plug with a 1/4-inch adapter in the box, plus Beyerdynamic's included USB-C to 3.5mm adapter for modern interfaces and laptops without a headphone jack. The cable attaches on both sides — a studio-routing design choice that some engineers like for sitting at a desk without the cord pulling from one side.

Ear pads are velour-covered and replaceable, which extends the long-term value of the headphone. The headband padding is functional but spare — reviewers at Home Studio Basics noted pressure accumulating at the top of the head after 30–45 minutes of wear. More on that under comfort.

Build quality feels solid for the price. Not premium, not flimsy. It is the kind of headphone that will survive a studio drawer for years without fuss.

## Sound quality

At 45 ohm impedance and 96 dB sensitivity, the DT 270 Pro will drive cleanly from most audio interfaces, phones, and laptops without a dedicated headphone amplifier. That is the right call at this price tier and positions it differently from higher-impedance studio cans.

The sound signature is **V-shaped** — elevated bass and treble relative to the mids. This is worth being honest about: if you expected a flat, dry monitoring tool in the mold of the classic AKG K240 or Sennheiser HD 280 Pro, this is not that. The DT 270 Pro sounds *vivid* and *punchy*, which is more enjoyable for casual listening but adds a layer of coloration for critical mix reference.

**Bass.** The low end has real extension and weight without becoming muddy. There is a clear emphasis compared to a neutral target — the kind of bass shelf that makes music feel energetic and full. Headphones.com described the presentation as "bombastic." That tracks. It is controlled and clean, not bloated — but it is not flat, and you should know that going in.

**Mids.** The midrange is where the DT 270 Pro does its best studio work. Compared to the DT 770 Pro, which sounds lean and somewhat disconnected in the mids, the DT 270 Pro integrates the frequency range more coherently. Vocals sit forward and intelligible. The upper mids can occasionally push toward "shouty" on certain vocal recordings at loud levels, but this is minor and not a session-ending problem.

**Highs.** The treble is where Beyerdynamic made the clearest improvement over the DT 770 Pro. There is no sharp 6–8 kHz spike. Headphone.com and Home Studio Basics both noted the absence of harshness, and this matches: the DT 270 Pro treble is extended and present without punishing you for listening at length. Compared to the DT 770 Pro's infamous treble peak, this alone justifies the new model's existence.

One measured concern worth naming: third-party measurements have documented elevated total harmonic distortion in the bass region at higher listening levels. This does not make the headphone unusable, but it is a genuine data point for engineers doing low-frequency mix work at loud volumes. For everything else — podcasting, vocal tracking, general reference — it is not a practical issue.

## Comfort and fit

Here is the significant caveat: **the ear pad openings are small and round**. For listeners with average to large ears, this means your ears may press against the inside of the cup rather than sitting fully inside the pad opening — functioning partly like an on-ear headphone despite the closed circumaural design. Listeners with smaller ears will likely find the fit genuinely comfortable. Listeners with larger ears need to try before committing.

The velour pads are soft and breathable, which helps during longer sessions. The headband pressure issue (noted above) is real for extended wear. The DT 270 Pro is not the headphone you wear for a 6-hour mix day without noticing it. For tracking sessions, podcasting, and 2-3 hour monitoring work, most users will be fine.

## How it compares

**Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro ($149).** The sibling and obvious comparison. The DT 770 Pro has more isolation in some pad versions and has thirty years of studio pedigree behind it. The DT 270 Pro wins on midrange coherence, treble smoothness, and the folding design. The DT 770 Pro wins on pad size and the fact that used units cost half this. For a new purchase today, the DT 270 Pro is the better-sounding headphone.

**Sony MDR-7506 ($99).** The most common studio monitoring headphone in the world and still a competent tool. The 7506 is brighter and thinner-sounding than the DT 270 Pro, with a notably analytical tuning that engineers either love or find fatiguing. The DT 270 Pro sounds fuller and more modern; the 7506 sounds more unforgiving. At $99 vs $150, the 7506 remains a legitimate budget choice.

**Audio-Technica ATH-M50x ($149).** The consumer-crossover studio standard. The M50x has a more consumer-friendly tuning (boosted bass and V-shape as well), similar portability story with the folding design, and a comparable price. The DT 270 Pro offers more midrange coherence and less fatiguing treble. Between the two, the DT 270 Pro is the more honest monitoring tool; the M50x is slightly more fun for casual listening.

## Who should buy this

The DT 270 Pro works best for:

- **Podcasters and vocal producers** who need a closed-back for monitoring while recording, where the V-shaped tuning flatters voice without obscuring clarity
- **Home studio tracking** — recording takes, punching in, monitoring a DAW mix with needed isolation
- **Anyone upgrading from the DT 770 Pro** who wants the same brand DNA with smoother treble and better midrange
- **Portable studio use** — the folding design and included USB-C adapter make this genuinely practical for working away from a fixed desk

It is a harder recommendation for:

- **Critical mix reference** — the V-shaped tuning will flatter bass-heavy mixes in ways a flat monitor would not; reach for the AKG K371 or Sennheiser HD 480 Pro if pure mix accuracy is the goal
- **Engineers with larger ears** — the pad size issue is real and personal fit matters
- **All-day session work** — the headband pressure and pad fit may add up over a very long day

## Verdict

The Beyerdynamic DT 270 Pro does what Headphones.com's headline claims: it is the most coherently tuned, least fatiguing closed-back Beyerdynamic has made at this price. The treble is fixed. The midrange is better integrated. The folding design and low impedance make it genuinely versatile.

The honest qualifier is that "best Beyerdynamic closed-back" is not the same as "flat studio reference monitor." The V-shaped tuning adds energy and color that more neutral options do not have. If you need that last degree of analytical flatness, look at the K371 or spend up to the HD 480 Pro. If you want a well-built, comfortable-enough, vivid-sounding closed-back for tracking, podcasting, and home recording at $150 — this is one of the better options in its class right now.
