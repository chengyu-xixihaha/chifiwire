Simgot's [EW200 ($40)]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-ew200-review" >}}) is on our [best under $50 list]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-iems-under-50-2026" >}}) for a reason — DLC diaphragm + competent tuning at a budget price. The **EW300 at $69** is the natural step up — same brand DNA, but with a **genuinely unique tribrid driver layout**: one dynamic + one micro-planar + one piezoelectric. At this price, this combination doesn't exist anywhere else.

Short version: **the EW300 is the most technically interesting IEM under $80 in 2026**. The tribrid setup isn't a marketing gimmick — it actually delivers measurable advantages over single-DD or DD+BA hybrids at this price.

The headline: **6mm annular planar driver in a $69 IEM**. Planar drivers normally start at $150+ (Letshuoer S12 Pro). Simgot put a small planar in for upper-mid duties, and it works.

## Who it's for

- **EW200 owner** wanting the next step up without breaking $100

## Build, fit, accessories

Aluminum metal shells with a smooth ergonomic shape — comfortable for long sessions. Lighter than fully-metal options like [Aria 2]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) but more premium than resin alternatives.

**Three swappable nozzles** with different damping profiles:
- **Reference** (the default) — Harman-derived balanced tuning
- **Warmer** — rolls off treble, lifts mid-bass
- **Brighter** — adds upper-treble sparkle for detail-focused listening

The nozzle effect is **audible but moderate** (~2-3 dB shifts in target bands), similar to Tanchjim Origin's nozzle system at 4x the price.

Accessories: carrying case, cable, 6 sets of tuning foam, 8 ring gaskets for nozzles, 1 extra tuning nozzle, 3 pairs of eartips. **Generous for $69** — most $70 IEMs ship with 3 silicone tips and a pouch.

**Optional USB-C DSP cable** ($25) adds:
- 10-band PEQ
- Volume controls on cable
- Pure-digital signal path (DAC built into cable)

This is useful if you want hardware EQ without app installation.

## Sound (Reference nozzle)

The EW300 targets **a balanced Harman-derivative with subtle warmth**. The tribrid setup gives each frequency range a specialized driver — DD for bass, planar for mids, PZT for treble extension.

### Bass

The 10mm DD handles bass entirely. **Sub-bass extension is clean down to 25 Hz** with good slam. Mid-bass is **lush and punchy without bleeding** into the lower mids.

Compared to single-DD options at this price (Aria 2), the EW300's bass is **more energetic and slammy** — closer to the [Truthear Zero Red]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-iems-for-bass-heads" >}}) profile but with better technical performance.

### Mids

This is where the 6mm planar earns its place. **Female vocals have an airy quality** that single-DD IEMs at this price struggle with. Male vocals have appropriate weight. Guitars sound natural — the planar avoids the "plasticky" character that cheap BAs sometimes introduce.

Compared to BA-based hybrids ([Hexa]({{< ref "vs/truthear-hexa-vs-moondrop-aria-2" >}}) at $79), the EW300's planar mids feel **more natural** at the cost of slightly less raw detail retrieval.

### Treble

The PZT (piezoelectric) driver extends treble cleanly above 10 kHz. **Hi-hats glisten, cymbals decay realistically, brushed snares have texture**. PZT drivers tend toward bright tonality — Simgot reigned it in here with good tuning, but treble-sensitive listeners should use the warmer nozzle.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Wider stage than most $69 IEMs**. The tribrid driver separation helps — bass, mids, treble each in their own "lane" gives more spatial clarity. Imaging is precise enough for gaming.

### Technical performance

**Class-leading at $69**. Detail retrieval matches some $150 IEMs ([Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}})). Transient response is fast (planar + PZT both have low mass). Resolution is impressive — the tribrid setup justifies the price premium over EW200.

## Source — easy to drive, scales

28 Ω + 121 dB sensitivity = **drives easily from phone**. Doesn't require a real source like S12 Pro does, but **scales noticeably with better DACs** — pair with a Moondrop Dawn Pro ($30) and the sub-bass tightens, the stage opens up.

The optional USB-C DSP cable is the cleanest source upgrade path — pure digital signal from phone to DSP-on-cable.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ **Unique tribrid driver setup at $69** (DD + planar + PZT)
- ✅ Three swappable nozzles for real tuning variation
- ✅ Mid-range timbre rivals $150 IEMs (planar advantage)
- ✅ Treble extension matches $200+ IEMs (PZT advantage)
- ✅ Generous accessory package (foam, ring gaskets, extra nozzle)

- ❌ Slightly bright with reference nozzle (treble-sensitive should use warmer)
- ❌ DSP cable is $25 extra

- ❌ Stock cable is plain (no modular plug)

**Buy if:** you want the most technically capable IEM under $80, you like nozzle tuning experiments, you've outgrown sub-$50 single-DDs.

**Skip if:** you specifically want single-DD timbre (Aria 2), you want hybrid with more BAs (Hexa), or you want premium build (Aria 2's zinc alloy).
