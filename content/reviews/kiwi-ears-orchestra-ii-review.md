Most all-balanced-armature IEMs chase one of two things: clinical accuracy or a wall of detail. The **Kiwi Ears Orchestra II** ($349) chases neither. It packs **ten proprietary balanced armatures per side** into a four-way crossover and then tunes the whole array for *enjoyment* — a mild U-shape with warmth, smoothness, and some of the best passive isolation in its class. It's the second run at Kiwi Ears' all-BA flagship, and it fixes the thing all-BA sets are usually accused of: sounding like a spreadsheet.

Short version: **it's an all-BA set you can listen to for hours, not just admire.** The technical performance is flagship-grade — detail retrieval and separation land where the price says they should — but the tuning prioritizes energy and musicality over ruthless neutrality. The one real catch is fit: this is a chunky shell with a wide nozzle, and small-eared listeners will fight it for a seal.

At 15 Ω and 114 dB/mW, the Orchestra II is easy to drive — a phone dongle runs it cleanly, though a clean source tightens up the low end. The bundled modular cable shipping with **both 3.5 mm and 4.4 mm plugs** is a genuinely nice touch at this price; you're not buying a balanced adapter separately.

## Sound signature

**Bass.** Two dedicated low-frequency BAs give the Orchestra II more low-end weight and warmth than the all-BA stereotype suggests. It's controlled, textured, and tuneful — but it is still *BA bass*. You get speed and definition, not the physical sub-bass rumble and slam a good dynamic driver delivers. For most genres that's a fair trade; for bass-heads chasing visceral impact, it's the format's built-in ceiling.

**Mids.** This is the heart of the set. The four mid BAs render vocals and instruments with a slight warm tilt — full, natural, and forgiving rather than dry or shouty. Reviewers consistently call the tonality one of the Orchestra II's standout traits: neutral-leaning but with enough warmth and smoothness to stay musical across genres.

**Treble.** Two high and two ultra-high BAs handle the top end with detail and air without tipping into harshness. The U-shape adds energy up top, so there's sparkle and excitement, but it stops short of the piercing peaks that make lesser multi-BA sets fatiguing. Cymbals and micro-detail come through cleanly.

**Stage & technicalities.** Detail retrieval and instrument separation are the payoff for ten drivers and a four-way crossover — this is where the Orchestra II earns "flagship." The presentation is precise and layered. It won't out-stage an open-back headphone, but for an IEM it images with confidence.

## Build, isolation & fit

The shell is transparent medical-grade resin with a silver honeycomb faceplate — you can see the driver array and circuitry inside, and the build feels like it costs more than $349. At **~7 g** each they're light.

**Isolation is the sleeper strength.** All-BA sets seal well by nature — no dynamic driver venting — and the Orchestra II is among the best-isolating sets in its class. If you commute, fly, or track in a loud room, that's a real advantage over a vented hybrid.

**Fit is the trade-off.** The **6.6 mm nozzle** and overall shell bulk mean small-eared listeners will twist and re-seat these more than usual to find a good seal. Tip-rolling helps, but if you've struggled with chunky IEMs before, audition or size carefully. See our [IEM tip-rolling guide](/guides/iem-tip-rolling-guide/) if you need to dial in the seal.

## All-BA vs hybrid: what you're actually choosing

The Orchestra II's most useful context isn't another all-BA set — it's the **hybrids** at the same money, like the Moondrop Blessing 3 (1 DD + 4 BA). The choice is real and it comes down to bass character:

- **All-BA (Orchestra II):** faster, cleaner, more coherent top-to-bottom, and it isolates better. Bass is tight and defined but lacks dynamic-driver slam.
- **Hybrid (Blessing 3 and similar):** a dynamic driver adds physical sub-bass weight and rumble the BAs can't match, at the cost of a little coherence at the driver crossover.

If you value coherence, isolation, and a fun-but-clean signature, the all-BA Orchestra II is the pick. If you want the low end to hit you in the chest, a DD hybrid wins.

## Who should buy it

- Listeners who want **flagship detail and separation** without a fatiguing, clinical tuning
- Commuters, travelers, and studio users who need **strong passive isolation**
- Anyone who prefers a **fun, warm-tinged U-shape** over strict neutrality
- Buyers who want a **balanced-ready** IEM out of the box (3.5 + 4.4 included)

## Who should skip it

- **Bass-heads** who need dynamic-driver slam — get a DD or hybrid
- **Small-eared listeners** wary of a 6.6 mm nozzle and a bulky shell
- **Reference purists** who want a strictly flat, monitor-style tuning — a Blessing 3 or Harman-target set fits better
- Anyone on a tighter budget — the performance-per-dollar curve flattens above $300

## Bottom line

The Kiwi Ears Orchestra II is what an all-BA flagship should be in 2026: technically excellent, genuinely isolating, and — crucially — *enjoyable*. Kiwi Ears took the format's usual weakness (sterile tuning) off the table and left the strengths (coherence, detail, isolation) intact. Its ceiling is BA bass and its wall is fit, but if neither is a dealbreaker, this is one of the most listenable ten-driver sets you can buy at the price.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does the Orchestra II need an amp?** No. At 15 Ω / 114 dB/mW it runs cleanly from a phone dongle. A clean dedicated source tightens the low end slightly, but it isn't power-hungry.

**Is all-BA better than a hybrid?** Neither is "better" — it's a choice. All-BA (like the Orchestra II) is faster, more coherent, and isolates better; a DD hybrid gives you physical sub-bass slam an all-BA set can't. Pick by whether bass impact or coherence matters more to you.

**Will it fit small ears?** It's a bulky shell with a 6.6 mm nozzle. Small-eared listeners can make it work with tip-rolling, but it's not the easiest fit. If that's a concern, see our [best ChiFi IEMs for small ears](/guides/best-chifi-iems-for-small-ears/).

**What's in the box?** The IEMs and a modular SP-OCC 2-pin cable with interchangeable 3.5 mm and 4.4 mm plugs, so you're balanced-ready without buying an adapter.
