NF Audio is the **underdog brand** in the sub-$100 ChiFi tier. They've been making IEMs since 2018 — predating most of the brands that dominate "best $100 IEM" lists today (Moondrop, Truthear, 7Hz). The **NA3 at $69** is their most popular product, targeting buyers who want a **warm, slightly V-shaped, "old-school musical"** signature instead of the modern Harman-target consensus.

Short version: **the NA3 is what you buy if you've owned a Moondrop Aria 2 or Truthear Hexa and decided you want something with more character**. Less analytical, more emotional. Read on for whether that fits you.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm dual-cavity ESC dynamic |
| **Impedance** | 32 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 105 dB/mW |
| **Frequency response** | 10 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Max SPL** | 125 dB |
| **Distortion** | <1% |
| **Cable** | Detachable 0.78mm 2-pin, 5N silver-coated OFC, 3.5mm SE |
| **Shell** | High-strength polycarbonate ("Pixel Monster" face design) |
| **Tuning** | V-shaped warm, slightly emphasized treble |
| **MSRP** | $69.99 |

The standout: **dual-cavity ESC (Equilibrium Sound Chamber) dynamic driver**. NF Audio's in-house design uses two acoustic chambers to control bass without bloating mids. Old-school approach (no BAs, no planar, no passive radiator) executed competently.

## Who it's for

- **Audiophiles tired of Harman target uniformity** — wants something with character
- **Listeners moving up from cheap IEMs** who want bass body + treble sparkle
- **Polycarbonate shell preference** (genuinely lighter than zinc alloy/steel)
- **Treble-tolerant** listeners who actively enjoy emphasis

Skip it if:
- You want neutral / reference tuning (any Moondrop / Truthear is better)
- You're treble-sensitive (NA3's V-shape will fatigue you)
- You want premium build (polycarbonate vs metal at this price tier)

## Build, fit, accessories

The NA3's **"Pixel Monster" faceplate** is distinctive — pixelated graphic design in three color options. Either you love it or you don't. The polycarbonate construction is **lightweight and ergonomic** — comfortable for hours.

The shell shape is universal — fits small and large ears equally. No pressure points on long wear.

The included **5N silver-coated OFC cable** is a step above stock at this price — most $69 IEMs ship with cheap 4-core twist. The NA3's cable feels closer to $20 aftermarket quality.

Accessories: 3 sets silicone tips, basic carrying pouch. Same as the Truthear Gate at half the price — pretty average for the tier.

## Sound

The NA3 has a **warm V-shape with emphasized treble** — bass is full, mids are slightly recessed, treble has bite. Different philosophy from modern Harman-target IEMs.

### Bass

**Mid-bass elevated, sub-bass extends but doesn't dominate**. Kick drums have weight; bass guitar lines have body. Sub-bass slams on EDM but doesn't overwhelm.

The dual-cavity design keeps bass tight — it doesn't bleed into the mids the way cheap V-shaped IEMs (KZ ZS10 Pro X era) did. Decent control for $69.

### Mids

**Slightly recessed**. Vocals are clear but not as forward as a Moondrop Aria 2 or Tin HiFi T3 Plus. Female vocals don't have the airy quality of VDSF-tuned IEMs.

For vocal-heavy music (K-pop, ballads), the NA3 isn't the right pick. For mixed listening where you want bass and treble emphasis, it works.

### Treble

**Emphasized and energetic**. Hi-hats sparkle; cymbals have texture; brushed snares have detail. Above 10 kHz extends cleanly with the dual-cavity design.

Trade-off: **treble peak that some find sibilant** on harsh recordings. The NA3 is more energetic than the Truthear Gate — try foam tips if you want to tame it.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Slightly wider than the Truthear Gate**. The V-shape contributes to a sense of width (mids recessed = perceived space). Imaging is decent for the price — instruments are positioned but not pinpoint.

### Technical performance

**Class-appropriate**. The dual-cavity design helps with bass control but the NA3 doesn't have BA-level resolution. Detail retrieval is fine without being notable.

## Source — phone is fine

105 dB/mW sensitivity means **phone drives it loudly**. Doesn't scale meaningfully with better sources — the NA3 is what it is from any output.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Distinctive warm V-shape — alternative to Harman saturation
- ✅ Dual-cavity bass control
- ✅ Quality silver-coated OFC cable included
- ✅ Polycarbonate is genuinely lightweight and comfortable
- ✅ Detachable 2-pin cable, future-proof
- ✅ Unique Pixel Monster design (if you like it)

**Cons**
- ❌ Polycarbonate shell less premium than metal alternatives
- ❌ Mids slightly recessed (vocal music suffers)
- ❌ Treble peak can sibilate on harsh recordings
- ❌ "Pixel Monster" design is divisive

**Buy if:** you want a warm, slightly energetic V-shape signature, you're not exclusively vocal-focused, and you appreciate the dual-cavity DD design philosophy.

**Skip if:** you want modern Harman-target tuning (Moondrop or Truthear), you're vocal-focused (Aria 2 or T3 Plus), or you're treble-sensitive (Salnotes Zero is smoother).
