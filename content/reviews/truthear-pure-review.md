The Truthear Hexa ($79) was the **default sub-$100 tribrid recommendation** for almost three years. Then Truthear shipped the **Pure** at $89.99 — same brand, same price tier, different tuning philosophy (tuned by **Listener** from Headphones.com instead of in-house). The audiophile community is split on which is better; the answer depends entirely on what you want.

Short version: **the Pure is the warmer, lusher, more "musical" sibling of the Hexa**. If you've outgrown analytical tuning and want easier listening, Pure is the pick. If you want maximum detail and treble extension, stay with Hexa.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 1 DD + 3 BA hybrid |
| **DD** | 10mm liquid crystal dome with silicone suspension, N52 rubidium magnet, dual-cavity internal magnetic circuit |
| **Impedance** | 18 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 120 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 20 kHz |
| **Shell** | CNC-machined aluminum faceplate + resin body |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin detachable, 3.5mm SE |
| **Tuning** | Warm-leaning, tuned by Listener (Headphones.com) |
| **MSRP** | $89.99 |

The headline change vs Hexa: **Listener tuning + N52 magnet DD**. Listener (Headphones.com's lead reviewer) is known for warm-target tuning preferences — the Pure reflects that. The N52 rubidium magnet is the strongest commercially-available DD magnet, giving the dynamic driver more authority.

## Who it's for

- **Hexa owner** who finds the tribrid slightly bright or analytical
- **Vocal music listener** — Pure's warmth flatters voices
- **Bass-leaning listener** without going full bass-head
- **Long-session enthusiast** — warm + smooth = no fatigue
- **Anyone who prefers "musical" over "reference" tuning**

Skip it if:
- You want detail-focused analytical sound (Hexa wins)
- You want treble sparkle on cymbal-heavy genres (Hexa)
- You hate dark/warm tuning generally

## Build, fit, accessories

The Pure's **CNC aluminum faceplate + resin body** is a mid-tier build — lighter than the all-metal options (Aria 2's zinc alloy, Tanchjim Origin's stainless steel) but more premium-feeling than the Hexa's all-resin construction.

The shell shape carries over from Hexa (proven comfortable). Same 0.78mm 2-pin connectors, same nozzle profile, same eartip compatibility — **you can use any aftermarket Hexa cable or tips**.

Stock accessories are basic: standard silicone tips, simple pouch. The Pure ships with **the same accessory package as Hexa**, no extras. Budget another $10-15 for [Spinfit CP145](https://www.amazon.com/) or [Moondrop Spring Tips](https://moondroplab.com/) — better seal = better bass.

## Sound

Pure targets a **warm reference signature** — neutral overall but biased warm in the mid-bass and lower mids, with politer treble than the Hexa.

### Bass

The N52 rubidium magnet does its job. **Sub-bass extends cleanly to 25 Hz** with audible texture. Mid-bass is more elevated than Hexa — kick drums have more weight, bass guitars have more body.

Not bass-head territory ([Truthear Zero Red]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-iems-for-bass-heads" >}}) is still the slam king), but **noticeably warmer than the Hexa's leaner bass**.

### Mids

This is where Pure earns its name. **Lush, natural midrange** without BA plasticky character. Vocals — male and female — have body and presence. The 3 BAs handle midrange but Truthear has tuned them to integrate seamlessly with the DD.

Some critics note "**slight timbre issues**" — the BAs aren't quite as natural as a pure DD setup on certain instrumentation (acoustic guitars can sound slightly synthetic). But for vocal-led music, the midrange is exceptional at this price.

### Treble

**Politer than Hexa**. The Pure rolls off treble more aggressively above 10 kHz, sacrificing some sparkle for fatigue-free listening. Cymbals are present but less defined; hi-hats have less bite.

Trade-off: **less treble detail, more comfort over long sessions**. If you found Hexa's treble peak occasionally fatiguing, Pure fixes it. If you loved Hexa's sparkle, Pure will feel "dark".

### Soundstage and imaging

**Similar width to Hexa, slightly more intimate**. Imaging is precise — instruments are placed clearly in 3D space. The warmer tuning brings vocals closer; the Hexa's brighter tuning pushes them slightly back.

### Technical performance

Detail retrieval is **class-appropriate but slightly lower than Hexa** (which prioritizes detail). Transient response is fine for a tribrid. Resolution is comparable — Pure isn't a downgrade, it's a different flavor.

## Source — easy from anything

18 Ω + 120 dB sensitivity = **drives easily from any source**. Phone, dongle, DAP — all work without issues. Doesn't scale dramatically with better sources; phone is fine for casual listening.

**Detailed comparison [here]({{< ref "vs/truthear-pure-vs-hexa" >}})**, but in short:

Both target similar price tier. **Aria 2 wins on**: vocal forwardness, treble smoothness, included modular cable. **Pure wins on**: bass weight, technical resolution via BAs. EW300 is the new $69 tribrid (1DD + 1 planar + 1 PZT). **Cheaper, more technical**. Pure has warmer / lusher tuning. **Pick by signature preference**.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Lush warm midrange, vocal-forward
- ✅ N52 magnet DD with strong sub-bass extension
- ✅ Comfortable shell (carried over from Hexa)
- ✅ Easy to drive from any source
- ✅ Tuned by Listener (Headphones.com) — proven tuning lineage
- ✅ Compatible with all aftermarket Hexa cables/tips

**Cons**
- ❌ Slight BA timbre issues on acoustic instruments
- ❌ Treble politely rolled off — sparkle-seekers should look elsewhere
- ❌ Same basic accessory package as Hexa (no included balanced cable)
- ❌ Resin body less premium than metal alternatives at this price

**Buy if:** you want a warm hybrid IEM at $90, you prioritize vocal music + long-session comfort, and you find Hexa too analytical.

**Skip if:** you want maximum technical detail (Hexa wins), you want premium build (Aria 2's metal), or you specifically need sub-bass slam (Zero Red).
