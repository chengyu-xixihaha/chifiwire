The Truthear Gate is what happens when Truthear takes the **Moondrop Chu 2 formula** ($20 single-DD, Harman-leaning tuning, low-friction first IEM) and asks: "what if we added a detachable cable for $2 more?"

That's basically the entire pitch. The sound is in the same ballpark as the Chu 2, the build is slightly less premium (resin vs Moondrop's metal), but the cable swappability is a real long-term advantage.

Short version: **the Gate is the right $20-tier pick for most buyers in 2026**, primarily because of the cable. Read on for the full picture.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm LCP (Liquid Crystal Polymer) dynamic |
| **Impedance** | 16 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 120 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 20 kHz |
| **Cable** | **Detachable** 0.78mm 2-pin, 3.5mm SE termination |
| **Shell** | Resin with metal faceplate |
| **Accessories** | 3 sets silicone tips, fabric pouch |
| **Tuning** | Harman-derived with slight sub-bass emphasis |
| **MSRP** | $21.99 |

The single most important spec: **0.78mm 2-pin detachable cable**. We'll keep coming back to this because it's the Gate's main differentiator.

## Who it's for

- **First-time audiophile buyer** who wants future-proof cable connection
- **Listener planning to upgrade to a balanced DAP** later (4.4mm aftermarket cable: $15-25)
- **Anyone who's lost an IEM to cable death** before
- **EDM / hip-hop listeners** who want more sub-bass than the Chu 2 offers
- **Lightweight-shell preference** (resin is ~30% lighter than Chu 2's zinc alloy)

Skip it if:
- You strongly prefer metal build feel (Chu 2's $20 IEM with zinc alloy beats this on tactile feel)
- You want maximum vocal forwardness (Chu 2's VDSF tuning is slightly more vocal-forward)

## Build, fit, accessories

The Gate's shell is **resin with a brushed metal faceplate**. The construction feels lighter than the Chu 2 — but in a "comfortable for hours" way, not a "cheap" way. Ergonomics are very good; the shape flows with the concha.

The **detachable 2-pin cable** is the centerpiece. Standard 0.78mm 2-pin connector means **any aftermarket cable works** — including $5 generic replacements, $15 silver-plated upgrades, $25 modular cables with 4.4mm plugs. You're not locked into Truthear's stock cable forever.

The stock cable itself is acceptable but plain — 4-strand twist, fixed 3.5mm plug, fine for daily use. Low microphonics. If/when it fails (cables fail eventually), $5 replaces it.

**Tip selection is basic**: 3 sets of silicone (S/M/L). Budget another $8-15 for [Spinfit CP145](https://www.amazon.com/) or [Moondrop Spring Tips](https://moondroplab.com/) — the seal upgrade is genuinely audible at any price.

Fabric pouch is included. **Not** a hard case, but better than no case (Chu 2 ships with just a sleeve).

## Sound

The Gate's tuning is **slightly more energetic than the Chu 2** — more sub-bass presence, more upper-treble sparkle, slightly less mid emphasis. Same Harman-derived target overall, just biased differently.

### Bass

**More sub-bass slam** than the Chu 2. Where the Chu 2 is polite, the Gate has real low-end weight on kick drums and electronic basslines. Mid-bass is comparable — tight, present without bloating.

For EDM, hip-hop, or rock, **the Gate has more drive**.

### Mids

Slightly recessed compared to the Chu 2. Vocals are clear but **less forward** — they sit in the mix instead of floating above it. Female vocals lose the "airy" quality the Chu 2 captures.

For mid-focused music (vocal-heavy pop, K-pop, acoustic), **the Chu 2 wins**.

### Treble

**More sparkle than the Chu 2**. Hi-hats have more shimmer; cymbals decay more naturally. The LCP diaphragm has the speed for clean treble transients.

Trade-off: **occasionally sibilant** on harsh recordings. The Gate's upper-treble lift can catch bad masters and emphasize "s" sounds. Not common but noticeable when it happens.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Slightly wider stage than the Chu 2**. Imaging is comparable — decent for the price, not spectacular. The wider perception comes from the upper-treble lift creating more air around instruments.

### Technical performance

Similar tier to the Chu 2 — class-appropriate for $20. Detail retrieval is fine; transient response is fine. LCP diaphragm is a marginal upgrade in speed over Chu 2's standard dynamic, but you have to listen attentively to notice.

## Source — phone is fine

At 16 Ω + 120 dB sensitivity, **easier to drive than the Chu 2** (28 Ω) and louder from any source. Phone, dongle, anything works.

Doesn't scale meaningfully with better sources. The Gate is a "plug and forget" IEM.

## The cable upgrade path

Where the Gate genuinely earns its $2 premium:

**Year 1**: stock cable works. No action needed.

**Year 2** (or whenever stock cable fails): $5 generic replacement keeps you running. No need to replace the IEM.

**Whenever you buy a balanced DAP**: $15-25 modular cable with 4.4mm plug gives you balanced output. Better separation, possibly slightly cleaner sound.

**Aftermarket upgrade**: $30-60 silver-plated cable, marginal sound improvement (mostly aesthetic upgrade).

Total cable optionality: priceless for a $22 IEM.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Chu 2 ($20)

The direct competitor. **Gate wins on**: cable swap-ability, sub-bass slam, treble sparkle, wider stage. **Chu 2 wins on**: build feel (metal vs resin), vocal forwardness, smoother treble for fatigue-free listening.

**Default to Gate** unless you specifically prefer metal build OR vocal-forward tuning.

[Read our full Chu 2 vs Gate comparison](/vs/moondrop-chu-2-vs-truthear-gate/).

### vs. 7Hz Salnotes Zero ($25)

Salnotes Zero is also detachable 2-pin, $3 more. **Salnotes wins on** warmth and sub-bass body. **Gate wins on** treble extension and slight upper-mid emphasis.

Pick by signature preference — both are reasonable.

### vs. Tangzu Wan'er S.G. ($20)

Wan'er is warm and mid-forward. Gate is more neutral with more treble. **Pick by preference** — Wan'er for warmth, Gate for clarity.

### vs. Truthear Hexa ($79)

Hexa is 3.5x the price and **technically much better** — 4 drivers, more detail, more refined tuning. Gate is the audition; Hexa is the upgrade if you love Truthear's house sound.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ **Detachable 2-pin cable** — future-proof, swappable, upgradable
- ✅ Slightly more sub-bass slam than the Chu 2
- ✅ Better treble extension and shimmer
- ✅ Lightweight resin shell, comfortable for hours
- ✅ Easy to drive from any source
- ✅ Fabric pouch included

**Cons**
- ❌ Resin shell less premium than Chu 2's metal
- ❌ Mids slightly recessed (worse for vocal-focused listening)
- ❌ Occasional sibilance on harsh recordings
- ❌ Only 3 sets of tips included (Chu 2 ships 6)
- ❌ Pouch, not hard case

**Buy if:** you want the cable longevity argument, you listen primarily to bass-heavy or treble-bright genres, and you don't mind resin construction at $22.

**Skip if:** you want metal shell (Chu 2 at $20), you want vocal forwardness (Chu 2 again), or you're treble-sensitive (Chu 2's smoother top end is safer).
