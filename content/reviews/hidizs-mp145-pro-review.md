The original Hidizs MP145 was one of the breakout budget planars of its generation — a big, whale-tail-shaped IEM with a 14.5mm driver, a warm-ish U-shaped tuning, and a famously large soundstage that punched well above its price. The **MP145 Pro** is the 2026 refresh, and it is not a cosmetic one. Hidizs rebuilt the engine: a new **"1 Tesla Fully Symmetrical Magnetic Circuit"** with a 7+7 N54H magnet array, roughly **+40% magnetic flux** and **+30% more copper** in the diaphragm trace versus the standard MP145. The shell looks nearly identical; almost everything you actually hear has changed.

At **$209 MSRP** (and around **$169 on Kickstarter / $189 on sale**), the Pro lands in the most crowded corner of the hobby — the sub-$300 budget-planar bracket where the [7Hz Timeless II]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-timeless-ii-review" >}}) and [Letshuoer S12 Pro]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) already live. So the question isn't whether a 14.5mm planar can sound good at this price — it can. It's whether the Pro's added energy and refinement are an upgrade you want, or whether they trade away the very thing that made the original special. Reviewers are split on exactly that, and we'll get into it.

## TL;DR

- **What it is:** $209 single 14.5mm planar IEM, the upgraded successor to the popular MP145, with a stronger 1-Tesla magnet array and +30% copper trace.
- **Sound:** Brighter, more neutral and more "grown-up" than the original — tighter mid-bass, forward and clearer mids, livelier upper-treble energy. Headfonics calls it "more open" with "sharper attacks."
- **The trade:** It gains detail and presence but loses some of the original's warmth and easy smoothness; it can be "slightly unforgiving on poorly recorded music" (Prime Audio).
- **Build:** Same oversized whale-tail aluminum shell, sub-9.5g per side, modular 2.5/4.4mm-style plug cable and three tuning filters in the box.
- **Drive:** 30Ω / 104dB — reasonably efficient for a planar, but like all planars it wants a real source to come alive.
- **Verdict:** A more technical, more refined MP145. Whether that's better depends on whether you wanted "fun" or "accurate."

## What's new vs the original MP145

This is the heart of the review, because the Pro is best understood as a re-tune of a known platform rather than a new IEM.

- **The magnetic circuit.** The big upgrade. Hidizs moved to a **1-Tesla fully symmetrical** design with a **7+7 array of N54H magnets**, claiming roughly **+40% magnetic flux** over the standard MP145, plus a diaphragm with **+30% more copper** in the trace. More flux and more conductor means tighter driver control and faster transients in theory — and reviewers do report exactly that.
- **The tuning.** Across the board, outlets describe the Pro as **brighter, more neutral, and more mid-centric** than the warmer, thicker original. Prime Audio: the original is "warmer and thicker overall" and "more relaxed and forgiving," while the Pro "tightens things up, adds a bit more energy in the presence and treble regions" for "better perceived detail, sharper attacks, and a more open presentation, but at the cost of some smoothness." Headfonics agrees the Pro is "more neutral, with a flatter perceived frequency response," and notably fixes the original's "laid-back vocals" with a "more forward, accurate" midrange.
- **The soundstage question — reviewers disagree.** This is the one spec to read carefully. Headfonics and Prime Audio describe the Pro's stage as **wider and more spacious** than the original, with better front-to-back layering. No Borders Audiophile sees it the opposite way, finding the Pro's stage "intimate and centered" and "congested" compared to the original's "gigantic soundstage," concluding the Pro is "more neutral, balanced and refined… but the fun factor is lower." Your mileage — and your tip/nozzle choice — will vary.

The accessory haul is genuinely strong for the price: a modular-plug cable, twelve pairs of tips across two materials, and three pneumatic tuning filters (low / balanced / high frequency) that let you nudge the tuning before you ever reach for EQ.

## Sound signature

Broadly, the Pro reads as **bright-neutral with a mild mid-bass lift** — a Harman-influenced profile that's more even and more revealing than the warmer original.

### Bass

Quality over quantity. Prime Audio describes "physical yet tight bass response," with "sub-bass that digs deep with typical planar cleanliness — fast, textured, and very well controlled," and more body through the mid-bass than a strict Harman tune without sounding bloated. No Borders Audiophile is more reserved, calling the bass "fast, round and thumpy" with a rolled-off sub-bass, and warns it "won't deliver muscular dynamics for bass-heavy genres." Either way: this is a tight, controlled planar low-end, not a basshead IEM.

### Mids

The most improved area over the original. Headfonics specifically credits the Pro with fixing the MP145's "laid-back vocals," delivering a "more forward, accurate" midrange with better-defined imaging. Prime Audio agrees the planar "really starts to show off" here — "transients are quick, almost effortless," vocals sit slightly forward with good note weight. The caveat (No Borders): upper mids are "notably boosted," so female vocals can occasionally sound "overly exuberant." The included **golden tuning filter tames this better than the silver one.**

### Treble

The most polarizing band. The Pro is "livelier-sounding due to its higher treble tuning" (Headfonics), with a presence lift around **6–8kHz** that "brings clarity but also edge" (Prime Audio). On clean recordings percussion gets a "sharp, snappy attack"; on poor ones it can expose sibilance. No Borders found the treble "dry and crunchy, lean in extension" with a roll-off past 10kHz that limits air. The consensus: more energy and detail than the original, at the cost of some smoothness and forgiveness.

### Soundstage & imaging

As noted above, this is where reviewers genuinely diverge. Prime Audio and Headfonics report a **wide stage with good depth and height, precise imaging, and excellent separation** — Headfonics specifically calls staging and imaging "more accurate, more defined" than the original. No Borders Audiophile instead hears an "intimate and centered" stage that feels congested next to the original's expansive presentation. Imaging precision isn't in dispute; the perceived *size* of the stage is. If a huge soundstage was your reason for wanting an MP145, audition before you buy.

## Build & comfort

The Pro keeps the original's **oversized, whale-tail-shaped aluminum shell** essentially unchanged, with only minor nozzle/vent tweaks. Build quality is premium — anodized aluminum (a limited 299-unit titanium edition also exists), confident 2-pin sockets, and a tidy braided cable.

The caveat is right there in the dimensions: **these are big.** Prime Audio achieved "excellent fit and comfort with no pressure build-up," helped by the sub-9.5g weight, but No Borders questions why Hidizs retained such a large housing without a clear soundstage payoff. If you have small ears, treat the shell size as a real fit risk and try before committing — the same caution applies as with any large planar.

## Driveability

At **30Ω and 104dB**, the MP145 Pro is more efficient than some planar rivals (the S12 Pro, for instance, is harder to drive). It'll reach listening volume off a phone or a basic dongle. But the rule for planars still holds: **give it real current and it firms up.** Bass control, dynamic swing, and that "effortless" transient quality scale with a proper dongle DAC or DAP, ideally over the included **balanced 4.4mm plug.** Don't judge any budget planar — this one included — off a laptop headphone jack.

## How it compares

### vs 7Hz Timeless II ($229)

The closest fight. Both are 14.5mm planars in the low-$200s. The [Timeless II]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-timeless-ii-review" >}}) brings a **tunable-nozzle system** (four nozzle pairs that physically reshape the treble) and a tiny ~5g coin-shaped shell that's far easier to fit than the MP145 Pro's whale-tail. The Timeless II's headline is its airy, extended treble; the MP145 Pro counters with a more mid-forward, vocal-centric presentation and a richer accessory box. **Pick the Timeless II for fit, treble air and tuning flexibility; pick the MP145 Pro if you want forward vocals and don't mind a large shell.**

### vs Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)

The value benchmark. The [S12 Pro]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) is cheaper, V-shaped, and the technical reference for sub-$200 planars, with deep, textured sub-bass and recessed mids — but it's harder to drive (102dB/mW, genuinely phone-hostile) and leaves the midrange behind. The MP145 Pro is more neutral, more mid-present, and easier to power, but costs ~$50 more. **The S12 Pro is the better V-shape-and-sub-bass value; the MP145 Pro is the more balanced, vocal-friendly, source-tolerant pick.**

## Who should buy

- Listeners who wanted an MP145 but found it too warm or its vocals too laid-back — the Pro fixes exactly that.
- Vocal- and detail-focused listeners who want a neutral-bright planar with strong, forward mids.
- Buyers who value a complete box — modular cable, twelve tips, three tuning filters — at the price.
- Anyone with a real dongle DAC or DAP to feed it.

## Who should skip

- Fans of the **original** MP145's big, warm, "fun" sound — the Pro is a different, more analytical animal, and No Borders Audiophile specifically prefers the original's character.
- Treble-sensitive listeners and people who mostly stream poorly mastered tracks — the 6–8kHz lift can expose sibilance.
- Small-eared listeners — the oversized whale-tail shell is a genuine fit gamble.
- Phone-only listeners who won't add a source.

## Verdict

The Hidizs MP145 Pro is a **legitimately re-engineered MP145, not a sticker swap.** The stronger 1-Tesla magnet array and copper-rich diaphragm deliver real gains in transient speed, midrange presence and perceived detail, and the accessory package is excellent. The catch is that "refined" cuts both ways: you gain accuracy and energy, you lose some of the original's warmth, smoothness and — by at least one reviewer's account — its signature expansive stage. As an upgrade it's clearly more *capable*; whether it's more *enjoyable* depends entirely on which MP145 you wanted.

For buyers chasing a neutral-bright, vocal-forward planar with a generous box at ~$209, it's an easy recommendation. For those who fell for the original's big, warm character, audition first — you may find you preferred the MP145 you already had.

**Rating: 4.3 / 5** — a strong, well-built technical planar held back only by its divisive stage character and large shell.
