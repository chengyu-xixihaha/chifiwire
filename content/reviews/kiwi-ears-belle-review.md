At $30, most IEMs are a compromise you tolerate. The **Kiwi Ears Belle** tries to be one you actually enjoy: a single **10mm Diamond-Like Carbon (DLC) dynamic driver** in a comfortable shell, tuned warm, with **hand-assembled, individually matched drivers** — a QC step usually reserved for pricier sets. It's Kiwi Ears applying its brand polish to the very bottom of the market.

Short version: **it's a warm, easy, well-built budget IEM that's better assembled than it has any right to be at $30** — with a tight, controlled low end as its standout. The knocks are exactly what the price predicts: the bass can occasionally bleed into the mids, the accessories are bare, and it won't touch a multi-driver set on technicalities. As a first IEM or a beater pair, it's an easy recommendation.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Kiwi Ears Belle |
|---|---|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm DLC (Diamond-Like Carbon) dynamic |
| **Impedance** | 32 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 103 dB SPL |
| **Isolation** | Up to ~22 dB passive |
| **Connector** | 0.78 mm 2-pin |
| **Cable options** | 3.5 mm / 3.5 mm with mic / USB-C digital with mic |
| **Fit** | Medium, good seal |
| **Price** | $29.99 |

At 32 Ω / 103 dB, the Belle is **medium-to-slightly-hard to drive** for a budget set — it plays fine from a phone dongle but tightens up noticeably with a little more clean power. One genuinely nice touch: you can buy it with a **USB-C digital cable and mic**, which turns it into a plug-and-play headset for laptops and Android phones with no dongle needed.

## Sound signature

**Bass.** This is the Belle's calling card. The DLC driver delivers a **tight, controlled, plentiful low end** that's the most impressive thing about the set — punchy without being bloated, and textured enough to flatter modern genres. For $30, the bass control is the standout.

**Mids.** Warm-tilted and easygoing. Vocals are full and pleasant rather than forward or analytical. The one caveat reviewers flag: when the bass gets busy, it **can bleed into the lower mids** and muddy things slightly. It's a budget-driver limitation, not a tuning mistake, and most listeners won't mind at this price.

**Treble.** Smooth and inoffensive. The Belle plays it safe up top — no sharp peaks, no sibilance, but also not the last word in air or sparkle. That's the right call for a budget set meant for long, fatigue-free listening.

## Build, fit & what's in the box

The build punches above the price. Each pair is **hand-assembled with individually measured and channel-matched drivers** — a level of QC you almost never see at $30, and it shows in consistent left/right balance. The medium-sized shell is comfortable and seals well, with up to ~22 dB of passive isolation.

The **accessories are where the price shows.** There's no carrying case, and the stock eartips are mediocre — most reviewers suggest tip-rolling to get the best out of it. Budget a couple of dollars for better tips and you've fixed the Belle's biggest real weakness. Our [IEM tip-rolling guide](/guides/iem-tip-rolling-guide/) covers the cheap upgrades.

## How it compares

- **Kiwi Ears Cadenza II (~$50):** the step up in Kiwi's own lineup. The [Cadenza II](/reviews/kiwi-ears-cadenza-ii-review/) adds a titanium-coated driver and KARS 2.0 tuning for a cleaner, more refined U-shape. The Belle is warmer and cheaper; the Cadenza II is the more technical set. If you can stretch to $50, the Cadenza II is the more complete IEM — but the Belle holds its own on bass control.
- **Moondrop Chu 2 (~$20):** the default sub-$30 rival. The [Chu 2](/reviews/moondrop-chu-2-review/) leans more neutral/Harman; the Belle is warmer and has the friendlier low end. Pick the Belle for warmth and bass, the Chu 2 for reference-leaning tuning.

## Who should buy it

- **First-time IEM buyers** who want a warm, easy, no-fuss set that sounds good from a phone
- Anyone needing a **cheap headset** — the USB-C mic cable option is genuinely useful
- Listeners who want **budget bass done with control**, not a bloated V-shape
- Buyers who value **build/QC consistency** over accessory count

## Who should skip it

- **Detail chasers** — a $30 single-DD won't resolve like a multi-driver set
- Anyone who wants a **complete package** out of the box — plan to buy tips
- **Reference/neutral purists** — the Belle is warm on purpose; a Chu 2 fits better
- If you can spend $50, the **Cadenza II** is the more capable IEM

## Bottom line

The Kiwi Ears Belle is a genuinely good $30 IEM: warm, comfortable, unusually well-assembled, with bass control that belies the price. It won't win a technicalities shootout and it ships with bare accessories, but as a first IEM, a headset, or a beater you won't worry about losing, it does exactly what a budget set should — sound good and get out of the way. Grab better tips and enjoy it.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does the Kiwi Ears Belle need an amp?** No, but it benefits from a little clean power. At 32 Ω / 103 dB it plays from a phone dongle fine and tightens up with a slightly stronger source. A cheap USB-C dongle DAC is all you need.

**Which cable should I get?** Three options: plain 3.5 mm, 3.5 mm with mic, or a USB-C digital cable with mic. Get the **USB-C mic version** if you want a plug-and-play headset for a laptop or Android phone; get plain 3.5 mm if you already own a dongle.

**Belle vs Cadenza II — which should I buy?** The Belle ($30) is warmer and cheaper; the [Cadenza II](/reviews/kiwi-ears-cadenza-ii-review/) ($50) is more refined and technical. If budget is the priority, the Belle delivers; if you can stretch $20 more, the Cadenza II is the better all-rounder.

**Are the accessories good?** No — that's the Belle's main weakness. No case, and mediocre stock tips. Budget a couple dollars for aftermarket tips and it's a much better IEM.
