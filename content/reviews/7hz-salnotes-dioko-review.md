The 7Hz Salnotes Dioko arrived in 2022 asking a question nobody had a clean answer to: can you put a planar magnetic driver in an IEM and sell it for $69? The Timeless — 7Hz's flagship at the time — cost three times as much and still drew arguments about whether planars belonged in an ear canal. The Dioko said: let the market decide. **Crinacle collaborated on the tuning**, 7Hz used the same 14.2mm driver family as the Timeless, and the price landed at $69.

Short answer: **yes, you can, and it works — with one hard requirement.** The Dioko is the cheapest real entry into planar sound. If you already own a dongle DAC and you want to know what the planar fuss is about without spending $150+, this is your IEM. If you're planning to plug it into a phone, stop reading and buy a [7Hz Salnotes Zero:2]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-zero-2-review" >}}) instead.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 14.2mm planar magnetic |
| **Impedance** | 16 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 102 dB/Vrms |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Shell** | Plastic/resin, lightweight universal fit |
| **Cable** | Detachable 0.78mm 2-pin, 3.5mm SE |
| **Tuning** | V-shaped — elevated bass and treble, slightly recessed mids |
| **MSRP** | $69.00 |

The 14.2mm driver is the same size as what powers the Timeless AE at $219. The difference between those two IEMs is everything around the driver: the shell, the tuning, the cable, and the driver implementation. At $69, 7Hz extracted enough performance to justify the price. They did not extract everything the driver is capable of — that's what the Timeless AE costs.

## Who it's for

- **Enthusiasts who already own a dongle DAC** and want to hear what planar sounds like without a major outlay
- **V-shape listeners** who want speed, texture, and energy rather than warmth and neutrality
- **People upgrading from budget dynamics** who want audibly better transient performance for under $100
- **Genre-flexible listeners** — EDM, hip-hop, metal, instrumental — the Dioko's tuning is built for energy

Skip it if:
- You listen from a phone or laptop headphone jack (it will sound flat and lifeless — not the Dioko's fault)
- You want neutral-reference sound (that's what the [Zero:2]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-zero-2-review" >}}) is for)
- You're treble-sensitive — the Dioko's top end is energetic and does not apologize

## Build, fit, accessories

The shell is plastic/resin — this is where the $69 price shows up most clearly. It doesn't feel cheap exactly, but pick it up next to the aluminum S12 Pro or the titanium-coated Timeless AE and you know immediately what the cost difference bought. The finish is clean, the shell has no sharp edges, and it is light enough to disappear in the ear during long sessions. For a workout IEM or a daily commuter, the weight is actually an advantage.

The 0.78mm 2-pin detachable cable is functional. It's not a standout accessory — no modular terminations, no premium conductor marketing — but it connects reliably and has acceptable microphonics. If you want to run balanced, you'll need a cable swap. At this price, that's not a complaint.

Fit is a standard universal shape. Tip selection matters here: the Dioko's V-shape puts energy at both frequency extremes, and a poorly sealing tip will exaggerate the treble while killing the bass. Get the seal right. Stock silicone tips are fine as a starting point.

## Sound

The Dioko is a **V-shaped planar**. That phrasing deserves unpacking, because planar V-shape and dynamic driver V-shape are different things. Dynamic V-shapes tend to be warm and impactful in the bass, with a treble that either sparkles or splashes depending on the driver quality. Planar V-shape is faster, more textured, and more precise — the same elevated bass and treble signature, but with the tighter control and faster decay that only a planar diaphragm provides. The Dioko is not a bass cannon. It is an energetic, technical, fast IEM that happens to be inexpensive.

### Bass

This is where the Dioko earns its reputation. Planar bass at $69 is genuinely different from dynamic driver bass at $69 — faster transients, more texture, sub-bass extension that doesn't sacrifice control for quantity. The rumble in electronic music is present and layered rather than a single blunt shelf. Kick drums hit and release cleanly; there is no mid-bass bloom coating the lower frequencies.

The trade-off is warmth. The Dioko's bass does not have the organic decay of a well-tuned dynamic driver. Acoustic bass instruments — upright bass, bass guitar — can sound slightly clinical, like they're rendered accurately rather than felt. For acoustic and jazz listeners, this may read as a flaw. For EDM, hip-hop, and anything with layered low-end production, it's the right tool.

### Mids

Recessed — that's the V-shape. Vocals are present, clear, and intelligible; they're just not sitting in the front row. Male vocals have enough lower-midrange body to feel grounded. Female vocals are where the recession shows most: the upper mids step back relative to the elevated treble, which can make sopranos and high altos feel slightly thin or bright depending on the recording.

**The Dioko is better for instrumental than vocal-centric music.** Guitars, synths, and piano all read as accurate; voices are the one area where mid-forward listeners will want more presence. If your library is 80% singer-songwriter or jazz vocals, look at the Zero:2 instead. If it's 80% instrumental with vocals as texture, the Dioko's mids are fine.

### Treble

Energetic and extended — the planar top end in full effect. Hi-hats glisten; cymbals have natural shimmer and decay; the upper register reveals micro-detail that most sub-$100 dynamic IEMs compress into a vague brightness. This is the other half of the planar advantage: not just fast bass, but fast treble that doesn't smear.

The caveat is sharp recordings. The Dioko resolves treble aggressively. Badly mastered files, harshly compressed streaming audio, and bright studio productions will push through without mercy. **If you are treble-sensitive, the Dioko will fatigue you.** It is not a forgiving tuning. A -2 dB shelf starting at 8 kHz tames it via EQ for those affected, but out of the box this IEM prioritizes detail and energy over comfort.

### Soundstage and imaging

Better than you expect at $69. The planar driver's fast transients translate directly into imaging precision — instruments sit in specific places rather than smearing left and right. Soundstage width is good, depth is present, layering is the real standout. On a well-recorded live album you can pull apart the mix and locate individual elements. Sub-$69 dynamic IEMs do not stage like this.

### Technical performance

This is the Dioko's strongest argument. Speed, separation, micro-detail — these are the planar virtues, and at $69 the Dioko delivers them at a level that most budget dynamic drivers simply cannot match. Complex passages stay legible. Transients are sharp. The gap in technicality between the Dioko and a $69 dynamic IEM is audible and real — not a spec sheet abstraction.

## Source — non-negotiable

**102 dB/Vrms is the number that decides whether you enjoy this IEM.** It looks fine in isolation; in practice it means the Dioko needs more voltage than a standard high-sensitivity dynamic driver to wake up properly. Run it from a phone and you will get volume, but the bass will be thin, the soundstage will shrink, and you'll wonder what the fuss was about.

**A basic dongle DAC is the minimum.** A FiiO KA13, an Apple USB-C dongle (genuinely capable), or a Moondrop Dawn are all sufficient starting points. A mid-tier dongle running 3.5mm single-ended is enough to hear the Dioko at its best; balanced output adds headroom but isn't required at this driver's sensitivity level.

If you don't own a dongle DAC and won't buy one, this IEM is not for you. This is not a soft suggestion — it's the difference between a good IEM and a disappointing one.

## Compared to

### vs. 7Hz Salnotes Zero:2 ($25)

Different tools entirely. The [Zero:2]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-zero-2-review" >}}) is a neutral-reference dynamic driver — forgiving source requirements, balanced tuning, excellent for vocals and long sessions. The Dioko is a V-shaped planar — needs a dongle, more technical, more energetic, less forgiving. **If you're new to the hobby and don't own a dongle, the Zero:2 is the better first IEM by a wide margin.** If you have the gear and want to hear what planar does, spend the extra $44 on the Dioko.

### vs. Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)

The [S12 Pro]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) is the step up — better build (aluminum vs. plastic), better cable (modular with 4.4mm), and a more refined tuning that takes the V-shape concept further with more sub-bass slam and smoother treble. The Dioko is 60% of the S12 Pro's performance at 43% of the price. If you have $160, get the S12 Pro. If you have $69 and want to test whether planar is your thing before committing more, the Dioko answers that question cleanly.

### vs. 7Hz Timeless AE ($219)

The [Timeless AE]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-timeless-ae-review" >}}) uses the same 14.2mm driver family in a titanium-coated steel shell with neutral-bright tuning, a modular cable, and meaningfully more refined driver implementation. It is a better IEM in every technical and material dimension. The comparison isn't really apples to apples — the Timeless AE targets a different listener (neutral-bright, serious source, premium build). The Dioko is for budget planar exploration; the AE is for people who've already decided planars are their preferred driver type and want the best implementation under $250. **If you like the Dioko, the Timeless AE is the logical upgrade.**

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Real planar performance at the lowest price it's been available
- ✅ Fast, textured, controlled planar bass
- ✅ Precise imaging and good soundstage for $69
- ✅ Micro-detail that outpaces budget dynamic drivers
- ✅ Lightweight, comfortable shell for long sessions

**Cons**
- ❌ Needs a dongle DAC — non-negotiable
- ❌ V-shape leaves mids recessed — not for vocal listeners
- ❌ Treble is energetic and unforgiving on bright recordings
- ❌ Plastic shell feels the price next to metal-bodied competitors
- ❌ 3.5mm SE only — balanced requires cable swap

**Buy if:** you already own a dongle DAC, you want to hear what planar magnetic sounds like without spending $150+, and you enjoy V-shaped energetic tuning for electronic, hip-hop, metal, or instrumental music.

**Skip if:** you're listening from a phone, you prefer neutral or mid-forward signatures, or you're treble-sensitive and don't EQ.
