The Moondrop Blessing 3 has been on Crinacle's **S-tier IEM rankings for nearly two years running** — an unusually long tenure for a ChiFi market that refreshes its consensus picks every few months. It costs $319.99. It uses a 1DD+4BA hybrid configuration that Moondrop has been refining since the Blessing 2. The question in 2026 isn't whether it's good — it's been empirically validated by enough reviewers that the goodness isn't in doubt. The question is whether it's still the right buy at three times the price of a Truthear Nova, or whether the mid-fi bracket has caught up to it.

Short answer: **the Blessing 3 is still the benchmark sub-$400 IEM, but the gap between it and the $150 tier is narrower than it's ever been**.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 1 DD (bass) + 4 BA (mids + highs) |
| **Diaphragm** | Liquid crystal polymer (LCP) DD |
| **Impedance** | 22 Ω ± 15% @ 1kHz |
| **Sensitivity** | 117 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **THD** | < 1% @ 1kHz / 94dB |
| **Frequency response** | 5 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin, modular plugs (3.5mm + 4.4mm both included) |
| **Shell** | Resin with gloss finish, semi-translucent faceplate |
| **MSRP** | $319.99 |

The modular cable inclusion matters — the Blessing 3 scales meaningfully with 4.4mm balanced output, and Moondrop doesn't charge extra for the adapter like some brands do at this price.

## Who it's for

- **Anyone who's maxed out the Truthear Nova** and wants to hear what another $170 buys in ChiFi technical performance
- **Genre-hopping listeners** who need one IEM for classical, jazz, pop, and electronic — the Blessing 3's Harman-derived tuning doesn't favor or penalize any genre
- **Detail retrievers** — people who listen for the things in music other IEMs obscure (room reverb, low-level textures, instrument layering in dense passages)
- **Source-curious buyers** — the Blessing 3 rewards better DAC/amps more noticeably than sub-$150 IEMs

Skip it if you want V-shaped excitement, heavy bass, or maximum treble sparkle — there are better-tuned options for each of those preferences.

## Build, fit, accessories

The resin shell is **the Blessing 3's only real compromise at the price**. For $320, you'll find metal builds elsewhere (the Letshuoer S12 Pro, the DUNU SA6 Ultra). Moondrop's resin is well-finished — the gloss faceplate with gradient paint is genuinely attractive — but it carries a premium, and some reviewers find the large shell a fit challenge for smaller ears.

The shells are on the larger side. If you tried the Blessing 2 and struggled with fit, the Blessing 3 is nearly identical in shape. Deeper insertion and a wide nozzle diameter means **tip choice matters more than most IEMs**. Moondrop includes their Spring Tips in the box (excellent), but Spinfit CP100 or Final Type E also work well.

The cable is well-braided, low-microphonics, and the modular system is a screw-collar design (not a magnetic click) — it feels secure without being difficult to swap. This is the same modular system as the Aria 2.

## Sound

The Blessing 3 targets **Moondrop VDSF** — their in-house Harman derivative that adds a mild sub-bass shelf over a linear midrange and extends treble beyond what pure Harman specifies. In practice, this produces a sound that is simultaneously reference-accurate and slightly more engaging than flat neutral.

### Bass

The LCP dynamic driver handles everything below ~800Hz, and **this is where the Blessing 3 earns its price**. Sub-bass extension is genuine — 30Hz rumble in electronic and classical recordings has texture and weight. Mid-bass is controlled, not bloated. The transition from dynamic to balanced armature happens cleanly; bass doesn't bleed upward into the lower midrange.

Compared to the Truthear Nova: the Nova's bass is also competent, but the Blessing 3's DD is noticeably faster, with better definition on complex bass passages. It's the difference between bass you hear and bass you feel.

### Mids

Four BAs split across midrange frequencies give the Blessing 3 **exceptional vocal rendering**. Male vocals have weight and chest resonance; female vocals reach without thinness. The upper midrange sits at the right level — slightly forward by Harman standards, which adds presence without tip-toeing into harshness.

Acoustic instruments are where the midrange quality becomes obvious: guitar body resonance, piano hammer attack, violin timbre. These are the frequencies where cheaper IEMs — even good ones — sound slightly processed. The Blessing 3 sounds more like the original.

### Treble

Extended, detailed, and somewhat demanding of source quality. **The Blessing 3 will tell you if your DAC is mediocre**. The upper treble (8–16kHz) is more present than the Nova or Aria 2 — not harsh on well-recorded material, but not forgiving of badly mastered music. Cymbals shimmer clearly; sibilance is controlled on most tracks, but vocalists with aggressive esses will expose the edge of the tuning.

Treble-sensitive listeners: the Blessing 3 is not for you. The Truthear Nova is the better pick.

### Soundstage and imaging

This is the technical achievement that justifies the price. **Imaging is the best in class under $400** — instruments are placed with surgical precision in three dimensions. The soundstage is wide and has real depth perception, not just lateral spread. Orchestral recordings are particularly revelatory: section separation, hall reverb, soloist positioning are all resolved clearly.

The Nova and Aria 2 have good imaging. The Blessing 3 makes them sound two-dimensional by comparison.

### Detail retrieval and dynamics

Micro-dynamics — the soft-to-loud gradations within a phrase, the decay of a note in a reverberant space — are rendered with a resolution that sub-$200 IEMs approximate but don't achieve. **The Blessing 3 doesn't just play more notes; it plays the space between notes more convincingly**.

## Source pairing

At 22Ω / 117dB sensitivity, the Blessing 3 is technically easy to drive — your phone won't struggle. But it scales. Running balanced from a FiiO JM21 or Moondrop Dawn Pro adds stage width, bass definition, and treble air in ways that are audible, not theoretical.

For stationary listening: a desktop DAC like the SMSL DO100 Pro or Topping E50 II paired with even a modest headphone amp reveals another level of resolution. For portable: the FiiO JM21 is the natural companion at a combined ~$450.

## Compared to

### vs. Truthear Nova ($149)
The Nova is the better all-rounder for less money — safer tuning, easier source matching, no fit concerns. The Blessing 3 is the better instrument — more detail, more stage, more dynamics. **Buy the Nova if you want the sound; buy the Blessing 3 if you want the experience.**

### vs. Moondrop Aria 2 ($89)
Not really comparable. The Aria 2 is warmer, more polite, less technical. The Blessing 3 reveals layers in music the Aria 2 smooths over. If you're debating between them, buy the Aria 2 and wait until you know you want more — the jump is worth making deliberately.

### vs. Letshuoer Cadenza 12 ($349)
The Cadenza 12 is more V-shaped — more bass, more treble sparkle, more fun at the cost of midrange density. The Blessing 3 is more balanced and more technically refined. **Genre preference decides this**: EDM and hip-hop favor the Cadenza 12; classical and jazz favor the Blessing 3.

### vs. Simgot EA1000 Fermat ($219)
The EA1000 Fermat punches at the Blessing 3's level in bass slam and treble extension but concedes imaging precision and mid coherence. It's a legitimate competitor — the best under-$250 challenge to the Blessing 3 currently. **Buy the Fermat if budget matters; buy the Blessing 3 if you want the benchmark.**

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Best-in-class imaging and soundstage under $400
- ✅ Textbook VDSF tuning — works across every genre
- ✅ Genuine sub-bass texture from the dedicated DD
- ✅ Modular 3.5/4.4mm cable in the box
- ✅ Scales meaningfully with better sources

**Cons**
- ❌ Resin shell feels less premium than metal alternatives at the price
- ❌ Large shell — smaller-ear users may have fit issues
- ❌ Treble can be demanding on harsh recordings
- ❌ Nova delivers 80% of the experience at half the price

**Buy if:** you want the reference point for sub-$400 ChiFi, you care about technical performance, and you've already heard good IEMs and want to hear more.

**Skip if:** you're coming straight from budget IEMs (the Nova is the better entry point to this tier), you want bass emphasis, or you're treble-sensitive.
