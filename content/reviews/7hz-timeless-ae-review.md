The original 7Hz Timeless launched in 2021 and immediately broke what people thought was possible with planar magnetic IEMs at the price. A 14.2mm driver in a metal shell for $200 — it rewrote the playbook. The **Timeless AE (Anniversary Edition)** is the result of 7Hz listening to everything the community said was wrong with the original: the treble was too hot, the shell could fit better, the cable could be more complete. At $219, the AE doesn't so much replace the original as correct it.

Short answer: **the 7Hz Timeless AE is the planar IEM to beat under $250 in 2026.** If you own a real source and you want the fastest, most detailed IEM in this price bracket, this is it.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 14.2mm planar magnetic |
| **Impedance** | 14.8 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 104 dB/Vrms |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Shell** | Titanium-coated stainless steel, magnetic faceplate |
| **Cable** | Silver-plated OCC, modular (3.5mm + 4.4mm included) |
| **Tuning** | Neutral-bright, extended treble, controlled sub-bass |
| **MSRP** | $219.00 |

The impedance is low (14.8Ω) but the sensitivity figure — **104 dB/Vrms** — is the number that determines source requirements. That's lower than it looks; it means the Timeless AE needs more voltage than a standard 100+ dB/mW dynamic driver. Plan accordingly.

## Who it's for

- **Anyone chasing maximum detail and transient speed under $250** — the planar driver advantage is real and audible
- **Neutral-bright signature listeners** — if you want a reference-leaning tuning with treble air and not a V-shaped bass cannon, the AE is tuned for you
- **Source owners** — dongle DAC at minimum, 4.4mm balanced preferred; this is not a phone-out IEM
- **Former Timeless owners** who bounced off the treble — the AE tames the peak that drove people away

Skip it if:
- You listen exclusively from a phone or laptop headphone jack
- You want a warm, mid-forward signature
- You hate any treble presence above neutral — the AE is still bright

## Build, fit, accessories

The titanium-coated stainless steel shell is one of the best-looking in this price class. The finish has a subtle luster that photographs well and wears without scratching the way anodized aluminum does. The magnetic faceplate is a premium detail — functional and visually distinctive. This does not look or feel like a $219 IEM.

The fit improvement over the original Timeless is real. 7Hz redesigned the nozzle angle and the overall shell geometry based on years of community feedback. The original Timeless had a reputation for sitting proud in the ear on smaller ear canals — the AE sits deeper and more secure without requiring aggressive tip-down cable routing. Larger ears won't notice the difference; smaller ears will notice it immediately.

The **modular cable** is the headline accessory win: silver-plated OCC conductors, 3.5mm and 4.4mm terminations both in the box. No choosing between balanced and single-ended at checkout, no cable upgrade tax. The cable itself has good flexibility and low microphonics. It's not the last cable you'll ever buy, but it's not the cable you'll replace in the first week either.

Accessories otherwise include multiple sets of silicone tips and a carrying case. The package is complete — there's nothing obviously missing the way budget IEM boxes sometimes feel.

## Sound

The Timeless AE is **neutral-bright with planar characteristics**: fast transients, exceptional detail retrieval, wide imaging, and the mild metallic tint in timbre that is the planar driver's signature trait at this price. If you've heard a planar IEM before, you know what that means. If you haven't: the AE will show you things in familiar recordings that you didn't know were there, and occasionally the high-frequency texture will have a quality that sounds slightly more processed than a well-tuned dynamic driver. That's the trade-off. Most listeners adapt within days and stop noticing it.

### Bass

Sub-bass is present and controlled. The planar diaphragm has the surface area to extend low with texture — this isn't the rolled-off planar bass of early budget attempts. Electronic music and bass-heavy R&B tracks reveal genuine rumble, not just a suggestion of it.

Mid-bass is lean. Kick drums hit with authority but don't linger. Acoustic bass instruments are tight. **If you want warmth and bloom in the low end, the Timeless AE is not your IEM.** It's engineered for accuracy, not pleasure in the mid-bass region, and listeners conditioned to V-shaped tuning or dynamic bass slam will find it underwhelming on first listen.

The reward is that the lean mid-bass keeps the mids clean. Nothing bleeds up from below.

### Mids

Midrange is the AE's most polarizing region. **It is clear, fast, and detailed — and slightly recessed relative to the treble.** Vocals have presence and articulation; nothing is obscured or muddy. But compared to a mid-forward tuning like the Truthear Nova or a hybrid like the Moondrop Blessing 3, female vocals can feel slightly clinical. The emotion is there; the warmth is not.

Male vocals fare better — there's enough lower midrange body that baritone and bass vocals feel appropriately weighted. The issue surfaces most with soprano and alto female voices, which can have a brightness at the top of the register that benefits from proper tip selection and source warmth.

Guitars, piano, and strings read as accurate rather than lush. If accuracy is your priority, this is a feature. If you want music to feel cozy, look elsewhere.

### Treble

This is where the AE distinguishes itself from the original Timeless — and from the competition. The original Timeless had a 10kHz peak that could be described, charitably, as "energetic" and, accurately, as "fatiguing on any recording that pushed the upper treble." **The AE smooths that peak** while keeping the extension that gives planars their characteristic air and micro-detail retrieval.

The result is treble that is **extended, detailed, and not harsh on well-recorded material.** Hi-hats have shimmer and natural decay. Acoustic guitar picks attack cleanly. Sibilance is controlled on most tracks — the AE is not a sibilance nightmare the way some budget bright IEMs are.

The caveat: badly mastered recordings, compressed MP3 files, and harshly produced EDM will still sound harsh on the AE because the AE resolves everything. Detail retrieval cuts both ways. If your library is well-recorded FLAC, the treble is a revelation. If you stream 128kbps on a budget compression codec, you will hear compression artifacts you didn't know were there.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Wide. Precise. Flat-out impressive for the price.**

The planar driver's fast transients translate directly into imaging precision — instruments are placed with specificity in three dimensions, not smeared across a vague left-right plane. Close your eyes on a well-recorded live album and you can point at the snare, the piano, the guitarist's amp. The stage has genuine depth perception, not just lateral spread.

This is the performance area where the Timeless AE most clearly punches above its price. Sub-$250 dynamic IEMs — including very good ones — do not stage like this. The Blessing 3 at $320 is better in this regard, but not dramatically so. At $219, the AE's soundstage is a technical achievement.

### Technical performance

Speed. Detail. Separation. These are the planar virtues, and the Timeless AE delivers them in full. Fast transients mean complex passages — dense orchestral arrangements, layered electronic tracks, double kick drum patterns — stay separated and legible where slower dynamic drivers smear. Micro-detail at the extremes of the frequency range is exceptional: the breath before a phrase, the room reverb behind a string section, the subtle pitch variation in a sustained piano note.

At $219, this is the strongest technical performance available in an IEM without stepping up to the Blessing 3 tier.

## Source — this is not optional

**104 dB/Vrms means the Timeless AE needs real power.** A phone jack or a basic $9 USB-C dongle will produce volume, but the bass will be underdeveloped, the soundstage will collapse, and you will conclude — incorrectly — that the AE is thin and unimpressive.

The minimum floor: a **dedicated dongle DAC running 4.4mm balanced**. The Moondrop Dawn Pro, FiiO KA17, or similar mid-tier dongles are the sweet spot. If you own a DAP, run it in high-gain balanced mode. A desktop setup (SMSL DO100 Pro or similar) will reveal another layer of resolution on top of that.

If you are not prepared to provide this source, buy a Truthear Nova instead and come back to the AE when your chain is ready. The AE underdriven is a waste of $219.

## Compared to

### vs. Original 7Hz Timeless ($199 when available)

The AE is the better IEM by every metric: smoother treble, better fit, more complete cable package. The $20 delta is worth it if you're choosing between them new. The original's treble peak was the dominant complaint in every long-term ownership report — the AE addresses it without going dark. **Buy the AE, don't hunt for an original to save $20.**

### vs. Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)

The S12 Pro is V-shaped where the AE is neutral-bright. The S12 Pro has more mid-bass slam and more bass quantity overall; the AE has better midrange clarity and a more refined treble region. Both are technically capable planars with excellent imaging. **Pick by signature**: if you want bass weight and fun, S12 Pro. If you want accuracy and detail, Timeless AE. At $60 more, the AE is the better-tuned and better-built IEM, but not necessarily the right IEM for every listener.

### vs. Moondrop Blessing 3 ($320)

Different tools at different prices. The Blessing 3's 1DD+4BA hybrid has better midrange timbre and coherence than any planar in this bracket — the dynamic driver's natural decay in the bass and the BAs' tonal accuracy in the mids give the Blessing 3 a more organic sound overall. The AE counters with faster transients and more treble air; planar speed is audible and real on complex material. **The Blessing 3 is the better all-rounder; the Timeless AE is the better technical monitor.** If $100 matters, the AE gives you 80% of the Blessing 3's technical performance and loses only in the midrange warmth.

### vs. Tangzu Fudu ($279)

The Fudu is a warmer planar — less treble extension, more mid-bass presence, a tuning that prioritizes listenability over analytical performance. The AE is brighter, more detailed, and a stronger technical performer. **The AE at $60 less is the better value unless you specifically want the Fudu's warmer character.** This is a signature preference call, not a performance call.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Best planar driver under $250 — period
- ✅ Lightning-fast transients, exceptional detail retrieval
- ✅ Wide soundstage, surgical imaging
- ✅ Improved tuning vs. original — treble peak corrected
- ✅ Premium titanium-coated steel shell with magnetic faceplate
- ✅ Modular cable with both 3.5mm and 4.4mm in the box
- ✅ Better fit ergonomics than the original Timeless

**Cons**
- ❌ Needs a real source — underdriven it sounds flat and thin
- ❌ Lean mid-bass — not for listeners who want warmth
- ❌ Slight planar timbre coloration (metallic quality on some textures)
- ❌ Midrange can feel clinical on female vocals

**Buy if:** you have a proper dongle DAC or DAP, you want the fastest and most detailed IEM under $250, and you prefer a neutral-bright signature over warmth.

**Skip if:** you're listening from a phone, you want mid-bass slam or midrange warmth, or you're moving to planars for the first time without a capable source to match.
