The sub-$300 ChiFi bracket is crowded. You've got the [Moondrop Blessing 3]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-blessing-3-review" >}}) pushing down from $319, the [Letshuoer Cadenza 12]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-cadenza-12-review" >}}) staking out the all-BA reference monitor niche at $289, and an army of single-DD and planar contenders each making a case for your money. Into this sits the **Thieaudio Oracle MKII at $299** — a 1DD+4BA tribrid that's quietly earned a strong reputation among enthusiasts who prioritize tonal accuracy and midrange quality over raw technical fireworks.

The original Oracle was well-regarded; the MKII refines the formula with a better BA driver implementation and a reworked crossover that improves driver coherence and treble extension. The result is an IEM that sounds more like a single coherent instrument than a committee of drivers negotiating a truce.

**Short version: the Oracle MKII is the IEM to buy under $300 if you care about timbre, midrange quality, and musical coherence. It won't win every benchmark against the Blessing 3 — but it doesn't need to.**

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 1 DD (10mm, bass) + 4 BA (mids + highs) |
| **Shell** | Medical-grade resin, semi-custom universal fit |
| **Cable** | Modular silver-plated cable, 3.5mm + 4.4mm included |
| **Fit** | Universal, semi-custom shell shape |
| **Tuning** | Neutral-warm, Harman-adjacent tilt |
| **MSRP** | $299.00 |

Two things stand out immediately: the medical-grade resin shell (semi-custom shaped for better passive isolation, which is genuinely excellent for a universal IEM), and the modular cable with both 3.5mm and 4.4mm terminations included. At $299, including balanced out of the box is expected — but seeing brands skip it at this tier is still common enough that it's worth flagging when someone gets it right.

## Who it's for

- **Vocal-first listeners** — jazz, acoustic, singer-songwriter, anything where timbre and tonal accuracy determine enjoyment
- **Classical and chamber music listeners** who need instruments to sound like instruments, not like driver artifacts pretending to be instruments
- **Neutral-warm seekers** who want something that's accurate without being clinical or fatiguing
- **Oracle original owners** who want a genuine upgrade — the MKII isn't a rebadge

Skip it if you want heavy bass emphasis, aggressive V-shaped excitement, or maximum treble sparkle. The Oracle MKII's character is musical and balanced, not theatrical.

## Build, fit, accessories

The semi-custom shell shape is the Oracle MKII's biggest ergonomic differentiator from most IEMs at this price. Medical-grade resin with a shape designed to mirror actual ear geometry means **passive isolation is notably better than typical universal IEMs** — particularly at mid and high frequencies. This matters for commuting, noisy offices, and anyone who uses IEMs as a light noise-isolation tool alongside their music.

The shell is on the smaller side compared to the Blessing 3, which makes the Oracle MKII a more practical choice for smaller ears — the Blessing 3's large shell is a fit concern for some listeners, the Oracle MKII much less so.

The modular silver-plated cable is well-built: low microphonics, proper strain relief, and the modular plug system is secure without being fiddly. The silver plating keeps source impedance low. Both 3.5mm single-ended and 4.4mm balanced are in the box.

## Sound

Thieaudio describes the Oracle MKII as neutral-warm with a "slight Harman tilt" — which in practice means a gentle sub-bass shelf, a linear midrange, and treble that's extended and detailed without being elevated or harsh. It's the kind of tuning that doesn't announce itself, but earns appreciation over longer listening sessions.

### Bass

The 10mm dynamic driver handles everything below the crossover, and it earns its role. **Sub-bass extension is genuine** — the low-end rumble in well-recorded orchestral or electronic music has texture and body, not just presence. The bass is not emphasized for fun-factor; it exists to accurately represent what's in the recording.

Mid-bass is clean and controlled. No bloom, no bleed into the lower midrange. The transition from DD to the BA array is the Oracle MKII's headline technical achievement: even knowing the crossover point exists, it's not audible as a handoff. The bass region doesn't sound like a separate driver doing its own thing — it sounds like a bottom end.

### Mids

This is the standout section and the reason the Oracle MKII earns its reputation. **The midrange is natural, musical, and unambiguously the best in this price bracket for vocals.**

Male vocals — baritones, tenors, the chest resonance that makes a singer sound human — are reproduced with weight and depth. Female vocals reach naturally without going thin or sibilant at the top. The BA drivers in the mid-region are tuned to avoid the "plasticky" texture that characterizes BA implementations when the tuning is off. This is not an easy balance at $299, and Thieaudio clearly did the crossover work to get it right in the MKII.

Acoustic instruments are similarly well-served. Guitar body resonance, piano sustain, violin timbre — the frequencies that make an acoustic instrument sound like the real thing rather than a reproduction of the real thing. Listeners who spend most of their time with these genres will notice the improvement over competitors immediately.

### Treble

Smooth, extended, and non-fatiguing. The Oracle MKII's treble has more air than the [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) at $149 — cymbal shimmer extends further, there's more sense of space in acoustic recordings — while avoiding the elevated upper treble that makes some IEMs exhausting on long sessions.

Cymbals are natural: the metal quality, the decay, the splash — all present without the "harsh spike" character that less carefully tuned IEMs exhibit. Sibilance is controlled. Even on vocalists and tracks that tend to expose sibilant peaks, the Oracle MKII stays composed.

This is not a treble-head IEM. If you want sparkle and air at all costs, the Cadenza 12 or S12 Pro will serve that preference better. The Oracle MKII's treble goal is accuracy and extension, not excitement.

### Soundstage and imaging

Wide for a sealed IEM — wider than you'd expect given the modest shell size. The depth perception is particularly impressive: front-to-back layering in orchestral recordings and complex acoustic arrangements is resolved with clarity that separates the Oracle MKII from sub-$150 IEMs that do well on lateral stage but compress depth.

Imaging is pinpoint. Instrument placement is consistent and precise across the soundstage. This isn't the Blessing 3 level of surgical three-dimensional imaging — but it's close, and at $20 less.

### Technical performance

Detail retrieval is best-in-class at the $300 ChiFi tier. The BA drivers handle micro-dynamics — the soft-to-loud gradations within a phrase, the textural variations in sustained notes — with resolution that competes with IEMs costing significantly more.

The MKII upgrade over the original Oracle is most audible here: the refined crossover and better BA driver selection tightened driver coherence and reduced the mild upper-mid grain that reviewers noted in the original. The result is a more cohesive sound that doesn't call attention to its architecture.

## Source pairing

The Oracle MKII is easy to drive — efficient enough for phone output — but scales meaningfully with better sources. The 4.4mm balanced cable included in the box is an invitation to use it: a balanced output from a FiiO JM21 or Moondrop Dawn Pro adds bass control, stage width, and treble refinement that are clearly audible.

For desktop listening, any clean DAC/amp combination reveals more of what the Oracle MKII can do. The improvements from better sources are a feature, not a sign of pickiness.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Blessing 3 ($319)

Both are the benchmarks at their respective price points, and they're close enough that the comparison is genuinely competitive rather than obvious.

The Blessing 3 has slightly better sub-bass extension and more "wow factor" in raw technical performance — imaging is more surgical, detail retrieval is fractionally higher, and the stage is a touch wider. It's also $20 more and has fit concerns for smaller ears.

**The Oracle MKII wins on midrange timbre** — male and female vocals are more naturally rendered, with better texture in the chest and head resonance frequencies. The tuning is also slightly more forgiving: the Blessing 3's treble can become demanding on poorly mastered recordings; the Oracle MKII stays smooth.

Choice comes down to priorities: **Oracle MKII for vocals, jazz, classical, and acoustic music; Blessing 3 for everything else and maximum technical performance.** Very close. Try both if you can.

### vs. Truthear Nova ($149)

The Oracle MKII is a clear upgrade — not a marginal one. The midrange is more natural and more resolved, imaging is more precise, soundstage depth is more convincing, and treble has more air and extension.

The Nova at $149 is still excellent value. But **the 2x price for the Oracle MKII is justified for enthusiast listeners** who've lived with a Nova and know they want more. It's the upgrade path that makes sense.

### vs. Letshuoer Cadenza 12 ($289)

Different tools for different jobs. The Cadenza 12 is an all-BA reference monitor — analytical, fast, extremely precise, with the slightly lean bass character that comes from an all-BA configuration without a dedicated dynamic driver. It's the right choice for critical listening, mastering reference, and listeners who want the most clinical accuracy available.

The Oracle MKII has better bass — full stop. The 10mm DD brings sub-bass texture and physicality that BAs simply don't reproduce the same way. **The Oracle MKII is more musical; the Cadenza 12 is more analytical.** Both are excellent; the right choice depends entirely on listening priorities.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Best midrange timbre and vocal rendering in the sub-$300 bracket
- ✅ Seamless DD/BA driver coherence — no audible crossover handoff
- ✅ Excellent passive isolation from semi-custom shell
- ✅ Modular cable with 3.5mm + 4.4mm included
- ✅ Non-fatiguing treble with genuine air and extension
- ✅ Pinpoint imaging and strong soundstage depth

**Cons**
- ❌ Bass quantity won't satisfy V-shape or bass-head preferences
- ❌ Not a technical benchmark in the way the Blessing 3 is — slightly behind on raw detail and imaging precision
- ❌ Treble is smooth but not sparkly — treble-heads should look elsewhere
- ❌ Resin shell may polarize buyers who prefer metal builds at this price

**Buy if:** you listen primarily to vocal music, jazz, acoustic, classical, or anything where timbre and tonal coherence matter most. Also excellent for pop, R&B, and indie — the neutral-warm tuning is forgiving and musical across genres without sacrificing accuracy.

**Skip if:** you want maximum bass impact, V-shaped excitement, or the absolute technical ceiling at $300 (the Blessing 3 edges it there).
