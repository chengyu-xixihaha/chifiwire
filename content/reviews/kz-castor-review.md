KZ spent the last decade earning a reputation for ChiFi IEMs that were technically interesting but **tuned for the V-shape masses** — boomy bass, sparkly treble, recessed mids. The **Castor** is the brand's pivot toward modern Harman tuning, with a twist: two versions (Standard / Bass) and on-shell switches that let you adjust the sound on the fly.

Short version: **the Castor is the best KZ in years, but you have to pick the right version and accept some QC variability**. The Standard (Harman-tuned) version at $35 is the right buy.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | Dual 10mm dynamic (stacked) — one for 20-200 Hz, one for mids/highs |
| **Impedance** | Standard: 31-35 Ω / Bass: 16-20 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | Standard: 105 ±3 dB / Bass: 103 ±3 dB |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Cable** | Detachable 2-pin 0.78mm (KZ's "Type C" connector — slightly different from standard 2-pin but compatible) |
| **Tuning switches** | 2 switches on shell = 4 tuning profiles |
| **Shell** | Resin with metal faceplate, lightweight |
| **MSRP** | $35 |

The driver topology is the interesting bit. KZ's **stacked dual-DD** dedicates one driver entirely to sub-bass / mid-bass (20-200 Hz), letting the other driver handle mids and highs without bass distortion eating into it. At $35, this is a real engineering choice — not just slapping two drivers in a shell.

## Get the Harman version. Not Bass.

There are two SKUs:
- **KZ Castor (Harman / Standard)** — neutral with mild bass shelf, what most listeners want
- **KZ Castor (Improved Bass)** — same drivers, much more bass elevation (~10dB lift in 40-300 Hz)

**Reviewer consensus and our recommendation: get the Harman version**. The Bass version is genuinely overcooked — bass bleeds into mids, vocals get murky. The Harman version is what put the Castor on best-buys lists.

If you're a die-hard bass-head, the Truthear Zero Red ($55) is a better bass-IEM than the Castor Bass — get that instead.

## Who it's for

- **First IEM under $50 with detachable cable** and some adjustability
- **People curious about Harman tuning** without spending $80+
- **KZ fans** looking for the brand's cleanest modern tuning
- **Backup pair** for the gym, travel, or a friend you're hooking on the hobby

Skip it if:
- You want the absolute best $35 IEM (Simgot EW200 at $40 is technically superior, just less tunable)
- You hate KZ's QC reputation (legitimate concern — see below)
- You want premium build quality

## Build, fit, and the QC question

The Castor shell is **light resin with a metal faceplate**. Ergonomics are good — KZ has always nailed shell shape — and the IEMs disappear in the ear after 5 minutes.

The on-shell tuning switches are a tiny rocker on each earpiece. Toggle with a fingernail or paperclip. There's no audible *click* between positions, just slightly different sound profiles.

**QC reality check**: KZ historically had inconsistent batches. Driver tolerances varied, occasional dead units shipped, the cable connectors weren't always seated right. **2025-26 production has been noticeably better** — most users get good units. If you do get a defective one, [Linsoul](https://www.linsoul.com) handles returns smoothly.

Don't buy the Castor from random Aliexpress sellers. Buy from an authorized retailer (Linsoul, HiFiGo, KZ official) so you have warranty coverage.

## Sound (Harman version)

### Bass

The dedicated sub-bass driver does its job — there's audible rumble in the 30-50 Hz range that the original Castor lacked. Mid-bass is tight, with appropriate punch on kick drums without becoming bloated.

This is the Castor's biggest improvement over older KZ models: **bass is engaging without dominating**.

### Mids

Female vocals are surprisingly clean for a $35 IEM. Male vocals have appropriate body. There's a slight emphasis around 2 kHz that gives vocals presence, but it's not enough to become shouty.

The trade-off vs. the Truthear Hexa: **mids are less resolving**. You hear the words clearly, but the texture and emotion are flatter than what a tribrid like the Hexa delivers. That's the price gap at work.

### Treble

KZ has historically over-emphasized treble; the Castor reins this in. Cymbals and hi-hats are clear but not piercing. Most listeners won't find it sibilant.

Treble extension is OK but not great — there's a roll-off above ~10 kHz that means the Castor lacks the air and sparkle of the EW200 or Hexa. Acceptable at this price.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Average for the price**. The Castor's stage is intimate but precise. Imaging is good enough for casual gaming. Don't expect the wide stage of the Hexa.

### Tuning switches in practice

The 4 profiles correspond to combinations of two switches (treble lift on/off, bass lift on/off). Honest assessment:
- **Both off**: Default Harman, the recommended setting
- **Bass on, treble off**: Slightly more sub-bass slam, suitable for EDM
- **Treble on, bass off**: Sparkly treble, more "V-shape" presentation
- **Both on**: Mid-V-shape with extra everything

The differences are real but **subtle** — maybe ±2 dB shifts in their target bands. Don't expect EQ-level transformation.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Chu 2 ($20)

Chu 2 is cheaper, simpler (single DD, fixed cable), tuned similarly. **Pick Castor for detachable cable + tunability + bass impact**. Pick Chu 2 if you want the cheapest viable audiophile IEM.

### vs. Simgot EW200 ($40)

EW200 is more technically capable, better treble, similar tuning philosophy. **EW200 wins on raw quality**; Castor wins on tunability and stronger bass.

### vs. 7Hz Salnotes Zero ($25)

Salnotes Zero is the spiritual predecessor — basic, well-tuned, no frills. **Salnotes Zero is cheaper and simpler; Castor adds the tunability**. Both are reasonable first-IEM picks.

### vs. Truthear Zero Red ($55)

Zero Red is the bass-head pick at $20 more. **Zero Red has way better bass control and weight**; Castor is the cheaper "I want bass without bleed" alternative.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ KZ's most refined tuning to date (Harman version)
- ✅ Dual-DD stacked design actually delivers cleaner bass
- ✅ Tuning switches add real (if subtle) flexibility
- ✅ Detachable 2-pin cable at $35
- ✅ Lightweight, comfortable for hours

**Cons**
- ❌ KZ QC reputation — get from authorized seller only
- ❌ Bass version is overcooked (skip it)
- ❌ Treble extension limited above 10 kHz
- ❌ Build is OK but not premium

**Buy if:** you want a $35 IEM that's actually well-tuned, with cable swappability and some adjustability. The Harman version is the right choice.

**Skip if:** you want maximum technical performance at this price (get EW200), or you want premium feel (skip everything sub-$80).
