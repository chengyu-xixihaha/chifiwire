The original 7Hz Timeless launched in 2021 and rewrote what a planar-magnetic IEM could be at a budget price — a coin-shaped metal puck with a 14.2mm driver that out-detailed almost everything near it. Since then 7Hz has iterated, most recently with the [7Hz Timeless AE]({{< ref "reviews/7hz-timeless-ae-review" >}}) (Anniversary Edition), which cleaned up the original's hot treble and improved the shell. The **Timeless II** is the next real step in the line: a fresh **14.5mm planar driver** with what 7Hz calls the "world's first dual-sided coated diaphragm," a **dual-sided N52 magnet array**, and — the headline feature — a **tunable nozzle system** that lets you physically swap nozzles to reshape the treble. At **$229**, it sits just above the AE and squarely in the most competitive corner of the budget-planar market.

So the question isn't "is this a good planar" — 7Hz long ago answered that. It's whether the new driver and the nozzle gimmick justify stepping up from a $160 S12 Pro or a $170 Aether, and whether the II meaningfully improves on the Timeless line it descends from. Short answer: **yes on both counts, for the right buyer.** This is the most versatile and best-bass Timeless yet, and the nozzles are more than marketing.

## TL;DR

- **What it is:** $229 single 14.5mm planar IEM, the latest in the 7Hz Timeless line, with four swappable tuning nozzles.
- **Sound:** Fast, surprisingly punchy planar bass with a slight sub-bass lift; clear, articulate, neutral-leaning mids; extended, airy, non-fatiguing treble. (Headfonics calls the treble "its standout feature.")
- **The hook:** Four nozzle pairs let you tune the top end from smoother to brighter without touching EQ.
- **Build:** ~5g coin-shaped metal shell, textured faceplate, 2-pin detachable cable, 4.4mm/3.5mm combo adapter in the box.
- **Drive:** 15.2Ω / 108dB/V @ 1kHz — runs off a dongle but scales noticeably with real power.
- **Verdict:** The best all-round Timeless yet. If you want one planar that can be both reference and fun, this is it.

## The tunable nozzle system

This is what separates the II from the rest of the line and from most rivals. 7Hz ships **four interchangeable nozzle pairs** that change the treble and upper-midrange response without any EQ. Reviewers describe the set slightly differently, but the consensus shape is:

- **Silver Flat** — the smoothest, least bright option. MoonStar recommends it for treble-sensitive listeners; it "tightens up the low-end for a cleaner presentation."
- **Gold Flat** — MoonStar treats this as the benchmark nozzle: "forward and detailed," especially through the mid and upper ranges.
- **Gold Bullet (domed)** — keeps bass and midrange presence while smoothing and refining the upper midrange; Audio46 notes the domed shape "enhances impact."
- **Silver Bullet (domed)** — the brightest, most treble-forward option, with the most sparkle (and the one most likely to stray from a "natural" tonality on bright tracks).

The practical takeaway: **gold = warmer/smoother top end, silver = brighter/airier; flat = balanced, bullet/domed = more bass impact and texture.** Headfonics specifically calls out using the silver "flower" nozzle to add a touch of lower-mid warmth. It's a genuinely useful system — closer to tip-rolling in effect, but built into the IEM. If you've ever bought two IEMs because you couldn't decide between bright and smooth, this scratches that itch with one.

## Sound signature

The default tonality (gold/flat) is **neutral-bright with a touch of warmth and a lifted, lively sub-bass** — recognizably a Timeless, but the best-rounded one yet.

### Bass

This is where the II surprises. Planar bass is usually fast but flat; here the larger driver and N52 array give it real **sub-bass presence and a sense of punch that's rare for the technology** (Headfonics). MoonStar describes it as "deep, controlled… fast decay" and "well-textured," while Audio46 calls it "punchy and well-controlled" with deep sub-bass rumble that varies with nozzle choice. It's clean and quick rather than thick, but it no longer feels lean — Headfonics rates it "one of the best heard from a planar IEM."

### Mids

**Neutral and clear**, with a hint of lower-midrange warmth. Upper mids are detailed and articulate, rendering vocal nuance well (MoonStar). Audio46 flags the presentation as "slightly analytical" — accurate and precise rather than lush, which is the planar trade-off. If you want syrupy vocals, this isn't that; if you want clarity and texture, it delivers.

### Treble

The standout. **Well-extended, airy, sparkly, and — crucially — non-fatiguing.** Headfonics calls the treble extension the II's defining strength, revealing detail "that uncovers new elements in the music" without turning harsh even on busy tracks. This is the area the original Timeless got wrong (too hot) and the AE corrected; the II goes further and lets you dial it with the nozzles.

### Soundstage & imaging

**Wide and spacious** with good instrument separation and precise imaging (MoonStar), and Audio46 praises an "expansive soundstage with impressive depth and height." MoonStar does note that **depth is closer to average for the price** — width and air are the strengths here, not holographic front-to-back layering.

## Build & comfort

The Timeless line's coin-shaped puck design carries over: a flat circular metal housing with a textured/diamond-pattern faceplate, about **5g per side**. It's light, sits flush, and disappears in average ears over long sessions — every outlet calls comfort good. Passive isolation is **average** (MoonStar) — fine for commuting and home, not the pick for a loud gym or plane if isolation is your priority. The 2-pin cable is detachable, and the 3.5mm + 4.4mm combo adapter means you're balanced-ready out of the box.

## Driveability

Don't let "108dB" fool you — that figure is **per volt, not per milliwatt**, and at **15.2Ω** the Timeless II is a planar that **scales with power**. It will play loud off a decent dongle (MoonStar drove it with a Shanling UA6; Audio46 used a FiiO BTR15), but every reviewer agrees it "truly shines" with more juice — a proper DAP (FiiO M23, iBasso DX180) or a desktop amp tightens the bass and opens the stage. A weak phone-dongle combo will leave it sounding flat and quiet. Budget for a real source.

## How it compares

**vs [Letshuoer S12 Pro]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) ($160).** The S12 Pro is the value benchmark of budget planars: a 14.8mm driver, a fully modular cable, and an easier-to-drive, more overtly **V-shaped** signature. It's $70 cheaper and the better straight-up bargain. The Timeless II counters with a **more refined, more tunable** presentation — four nozzles versus a fixed tuning, cleaner treble, and arguably better-textured bass. If you want maximum value and don't care about nozzles, S12 Pro. If you want tuning flexibility and a more grown-up top end, the II.

**vs [Kiwi Ears Aether]({{< ref "reviews/kiwi-ears-aether-review" >}}) ($170).** The Aether uses a bigger **15.3mm driver** tuned for a smooth, spacious, slightly relaxed sound — the easygoing, non-fatiguing planar. The Timeless II is the more energetic and detailed of the two, with crisper treble and punchier bass, but it asks more of your source and (on the silver nozzles) more of your treble tolerance. Aether for laid-back comfort; Timeless II for detail and adjustability.

Against its own family: the II is essentially **a more capable, more flexible AE** — newer driver, better bass slam, and the nozzle system the AE lacks. For the full field, see our guide to the [best planar IEMs under $300]({{< ref "guides/best-planar-iems-under-300-2026" >}}).

## Who should buy

- **Detail-and-speed listeners** who want the planar advantage but a bass that actually punches.
- **Tinkerers** who love tailoring sound — the four nozzles are a real, usable tuning range.
- **Owners of a proper source** (good dongle, DAP, or amp) who can feed it.
- **Anyone who found the original Timeless too bright** — the II + silver-flat or gold nozzles fixes that.

## Who should skip

- **Phone-only listeners** with no dedicated source — it'll underperform.
- **Warm/lush-vocal seekers** — even the smoothest nozzle is "neutral with detail," not romantic.
- **Pure value hunters** — the S12 Pro gets you ~85% of the way for $70 less.
- **Isolation-critical commuters** — passive isolation is only average.

## Verdict

The 7Hz Timeless II is the **best all-round Timeless to date** and one of the most versatile planars in the sub-$300 class. The new 14.5mm dual-sided driver delivers the line's trademark detail and treble air with notably better bass, and the tunable nozzle system turns a single IEM into several voicings — from smooth to sparkly — without EQ. It demands a real source and won't suit anyone chasing warmth, but for detail-first listeners who like to fine-tune, it's an easy recommendation. At $229 it's a small step up from the AE in price and a real one in flexibility.

**Rating: 4.4 / 5**
