The DAC market has a problem: **most listeners can't actually tell DACs apart in blind tests above the $100 mark**. Audio Science Review (ASR) measurements regularly show $200 DACs hitting the same THD+N and SNR floors as $2,000 DACs. So why buy one over the other?

The honest answer: **features, build, and feature stability**. Once you're past the audibility threshold, you're paying for inputs, software, longevity, and confidence that the manufacturer won't ghost you.

The **Topping E50 II** at $200 (succeeded the original E50 at $269) is the **rational buyer's pick** in this category. AKM Velvet Sound chip, XMOS USB controller, full Bluetooth with LDAC, 10-band parametric EQ via app, measurements that are essentially perfect.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **DAC chip** | AKM AK4497S Velvet Sound |
| **USB controller** | XMOS XU316 |
| **Inputs** | USB-C, optical (Toslink), coaxial, Bluetooth 5.0 (LDAC, aptX-HD, aptX, AAC, SBC) |
| **Outputs** | RCA single-ended, **balanced XLR (TRS)**, **6.35mm headphone out** |
| **PCM support** | Up to 768 kHz / 32-bit |
| **DSD support** | Native DSD512 |
| **THD+N** | 0.0001% @ 1kHz |
| **SNR** | 131 dB |
| **PEQ** | 10-band parametric EQ via Topping app |
| **MSRP** | $199.99 |

The standout features: **AKM AK4497S** (Velvet Sound — known for "musical" rather than "clinical" tonality), **balanced output**, **10-band PEQ in firmware** (not just software DSP — applies system-wide).

## Who it's for

- **Desktop setup** with active speakers or a separate headphone amp
- **Phone-as-source users** who want a Bluetooth DAC (LDAC support)
- **TV / console listeners** who want hardware decoding (USB or optical from PS5/Switch)
- **PEQ enthusiasts** who want hardware EQ that survives source changes

Skip it if:
- You only listen on headphones AND want everything in one box (get an integrated DAC/amp like Topping DX5 instead)
- You want absolute maximum measurements ($600+ DACs measure marginally better but you won't hear it)
- You hate AKM's house sound ("Velvet Sound" is slightly warmer than ESS chips — flat ESS lovers might prefer the SMSL DO100 Pro)

## Build, aesthetics, ergonomics

The E50 II is **small** — about the footprint of a hardback book, 1.5" tall. Aluminum chassis, matte finish, small OLED display on the front. Available in black or silver.

The display shows current input, sample rate, and volume. Volume knob is rotary with push-to-mute. Two function buttons select inputs and toggle EQ.

The build is "Topping standard" — competent, not fancy. Feels like a $200 product, no more no less. The OLED is sharper and more legible than the cheap displays found on competitors.

## Bluetooth — the underrated feature

The E50 II's Bluetooth 5.0 implementation **actually matters** if you stream from your phone. LDAC at 24-bit / 96 kHz means you can play Tidal Master quality wirelessly to your desktop setup. aptX-HD and AAC also supported.

The Qualcomm Bluetooth chip handles up to 3 paired devices. Pairing is fast. Audio drops are rare in a normal office environment.

## Sound

Honestly? **Same as every other competent DAC at this price**. The AKM AK4497S has a slight "warmth" to it compared to ESS chips, but the difference is in the noise floor (the literally inaudible bit) — not in tonality.

What you'll notice:
- **Dead silent** when nothing's playing (131 dB SNR delivers)
- **No distortion** at any reasonable listening level
- **Clean transients** — kick drums hit cleanly, no smearing
- **Black background** that lets quiet passages stay quiet

What you won't notice (vs. a $1,000 DAC):
- Any audible difference

If you ABX test the E50 II against a Chord Hugo TT2 ($5,000), you will fail to identify which is which. This isn't a Topping criticism — it's a category truth.

## The PEQ killer feature

Most DACs offer no EQ. Some offer presets. Topping offers **10-band parametric EQ in hardware**, configurable via the Topping app on iOS/Android.

This means:
- Set EQ once, applies to **every source** (USB / optical / Bluetooth)
- Survives reboots and source switches
- Doesn't depend on your computer's audio software
- Free, no subscription

If you've ever EQ'd a headphone via PEACE/EqualizerAPO on Windows and lost the settings when you switched apps, you understand why this is valuable.

## Compared to

### vs. SMSL DO100 Pro ($219)

DO100 Pro uses dual ES9039Q2M ESS chips, includes HDMI ARC for TV audio, has MQA hardware decoding. **DO100 Pro wins on input variety and ESS neutrality**; E50 II wins on PEQ feature and AKM warmth. Toss-up by use case.

### vs. Topping D50S ($259)

D50S is the older sibling with dual ES9038Q2M chips. **E50 II is newer, costs $60 less, and offers PEQ**. Get E50 II unless you need the D50S's higher-power balanced output for a separate amp.

### vs. SMSL D-6s ($129)

D-6s is the budget option in this segment — single ES9038Q2M, fewer inputs, no PEQ. **Save $70, lose features and Bluetooth quality**. Reasonable if your needs are minimal.

### vs. iFi Zen DAC 3 ($229)

Zen DAC 3 is the UK alternative — Burr-Brown DAC, includes a built-in headphone amp. **Zen DAC 3 if you want an all-in-one**; E50 II if you want pure DAC + planned upgrade path to a separate amp.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Effectively transparent measurements at $200
- ✅ **10-band hardware PEQ** via app — system-wide EQ that survives source changes
- ✅ AKM AK4497S Velvet Sound chip (slightly warm character)
- ✅ Full Bluetooth 5.0 with LDAC, aptX-HD, aptX
- ✅ Balanced XLR output + 6.35mm headphone out
- ✅ Compact, well-built, OLED display

**Cons**
- ❌ No HDMI ARC (DO100 Pro has this for TV)
- ❌ Single-input limitation if you have many sources (must switch)
- ❌ Headphone out is a bonus, not a primary feature — get a real amp for serious listening
- ❌ Topping support / firmware updates can be slow

**Buy if:** you want a transparent desktop DAC with PEQ, you stream from phone via Bluetooth, and you're skeptical of paying more for similar measurements.

**Skip if:** you need an integrated DAC/amp (Topping DX5 or iFi Zen DAC 3), you specifically want ESS sound (DO100 Pro), or you need HDMI input for TV audio.
