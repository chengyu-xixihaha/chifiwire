If you read enough audiophile forums, you'll see two consensus picks for "best execution of the Harman target under $200": the Moondrop Blessing 3 (~$320, technically over budget) and the **Truthear Nova** at $150. The Nova is the cheaper, more accessible option — and many reviewers think it's the better-tuned of the two.

Short version: **if you want the cleanest implementation of a "neutral with sub-bass boost" tuning under $200, the Nova is the pick**. It's not the most exciting IEM, not the most resolving, not the most fun. It's the most *correct*.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 1 DD + 4 BA tribrid (10mm DD + 4 BAs across mids and highs) |
| **Diaphragm material** | PU + LCP (liquid crystal polymer) composite |
| **Impedance** | 14.8 Ω ± 15% @ 1kHz |
| **Sensitivity** | 123 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **THD** | <1% @ 1kHz / 94dB |
| **Frequency response** | 8 Hz – 40 kHz (free field) |
| **Cable** | 4-strand, 448-core OCC, 0.78mm 2-pin, 3.5mm SE |
| **Shell** | Black acrylic with glittery blue faceplate (seashell relief pattern) |
| **Accessories** | 3 sets silicone tips, 2 sets double-flange tips, 1 set foam tips, case |
| **MSRP** | $149.99 |

The standout combination: **4 BAs handle mids and treble, while a 10mm LCP+PU dynamic handles bass**. This split is what allows the Nova to nail both ends of the FR cleanly — BAs are fast and precise for upper frequencies, the dedicated DD has the surface area for proper sub-bass slam.

## Who it's for

- **You've heard "Harman" tossed around and want to actually hear what it sounds like** done right
- **You want a single IEM for genre-hopping** (Harman works for pop, rock, jazz, electronic, classical)
- **You're sensitive to tuning errors** (peaks, dips, weird upper-mid emphasis)
- **You're upgrading from a sub-$100 IEM** and want a meaningful step up in resolution + tuning maturity

Skip it if:
- You want excitement, color, or a V-shape (Nova is intentionally polite)
- You want maximum sub-bass slam (Truthear Zero Red is more bass-forward at less money)
- You want fewer BAs for "natural timbre" reasons (Tanchjim Origin's single DD is the alternative)

## Build, fit, accessories

The Nova's **acrylic shell is a step down from metal options** like the Aria 2 or Letshuoer S12 Pro — but only in weight, not in fit. The shape is universal-friendly; smaller-ear users won't get pressure points, larger-ear users won't feel the IEM swim around.

The glittery blue faceplate with seashell relief is divisive. Some love it (a rare ChiFi design that's not generic). Some find it gaudy. Either way, it's well-executed CMF (color/material/finish).

**Accessories punch above the price**:
- 3 sets standard silicone tips (S/M/L)
- 2 sets double-flange tips (for deeper insertion / better isolation)
- 1 set foam tips
- Hard case

Most $150 IEMs ship with 3 sets of generic silicone and nothing else. The Nova's tip selection alone is worth ~$20 if you bought separately.

The stock cable (4-strand 448-core OCC) is fine — well-braided, low microphonics — but **does not include modular plugs**. You get 3.5mm single-ended only. If you have 4.4mm balanced output and want to use it, budget $20-30 for an aftermarket modular cable.

## Sound

The Nova targets **Harman IE 2019 V2** — the audiophile-community-validated tuning that adds a sub-bass shelf to the Harman target. Most ChiFi IEMs *claim* Harman; the Nova actually hits it within a few dB across the entire FR.

### Bass

Clean sub-bass extension down to 30 Hz with audible texture. The 10mm DD's LCP+PU diaphragm is fast enough to keep up with the BAs, so bass doesn't smear into the lower mids. Mid-bass is tasteful — present, not bloated.

**Listen to "Limit to Your Love" by James Blake** — the sub-bass rumble that defines that track is reproduced with weight but without overwhelming the vocals. Most $150 IEMs either underplay this (single DDs without sub-bass) or overplay (V-shape bass-heads).

### Mids

The Nova's mids are where the BA implementation shows. **Vocals — both male and female — sit at the right level**, neither recessed nor pushed forward. Timbre is natural for a BA-handled midrange (BAs sometimes sound "plasticky" when poorly tuned; Truthear avoided that).

Guitars in a dense mix retain their individual character. Piano sustain is clean. There's no upper-mid shout, no nasal honk.

### Treble

Extended cleanly up to ~14 kHz, with a gentle roll-off above. **No peaks, no harshness**. Cymbals shimmer; hi-hats have appropriate bite without being abrasive. Female vocal sibilance is well-controlled — even on tracks that expose lesser IEMs.

The trade-off: some listeners find the treble *too* polite. If you want sparkle and air at all costs, the S12 Pro or Truthear Hexa will give you more.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Wide stage, very accurate imaging**. The Nova places instruments precisely, with clear depth perception. Better than the Aria 2, on par with or slightly better than the Hexa.

### Technical performance

This is where the price premium over the Hexa (~2x) earns its keep. **Detail retrieval is meaningfully better**, particularly in micro-dynamics and the layering of complex passages. Speed is fast (BAs help here). Coherence between drivers is excellent — no obvious "BA region vs DD region" handoff audible.

## Source — easy but rewarding

At 14.8 Ω + 123 dB sensitivity, the Nova is **trivial to drive**. Your phone will get plenty loud. The catch: the Nova **scales with better sources** — plug it into a Moondrop Dawn Pro, a FiiO JM21, or an iFi Hip-DAC and the bass tightens, the stage opens up, and the resolution increases noticeably.

It's the rare IEM that's both forgiving of mediocre sources AND benefits from better ones.

## Compared to

### vs. Truthear Hexa ($79)

The Hexa is the entry point to Truthear's house sound; the Nova is the mature execution. Nova has better mid coherence (1 more BA, better implementation), more refined treble (no Hexa's slight peak), and improved sub-bass. **Worth the 2x price if you can swing it.** Otherwise the Hexa is 80% there.

### vs. Moondrop Aria 2 ($89)

Aria 2 is warmer, more mid-forward, more "musical reference." Nova is more neutral, more technical, more "true reference." **Pick by preference**: warm and lush = Aria 2, accurate and clean = Nova.

### vs. Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)

S12 Pro is V-shaped planar — bass and treble emphasized, mids recessed, treble sharper. Nova is neutral tribrid — flat tuning, more balanced presentation. **Pick by signature**: V-shape excitement = S12 Pro, neutral accuracy = Nova.

### vs. Moondrop Blessing 3 ($320)

The Blessing 3 is the next step up — same Harman-target philosophy, more refined BAs, premium build. **Worth the upgrade only if you've maxed out the Nova and want the last 15%.**

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ The most accurate Harman 2019 V2 tuning under $200
- ✅ Excellent driver coherence (no obvious BA/DD handoff)
- ✅ Wide soundstage, precise imaging
- ✅ Comprehensive tip selection
- ✅ Easy to drive, scales with better sources

**Cons**
- ❌ Stock cable is SE-only (no modular)
- ❌ Polite treble — won't satisfy sparkle-seekers
- ❌ Acrylic shell feels less premium than metal alternatives
- ❌ Glittery faceplate is divisive

**Buy if:** you want the textbook execution of neutral-Harman tuning, you genre-hop, and you're willing to spend $150 for a noticeable jump over sub-$100 options.

**Skip if:** you want excitement / V-shape, you want metal build at this price, or you only listen on a phone with no source upgrade planned.
