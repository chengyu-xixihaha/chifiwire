The HD 560S launched in 2020 and has been a mid-fi reference point ever since. At $200, it targets the exact price where open-back headphones start delivering genuinely resolving performance — and Sennheiser has tuned it flat enough that you'll hear everything in your music, whether you want to or not.

This is a headphone that gets called "defective" regularly. It's not defective. It's just bright, and that brightness catches people off guard.

---

## Who This Is For

The HD 560S is a tool. It's for people who want to hear what's in their recordings without the headphone adding warmth, bass weight, or personality. If you're mixing, editing, or doing critical listening across genres, this is one of the most accurate $200 headphones available.

If you're listening for enjoyment, particularly with bass-heavy or high-energy music, the HD 560S will work but it won't excite you. That's not a flaw — it's the design intent.

---

## Sound Signature

**Bass:** Extended but not emphasized. Sub-bass rolls off gently below 40Hz. Mid-bass is neither boosted nor recessed. Drums hit cleanly, bass guitars have body but not bloom. If you're coming from a consumer-tuned headphone, bass will sound "thin" — it's not thin, it's flat.

**Midrange:** Accurate and well-separated. Vocals sit at a natural distance, neither pushed forward nor pulled back. Male and female vocals both render without coloration.

**Treble:** This is where most people have an adjustment period. The HD 560S has a measured peak in the upper treble that's more pronounced than the HD 6XX/650 or most "warm" headphones. Cymbals are detailed and forward. On well-recorded material, this adds air and detail. On compressed, harsh recordings, it's fatiguing. If you're streaming lossy audio at low bitrates, you will hear it.

**Soundstage and imaging:** Excellent for the price. Open-back construction gives realistic width; the tuning gives each instrument a clear position. In the $200 bracket, the HD 560S consistently wins in imaging.

---

## Build and Comfort

Plastic-heavy construction, which is normal for Sennheiser at this price (they allocate budget to the driver, not the housing). The build feels secure but doesn't feel premium.

Comfort is outstanding. The velour earpads are large, the headband has minimal clamp force, and the weight (240g) disappears during extended sessions. Multiple users report 4–5 hour sessions without fatigue from fit.

The detachable cable uses a 3.5mm connector on the left cup. Sennheiser's stock cable is functional but cheap-feeling — a basic 1.8m cable is the first thing most owners replace.

---

## "Is My HD 560S Defective?"

Short answer: almost certainly not.

The most common complaint posted in audio communities is that the treble sounds "scratchy," "hissing," or "metallic" on certain tracks. Specifically: tracks with lots of sustained cymbal, harsh female vocals, or heavy reverb on high frequencies.

This is the headphone doing its job. Those elements are in the recording. The HD 560S is one of the few headphones at this price that resolves them clearly enough to hear them. The same tracks on a warmer headphone — an HD 650, a Sony MDR-7506, a Beyerdynamic DT 770 — will mask those artifacts with their own coloration.

If the brightness is genuinely a problem for your use case: EQ down from 8–12kHz by 2–4dB. It takes less than a minute and transforms the headphone for casual listening without touching the midrange.

---

## Compared to Alternatives

**vs. Sennheiser HD 600 (~$250):** The HD 600 is smoother, warmer, and slightly more musical at the cost of some imaging sharpness. The HD 600 is the more natural-sounding headphone; the HD 560S is the more analytical one. Both are excellent. Pick based on whether you're monitoring or enjoying.

**vs. Sennheiser HD 480 Pro (~$180):** The HD 480 Pro is a studio monitoring headphone with an almost completely flat response. The HD 560S has more treble air. For mixing, the 480 Pro is arguably more honest; for general listening, the 560S has more perceived detail.

**vs. Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro (~$180):** Both have elevated treble. The DT 990 Pro is more V-shaped with more bass; the HD 560S is flatter. The DT 990 Pro is more fun; the HD 560S is more accurate.

---

## Verdict

The HD 560S is worth $200 for anyone who wants a true reference point in their listening chain. It will show you what's in your audio without adding or removing character. That's its singular purpose, and it does it better than most things at the price.

If you want warmth, fun, or an easy listen: look at the HD 600, the Hifiman HE400se, or something tuned with a more consumer-friendly curve.

If you want honest: buy this.
