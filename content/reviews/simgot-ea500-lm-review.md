The original Simgot EA500 had a good story: tunable nozzles at $79, metal build, a competent Harman-ish tuning. Then Simgot went back and asked whether a better diaphragm would meaningfully change the answer. The **EA500 LM** — "LM" standing for **Liquid Metal + beryllium composite** — is that experiment at $108.99.

At $109, the EA500 LM sits directly between the Moondrop Aria 2 ($89) and the Truthear Nova ($149). That's a contested bracket. The Aria 2 has the brand recognition; the Nova has multi-driver technical credentials. The EA500 LM's pitch is **metal build, faster transient response from the LM diaphragm, and genuine tuning flexibility** — three things neither competitor can fully match.

Short version: **the EA500 LM earns its price premium over the Aria 2 on technical performance, and its metal construction is embarrassingly good for the money**. Treble-sensitive listeners should start with the Gold nozzle and never look back.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm DD, **Liquid Metal + beryllium composite diaphragm** |
| **Impedance** | 36 Ω @ 1kHz |
| **Sensitivity** | 121 dB/Vrms |
| **THD** | < 0.05% @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 5 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Shell** | Aluminum alloy + medical-grade resin nozzle |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin detachable, 3.5mm SE termination |
| **Tuning nozzles** | **3 included** — Gold (warm), Silver (neutral), Black (bright) |
| **MSRP** | $108.99 |

Two specs jump out. First, **36Ω impedance is high** for a modern ChiFi IEM — this matters for source pairing more than usual (see below). Second, the LM diaphragm is genuinely novel at this price: liquid metal alloys offer higher stiffness-to-mass ratios than standard PU diaphragms, which translates to faster transient response and reduced coloration at high frequencies. Simgot uses a beryllium composite rather than pure beryllium (cost and safety), but the mechanical properties are meaningfully different from commodity PU film.

## Who it's for

- **Aria 2 buyers who want more detail and a better build** — the EA500 LM is the natural upgrade
- **Tuning experimenters** who want three genuinely different sound profiles from one IEM without reaching for EQ
- **Single-DD preference listeners** — the LM driver has more natural timbre than planar or multi-BA alternatives at the price
- **Genre-switchers** — Gold nozzle for jazz and acoustic, Silver for pop and rock, Black for EDM if you're not treble-sensitive

Skip it if you want V-shaped bass emphasis (the Truthear Hexa handles bass differently and the Zero Red is better for bass-heads), or if you need balanced output out of the box (no modular cable included).

## Build, fit, accessories

The EA500 LM's build is **the most obvious thing that separates it from every other $109 IEM**. The aluminum alloy shell is CNC-machined, with a satin finish that catches light cleanly without fingerprinting like polished metal does. Next to a Moondrop Aria 2's zinc-alloy housing, the EA500 LM feels more substantial and more considered.

The medical-grade resin nozzle is **the right call for skin contact**. Simgot uses resin at the nozzle specifically — this is the part that sits inside your ear canal, and resin is warmer, less thermally conductive, and gentler on extended wear. The three included nozzles swap in and out with a satisfying screw-on mechanism. No tools needed; no wobble once seated.

Fit is **medium-depth, universally accommodating** — the shell doesn't protrude aggressively. Most ear tip standards (Spinfit CP100+, Final Type E, the included silicone tips) work. Tip-rolling changes the sound noticeably with the Black nozzle in particular — foam tips tame the top end if you find the Black nozzle too much.

The stock cable is decent: well-braided, low-microphonics, tangle-resistant. The 3.5mm SE-only termination is the expected limitation at this price. No balanced cable is included, which is an easy complaint but a fair one — the Moondrop Aria 2 doesn't include balanced either, and the EA500 LM's 36Ω impedance means it responds more to source quality than most dongles.

Accessories in the box: three nozzles, multiple tip sizes, a carry case. The case is hard-shell, compact, and better than what you'd expect at $109.

## Sound

All impressions below are with the **Silver nozzle (neutral)** unless specified. Tracks used include Nils Lofgren's *Keith Don't Go* (acoustic guitar texture and imaging test), Billie Eilish's *when the party's over* (vocal presence and upper-mid tracking), Massive Attack's *Teardrop* (sub-bass extension and electronic texture), and Rodrigo y Gabriela's *Hanuman* (transient speed and guitar attack definition).

The EA500 LM targets a **Harman-derived curve with slightly elevated mid-bass and a forward upper midrange**. In practice this produces sound that's energetic without being fatiguing on most material — closer to "musical" than "reference-accurate."

### Bass

Sub-bass extension is genuine and one of the EA500 LM's clearest technical wins over the Moondrop Aria 2. In *Teardrop*, the low-end drone has texture and depth — not just presence but articulation. The LM diaphragm's faster transient response shows here: bass notes start and stop with better definition than standard PU-diaphragm single-DDs tend to manage.

Mid-bass is elevated relative to a flat target — kick drums punch, bass guitar has body — but it's not bloated. The elevation adds musicality without obscuring the lower midrange. Listeners coming from Harman-neutral tuning (Truthear Hexa, 7Hz Zero) will notice more warmth; listeners coming from V-shaped IEMs will call it neutral.

The bass character changes **meaningfully with nozzle choice**: Black nozzle reduces the relative bass weight (the upper treble rise changes the perception of the low end); Gold nozzle warms it further. Silver is the middle ground.

### Mids

Forward upper mids are the EA500 LM's personality — this is not a recessed-midrange V-shape. Vocals sit in front of the mix, sometimes aggressively so. On *when the party's over*, Billie Eilish's voice has real presence and texture; on Nils Lofgren's acoustic guitar, the midrange resonance is full and natural.

The single-DD timbre is **coherent in a way multi-driver IEMs at this price can't quite replicate**. There's no crossover handoff to hear, no gap between the character of the low frequencies and the character of the mids. The LM diaphragm handles the full range with a consistent character.

The caveat: the upper-midrange forwardness is real, and on dense mixes (layered guitars, full orchestral passages), it can push certain elements closer than they naturally sit. This isn't distortion — it's a tuning choice, and it's one that many listeners will prefer. It's not neutral.

### Treble

**This is where the nozzle system earns its keep.** With the Silver nozzle, treble is present and detailed — hi-hat articulation in *Hanuman* is sharp and crisp without glare, and cymbal decay tracks realistically. With the Black nozzle, there's a noticeable treble lift that adds sparkle and air; it's exciting on electronic music and modern pop, but extended listening sessions may reveal the elevation. With the Gold nozzle, treble smooths back into the mix — the sound gets warmer, more forgiving, less incisive.

Treble-sensitive listeners: **start with Gold and stay there**. The ear-gain region is tamed, upper treble peaks are flattened, and the overall signature becomes one of the most agreeable in this price range. The detail retrieval is still there; the edge is just filed off.

The LM diaphragm's contribution to treble is the most audible in direct A/B — there's less smearing on fast transient attacks compared to standard PU diaphragms. Guitar pick attacks and drum stick hits have a sharpness that rewards well-recorded acoustic music.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Wide for a single DD at $109** — the EA500 LM creates more lateral space than the Moondrop Aria 2 and noticeably more than the Truthear Hexa. Imaging is solid but not exceptional; instrument positioning is clean and separated rather than precisely localized. In complex arrangements, layers coexist without smearing into each other.

Depth perception is decent but not this IEM's strength. The soundstage spreads laterally; it doesn't project far forward. This is a common characteristic of single-DD IEMs at this price, and the EA500 LM performs at the better end of the category.

### Technical performance

Detail retrieval is **the EA500 LM's best technical argument**: it extracts more low-level information than the Moondrop Aria 2 or Tanchjim Origin at comparable price, and it's competitive with the Truthear Hexa on most material despite the Hexa using more conventional tuning. The LM diaphragm delivers on its promise here — faster attack and decay on transients translate to better definition in complex passages.

Where the EA500 LM concedes ground is **overall coherence compared to the Truthear Nova** ($149, tribrid). The Nova has better driver integration across its frequency range and more precise imaging. That's the technical ceiling the EA500 LM runs into — and it's a ceiling that costs $40 more to clear.

## Source pairing

**The 36Ω impedance is the EA500 LM's most important spec for buying decisions**. At 121dB/Vrms sensitivity (not mW), it's efficient enough that a phone or basic dongle will drive it to volume — but 36Ω is high enough that output impedance matters. **Avoid sources with high output impedance** (some older dongles, budget desktop amps without proper specifications); these will dampen the bass and mess with the frequency response.

Recommended sources: Moondrop Dawn Pro ($30 dongle), FiiO KA5 ($50), or similarly clean low-output-impedance sources. Desktop: SMSL DO100 Pro or Topping E50 II — either reveals the LM diaphragm's speed advantages clearly.

The EA500 LM scales less dramatically than the EA1000 Fermat but more than the Aria 2. A $30-50 dongle is the sensible pairing; anything cleaner is appreciated, not required.

## Nozzle comparison

| Nozzle | Character | Best for |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Gold** | Warm, smooth, relaxed treble | Jazz, acoustic, classical, treble-sensitive listeners |
| **Silver** | Neutral-Harman, balanced across the range | Most genres, default recommendation |
| **Black** | Bright, more treble air, elevated sparkle | EDM, pop, listeners who want extra detail/energy |

The differences between nozzles are **2-3 dB shifts in the treble region** — audible and meaningful, not marketing language. Spend a day with each before committing. Most listeners settle on Silver or Gold.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Aria 2 ($89)

The Aria 2 is warmer, more forgiving, and $20 cheaper. It's the safer recommendation for a first ChiFi IEM and the right answer if someone just wants a smooth, pleasant sound without thinking about it too hard. The EA500 LM is **more detailed, more technically capable, and has a significantly better build** — but it also requires more attention to source pairing and nozzle selection. If you know what you want and can handle the upper midrange energy, the EA500 LM is the better IEM. If you want something that disappears out of the box, the Aria 2 is fine.

### vs. Truthear Hexa ($79)

The Hexa is more analytical and more reference-accurate — it targets Harman more precisely and doesn't add the upper-midrange forward lean. The EA500 LM is more musical and more colored, with better bass slam and a more engaging presentation. **Buy the Hexa if you want a flat reference; buy the EA500 LM if you want a sound with more personality and a better build.**

### vs. Simgot EW200 ($19)

The EW200 is Simgot's $19 entry point with a DLC diaphragm — it's an overachiever at its price. The EA500 LM is **meaningfully better in every area**: more sub-bass extension, more controlled midrange, more treble detail, better build, and the nozzle tuning system on top. The EW200 remains the best argument for spending $20 on an IEM; the EA500 LM is the argument for spending $109 and not needing to upgrade for a while.

### vs. Tanchjim Origin ($99)

The Origin at $99 is warmer, more natural in the mids, and has its own three-nozzle tuning system. The EA500 LM has **more upper-midrange energy, better transient speed, and better build quality** (the Origin uses stainless steel for the shell but the EA500 LM's aluminum feels more refined at this specific price). If you want naturalness and warmth, Origin is the call. If you want more energy and technical edge, EA500 LM wins.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- Metal build at $109 — genuinely unusual, noticeably better than resin competitors
- Three nozzle filters deliver real, audible tuning differences — this isn't marketing
- LM diaphragm improves transient speed and detail retrieval over standard PU
- Sub-bass extension and mid-bass punch are best-in-class at the price
- Forward upper mids give vocals real presence and engagement
- Wide soundstage for a single-DD at this price

**Cons**
- 36Ω impedance requires attention to source output impedance — not all dongles are equal
- No balanced cable or modular termination included
- Upper midrange energy can be too forward on some tracks (Gold nozzle solves this)
- Black nozzle can be fatiguing on extended sessions
- Slightly below Truthear Nova in overall coherence and imaging precision

**Buy if:** you want the best single-DD build quality under $150, you're upgrading from the Moondrop Aria 2 and want a technical step up, or you want genuine tuning flexibility without touching EQ.

**Skip if:** you need balanced output from the box, you're treble-sensitive and unwilling to experiment with nozzles, or your source has high output impedance.
