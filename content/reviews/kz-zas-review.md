Eight drivers for forty dollars. That sentence is designed to impress you, and it works — especially on newcomers who've just discovered the ChiFi rabbit hole. The **KZ ZAS** packs one dynamic driver plus seven balanced armatures into a $40 shell, which sounds like it should embarrass anything near its price bracket.

It doesn't, quite. But it's more complicated than a simple thumbs down.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 1 dynamic + 7 balanced armature (hybrid) |
| **Impedance** | 24 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | ~112 dB |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 40 kHz |
| **Cable** | Detachable 0.75mm 2-pin |
| **Shell** | Resin body, metal faceplate |
| **Tuning** | V-shaped |
| **MSRP** | ~$40 |

One cable note worth flagging: KZ uses **0.75mm 2-pin connectors**, not the 0.78mm standard shared by Moondrop and Truthear. The difference is small but real — cables from those brands won't seat correctly on the ZAS without adapter shenanigans. Stock with KZ-compatible cables if you're buying aftermarket.

## What "1DD+7BA" actually means

Driver count is ChiFi marketing's favorite number game, and KZ plays it well. Here's the reality:

The single **dynamic driver** handles sub-bass and mid-bass. It's the warm, physical punch you feel on kick drums. The **seven balanced armatures** are divided across mid-bass, midrange, and treble duties — multiple BAs stacked on top of each other to cover different frequency bands.

In theory, more drivers means cleaner frequency division, better extension, and fewer compromises. In practice at $40, the BAs are budget-grade units, and **squeezing seven of them into coherent tuning is genuinely difficult**. Driver count doesn't equal implementation quality. The ZAS sounds like eight drivers are present; it doesn't always sound like they've agreed on what to do.

The ZAS is KZ's flagship-approach-on-a-budget play. That's an honest framing — impressive ambition, execution that shows the price.

## Sound

### Bass

This is where the ZAS earns its fans. The DD delivers **elevated, punchy mid-bass** that hits with real impact on hip-hop, EDM, and anything with a driving kick pattern. Sub-bass extension is present — the 20-50 Hz region has rumble, not just warmth. Electronic music in particular sounds energetic and engaging.

The bass presentation is what most people want when they buy their first "audiophile" IEM. It's fun. It's immediate. If you've been listening to Apple earbuds, the ZAS will feel like a revelation.

The criticism: mid-bass can get slightly bloated on complex mixes. Kick drums and bass guitar sometimes blend into each other rather than occupying distinct space. Not a deal-breaker for casual listening, noticeable on anything dense.

### Mids

This is the ZAS's weakest point, and it's where V-shaped tuning extracts its cost.

Vocals are **recessed relative to bass and treble** — they sit behind the mix rather than in front of it. Male vocals particularly lose body. Acoustic guitar sounds thin and distant. Piano recordings lose intimacy.

If you listen primarily to singer-songwriter folk, jazz vocals, or acoustic music, the ZAS will frustrate you. The tuning was not designed for those genres.

For genres where vocals are one element among many — pop, R&B, metal, electronic — the recession is less obtrusive. You still hear lyrics fine. They just don't have emotional presence.

### Treble

Energetic, extended, and occasionally too much of both.

The BAs push treble presence and sparkle aggressively. At moderate volumes, this adds air and detail to cymbals and hi-hats. At higher volumes, or on recordings that are already bright, **the ZAS can become harsh** — there are treble peaks in the upper midrange and lower treble that edge toward sibilance on female vocals and sustained string notes.

Treble-sensitive listeners should be cautious. If you've returned IEMs for being "too bright" or "piercing" before, the ZAS probably isn't for you.

For listeners who like treble presence — and plenty do — the ZAS delivers sparkle and detail that feels dramatic at this price. Just keep the volume reasonable.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Wider than single-driver IEMs** at this price. The multi-BA array creates left-right separation that a single DD at $40 typically can't match. Instruments have room to breathe, and the stage feels more expansive than budget-single-driver alternatives.

Imaging precision is adequate but not refined — you can place instruments broadly left/center/right, but pinpoint accuracy isn't there. For gaming or classical music where you want surgical placement, the ZAS won't satisfy. For most listening, the wider stage feels like a genuine plus.

### Coherence and the real weakness

Here's what a first listen to the ZAS misses, and a second listen reveals: **the DD and the 7 BAs don't always agree**.

At the crossover frequencies — where the dynamic driver hands off to the balanced armatures — there's a tonal discontinuity that appears on certain material. Bass hits feel physically separate from midrange texture. The low end sounds like one IEM; the mids and highs sound like a different one.

This isn't unique to the ZAS — hybrid coherence is genuinely hard to achieve — but at $40, KZ hasn't solved it. More expensive hybrids like the Truthear Zero:Red ($55) handle the DD-to-BA transition more smoothly. The ZAS's incoherence is a real engineering limitation, not just audiophile nitpicking.

## Comparisons

### vs. 7Hz Zero:2 ($25)

The Zero:2 is **technically more impressive** despite its single dynamic driver and lower price. Its tuning is neutral-to-slightly-warm, frequency response is smoother, and there's no coherence gap because there's only one driver. It doesn't have the ZAS's bass slam, but everything it does sounds more organized.

**Choose the ZAS** if you want V-shaped energy and big bass impact. **Choose the Zero:2** if you want a better-performing IEM and don't mind less bass excitement. For most listeners trying to build an honest opinion of ChiFi, the Zero:2 is the more useful data point.

### vs. Moondrop Chu 2 ($20)

Same story. The **Chu 2 is more technically consistent** at $20 less — smoother frequency response, more natural mids, better coherence. It's a fixed-cable, single-DD IEM that performs like it costs twice what it does.

The ZAS has more bass and a wider soundstage. The Chu 2 is a better IEM in almost every technical dimension.

### vs. Truthear Zero:Red ($55)

The **Zero:Red is also V-shaped**, also bass-forward — but it's executed better at every level. Treble is smoother and less fatiguing. Bass is tighter and better-extended. The DD-to-BA coherence gap is narrower. It costs $15 more.

If you want fun, exciting, V-shaped sound in this price range, the Zero:Red is where that sound is done properly. It makes the ZAS feel rough in direct comparison.

## Build and fit

The ZAS shell is **comfortable resin** with a metal faceplate that gives it a premium visual presence above its price. Ergonomics are solid — KZ's shell shapes have been refined over hundreds of models and they know how ears work. Long listening sessions are fine.

Cable quality is typical KZ stock: usable, not special, replaceable. The detachable 0.75mm connection means you can upgrade if you care, though remember the connector compatibility issue noted above.

No major build complaints at $40. The ZAS doesn't feel like it's about to fall apart.

## The honest verdict

The KZ ZAS is a **crowd-pleaser built to impress first-time buyers with a spec sheet**. Eight drivers at $40 sounds like it shouldn't exist. For someone who's never owned a ChiFi IEM before, the out-of-box impression is strong — wide soundstage, punchy bass, sparkly treble.

Spend more time with it, or compare it against the Chu 2 and Zero:2, and the cracks show. The mids are noticeably recessed. The treble can fatigue. The hybrid coherence is uneven. For $40, there are more technically refined options.

That said, the ZAS has its use case. Pure entertainment listening, bass-heavy genres, users who know they want V-shaped fun and don't want to agonize over it. It's not a bad IEM — it's a specifically appealing one that analytical listeners won't love.
