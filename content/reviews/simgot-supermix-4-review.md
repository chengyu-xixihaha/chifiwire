At $109, Simgot already offers the [EA500 LM]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-ea500-lm-review" >}}) — a single-DD with a liquid metal diaphragm and three swappable nozzles that earns its price every day of the week. So why build the **SuperMix 4**?

The answer is the driver configuration: **1 dynamic driver + 2 balanced armatures + 1 planar magnetic tweeter**. A planar tweeter at $109 is not something you see often. Simgot calls this the "SuperMix" arrangement — their shorthand for stacking every driver technology in one shell and tuning them into a coherent V-shaped package. The DD handles bass, the BAs cover mids, and the planar tweeter takes care of the top-end extension.

Short version: **the planar tweeter is the real deal, the bass hits hard, and the V-shape is unapologetically tuned for fun**. If you want neutrality, the EA500 LM is still your Simgot. If you want bass impact and treble air that BA competitors genuinely can't match at this price, the SuperMix 4 makes a strong case.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 4-driver tribrid: 1× 10mm DD + 2× BA + 1× planar magnetic tweeter |
| **Impedance** | ~18 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | ~110 dB/Vrms |
| **Shell** | Metal, universal ergonomic fit |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin detachable, **modular** (3.5mm + 4.4mm included) |
| **Tuning** | V-shaped — elevated bass, slightly recessed mids, extended treble |
| **MSRP** | $109 |

Two specs worth flagging immediately. At 18Ω impedance and 110dB/Vrms sensitivity, the SuperMix 4 is easier to drive than the EA500 LM (36Ω) — a phone or basic dongle gets you there without problems. And the **modular cable with both 3.5mm and 4.4mm terminations included** is genuinely generous at this price; the EA500 LM doesn't include balanced, the Hexa doesn't include balanced, and here Simgot ships both in the box.

The planar tweeter is the headline technology. Unlike full-size planar magnetic headphones that use a planar driver for the entire frequency range, the SuperMix 4's planar element handles only the upper frequencies — essentially replacing the BA tweeter you'd find in a conventional tribrid. The advantage: planar tweeters have more consistent directivity, faster transient response, and extend higher without the harmonic distortion that some BA tweeters introduce in the top octave.

## Who it's for

- **Electronic, pop, hip-hop, gaming, anime soundtrack listeners** — the V-shape and planar tweeter extension are built for genres that reward bass impact and treble sparkle
- **BA treble upgraders** — if BA tweeters at this price feel splashy or artificial, the planar approach is noticeably different
- **Modular cable buyers** — the included 4.4mm balanced termination adds genuine value
- **Multi-driver explorers** who want to hear what a planar tweeter does before spending $150+ on an S12 Pro or Timeless

Skip it if you want accurate timbre for acoustic and classical listening, if mids-forward tuning is your preference, or if you're buying a single IEM that has to do everything — the [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) remains the safer all-rounder at $79.

## Build, fit, accessories

The SuperMix 4 shell is metal, with the ergonomic universal shape Simgot has refined over several generations. The housing sits flush against the ear without the awkward protrusion you get on some IEMs with complex driver configurations. Passive isolation is solid — the metal shell and the standard-depth nozzle provide enough attenuation for commuting and office use.

The 0.78mm 2-pin connector is the ChiFi standard, so cable swaps are easy. The modular cable is the accessory highlight: both 3.5mm SE and 4.4mm balanced terminations are in the box, which means you're not hunting for an aftermarket balanced cable on day one. Build quality on the cable is above average — low microphonics, not spongy in the hand.

Fit is accommodating for most ear shapes. The nozzle bore is standard-sized; most third-party ear tips (Spinfit CP100+, Final Type E) work without issue. Tip-rolling has a noticeable effect on the perceived bass weight and treble sharpness — wider bore tips open up the top end further.

## Sound

Impressions below are with the 3.5mm termination unless noted. Tracks used include Flume's *Never Be Like You* (electronic bass texture and V-shaped tuning test), FKA Twigs' *cellophane* (vocal presence in a recessed-mids signature), Nils Lofgren's *Keith Don't Go* (timbre accuracy), and Porter Robinson's *Shelter* (planar tweeter extension and soundstage in electronic music).

The SuperMix 4 is **unapologetically V-shaped**. Sub-bass rumbles, mid-bass punches, upper mids step back, and the planar tweeter delivers extension and air that reaches comfortably above what BA competitors manage here. This is an exciting signature, not a reference one.

### Bass

The 10mm DD earns its place. Sub-bass extension is deep — in *Never Be Like You*, the low-end synth drone has genuine physical texture, not just presence. Mid-bass punch is elevated and impactful: kick drums hit with authority, bass guitar has density. Decay is controlled for a V-shaped tuning — this isn't bloated, slow bass. The DD handles the low end with better speed than most warm-tuned single drivers at the price.

What the SuperMix 4 bass is not: as technically clean as the [EA500 LM]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-ea500-lm-review" >}}). The EA500 LM's liquid metal diaphragm delivers faster attack and more precise separation in the bass frequencies. The SuperMix 4 trades some of that definition for more slam and sub-bass weight — a conscious tuning choice, not a limitation.

The V-shape's elevated bass isn't subtle. Listeners coming from neutral IEMs will notice immediately. Listeners coming from consumer headphones or gaming IEMs will feel at home.

### Mids

The V-shape's price is here. Upper mids are recessed relative to the bass and treble shelves, which means **vocals sit behind the mix rather than in front of it**. Instruments take priority over singers. In *cellophane*, FKA Twigs' voice has presence and detail — the planar tweeter's resolution lifts fine vocal texture — but the overall impression is that the voice is sharing the stage rather than commanding it.

Male vocals fare better than female. Male voices sit lower in frequency and align more naturally with the DD's mid-bass energy; female voices occupy the recessed upper-mid zone and can sound slightly thin on tracks with sparse production. This isn't an unusual characteristic for V-shaped IEMs — it's a known trade-off of the signature, not a driver integration flaw.

The BA mids handling is clean where it counts: no obvious crossover glare in the lower-mid transition region, no hollow coloration in the midrange body. The three-way driver integration is well-executed for the price.

### Treble

**This is where the SuperMix 4 separates itself from every other $109 IEM.**

The planar magnetic tweeter delivers treble extension and air that BA tweeters at this price point consistently fail to match. In *Shelter*, the high-frequency shimmer on synth pads has a smooth, extended quality — the kind of "reach" that usually shows up at $150-200 in the Letshuoer S12 Pro or Timeless range. Hi-hat articulation in complex electronic arrangements is precise without the occasional "tizz" that BA tweeters introduce. Cymbal decay tracks naturally.

Critically, the extended treble is **not harsh**. Planar tweeters, when implemented well, avoid the sharp resonant peaks that BA tweeters sometimes produce in the 8-10 kHz region. The SuperMix 4's top end sparkles without sibilance on most well-recorded tracks. This is the party trick — and it works.

For treble-sensitive listeners: the SuperMix 4 has more upper-frequency energy than a smooth/warm tuning, but the planar delivery is less irritating than a bright BA tuning at comparable energy levels. Extended listening is manageable for moderately treble-sensitive ears. Highly treble-sensitive listeners should still approach with caution.

### Soundstage and imaging

Strong for the price. The SuperMix 4 creates a wider soundstage than the EA500 LM and meaningfully wider than the [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}). The planar tweeter contributes here: upper-frequency information has more spatial coherence with a planar driver, which translates to better perceived air and separation in busy mixes.

Imaging is accurate and stable — instruments occupy fixed, distinguishable positions. Depth is moderate, which is typical of IEMs in this tier; lateral spread is where the SuperMix 4 earns its stage points. In gaming and anime soundtracks, the wide, energetic presentation is a genuine feature.

### Technical performance

Detail retrieval is high for the price, driven by the planar tweeter's resolution in the upper frequencies. The SuperMix 4 extracts more fine treble information than any BA-equipped $109 IEM can. In the bass and mids, the picture is more conventional — competitive but not class-leading.

Coherence is the multi-driver concern, and Simgot has done the hard work. There are no audible crossover points — the handoff from DD to BA to planar reads as a continuous frequency response, not three separate driver personalities. Given the complexity of a 4-driver tribrid with three different transducer technologies, this is a meaningful engineering achievement at $109.

## Source pairing

At 18Ω / 110dB/Vrms, the SuperMix 4 is not demanding. A phone gets it to volume without noise. A basic dongle DAC (Moondrop Dawn Pro, Apple USB-C adapter) improves dynamics and detail. The 4.4mm balanced cable, once you have a compatible source, provides a noticeably cleaner noise floor and slightly better channel separation.

Unlike the EA500 LM, output impedance is not a pressing concern — the SuperMix 4's lower impedance means output impedance mismatches have less effect on frequency response. Still, a low-output-impedance source is always preferred.

## Compared to

### vs. Simgot EA500 LM ($109)

Same price, different philosophy. The EA500 LM is a single DD with three swappable tuning nozzles and a neutral-to-Harman tuning that rewards source quality and careful matching. The SuperMix 4 is a 4-driver tribrid with a V-shape that works straight out of the box.

The EA500 LM wins on mids coherence (single DD, no crossover) and bass technical precision (the LM diaphragm). The SuperMix 4 wins on treble extension and air (planar vs LM diaphragm in the top octave), bass slam (more elevated), and accessory value (modular cable). **Buy the EA500 LM for neutrality and tuning flexibility; buy the SuperMix 4 for V-shaped excitement and planar treble.**

[Read our Simgot EA500 LM review.]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-ea500-lm-review" >}})

### vs. Truthear Hexa ($79)

The Hexa is a 1DD+3BA tribrid at $30 less. It's more neutral, more analytical, less bass-elevated, and has class-leading technical performance for $79. The SuperMix 4 has more bass slam, meaningfully better treble extension (planar vs BA tweeters), and a wider soundstage — at the cost of neutrality and mids accuracy.

The Hexa remains the better all-purpose recommendation. The SuperMix 4 is the "fun" upgrade for listeners who know the Hexa and want more energy. **Hexa for analytical accuracy; SuperMix 4 for excitement and better treble air.**

[Read our Truthear Hexa review.]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}})

### vs. Moondrop VOID ($149)

The VOID is a single DD at $40 more. It has a more natural, organic timbre — the coherence that only a single-driver design achieves — and the kind of realistic instrument texture that tribrid crossovers give up to gain technical reach. The SuperMix 4 has more treble sparkle, wider staging, and more bass impact, but the VOID sounds more like real instruments in a real room.

These are different tools. **SuperMix 4 for electronic, gaming, and pop; VOID for acoustic, jazz, and anything where timbral accuracy matters.**

## Verdict

✅ **Buy if:**
- You want the most treble extension and air available at $109 — the planar tweeter genuinely delivers
- V-shaped signatures are your preference — electronic, pop, hip-hop, gaming
- You want modular balanced cable included without buying aftermarket
- You're curious about planar technology without committing to $150+ planars

❌ **Skip if:**
- Timbre accuracy matters for your listening — jazz, classical, acoustic favor the VOID or single-DD options
- Mids-forward tuning is your preference — vocals take a back seat here
- You want a single IEM that handles everything — the Hexa at $79 is a more versatile tool
- Female vocals are your primary focus — the V-shape and recessed upper mids can thin them out
