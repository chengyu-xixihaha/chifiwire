Moondrop has a habit of making the "safe" choice easy. The Aria 2 is the safe $89 buy, the Blessing 3 is the safe $320 buy. The **VOID** is supposed to be the safe $149 buy — and that would be an easy pitch if the Truthear Nova didn't exist. The Nova is also $149, also from a well-regarded ChiFi house, and it brings a 1DD+4BA tribrid configuration versus the VOID's single dynamic driver. On paper, the Nova looks like better value.

The VOID's answer to that: **coherence**. A single well-executed driver does things that hybrid stacks still struggle with, even at this price. Whether that trade is worth $149 is what this review is about.

Short version: **the VOID is the best single-DD IEM Moondrop has made**, and the decision between it and the Nova is a genuine one, not an obvious call.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm LCP (liquid crystal polymer) dynamic driver |
| **Impedance** | 10 Ω ± 15% @ 1kHz |
| **Sensitivity** | 126 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Shell** | CNC-milled full aluminum, swappable nozzles (two types included) |
| **Cable** | Modular — 3.5mm SE and 4.4mm balanced plugs included |
| **Target curve** | Moondrop VDSF |
| **MSRP** | $149.99 |

Two things stand out in that table. First, **126 dB/Vrms sensitivity at 10Ω** — this thing is easy to drive and picks up hiss easily; if you're using a noisy dongle DAC you'll hear it. Pair it with something quiet (Apple USB-C adapter, Qudelix-5K, Topping G5). Second, the **swappable nozzles** are a real tuning feature, not a gimmick: the two included nozzle types shift the treble balance by a few dB, giving you a choice between the reference VDSF presentation and a slightly brighter, more extended version. More on that below.

## Build and fit

Full CNC aluminum means the VOID has real weight and no flex anywhere. The shell geometry is ergonomic-universal — slightly angled nozzle, not too deep a fit — and in practice it works for a wide range of ear shapes. Nozzle diameter is medium-large; if smaller nozzles work better for you, the swappable nozzle system is also how you'd reduce fit depth slightly.

The cable is the same modular system from the Aria 2: braided, low microphonics, screw-collar 2-pin connection, and both 3.5mm SE and 4.4mm balanced adapters in the box. At $149, competitors often include one cable and charge you for the other. Moondrop doesn't. That matters.

The included tips are adequate — better than what ships with the Aria 2, closer to medium quality. If bass sounds lean on first listen, try the wider-bore tips from the included set, or swap to Spinfit CP145.

## Sound

The VOID follows the VDSF target curve Moondrop uses across its lineup — neutral-warm, sub-bass emphasis, smooth and non-aggressive treble. What the LCP driver adds to that foundation is speed, resolution, and an absence of the softness that sometimes makes cheap dynamic drivers sound vague.

### Bass

Sub-bass is where the LCP driver earns its price premium. Extension is confident and physical — you feel low notes, not just hear them — without the mid-bass bloat that the Aria 2 occasionally tips into. Kick drums sit at a level that adds weight without muddying the lower mids. Bass guitar lines stay articulate even in busy mixes. This is a cleaner, faster low end than anything Moondrop has shipped under this price, and it's the clearest upgrade over the Aria 2 on first listen.

It's not a bass-head set. Sub-bass presence is full, not pounding. If you need physical slam above everything else, look elsewhere.

### Mids

Single-driver advantage is most audible here. Vocals — male and female — have the kind of natural, unforced timbre that hybrid IEMs at this price often can't match because the crossover between DD woofer and BA tweeter introduces a tonal seam somewhere in the 1–3kHz range. The VOID has no crossover. Guitars have appropriate bite, pianos have proper weight, strings have texture without edginess. Moondrop tuned the mids to be **slightly forward** — vocals sit in front of the mix rather than behind it — which is the right call for most listening use cases.

### Treble

The nozzle swap earns its reputation here. Default nozzle: smooth, slightly rolled off above 12kHz, zero fatigue, zero sibilance. If that sounds like "boring," the second nozzle type adds a controlled upper-treble lift that brings out cymbal definition and air without introducing harshness. Both are listenable for long sessions; the second is more engaging for hi-hat-heavy music and acoustic recordings.

Neither nozzle will satisfy a dedicated treble-head. If you want sparkling upper extension above everything else, the Truthear Nova or a Letshuoer S15 will serve you better. The VOID is not the right tool if treble is your priority.

### Soundstage and imaging

Soundstage is **moderate width** — not congested, not holographic. Imaging is accurate and consistent across the frequency range, which is the single-driver advantage again: there's no zone where the soundfield suddenly shifts because a different driver takes over. Depth is more convincing than width, with clear front-to-back layering. Not a gaming IEM, but more than adequate for music listening.

### Technical performance

This is where the LCP driver justifies the $149 ask. Detail retrieval is better than the Aria 2 by an audible margin — small details (room reverb tails, breath before a vocal phrase, string texture) that the Aria 2 softens are present in the VOID's presentation. Transient response is quick; nothing sounds sluggish or smeared. THD figures aren't published by Moondrop for the VOID, but distortion artifacts are inaudible in normal use.

The coherence benefit is real and not subtle. Play a complex orchestral passage or a well-produced pop track with layered production, and the VOID presents it as an integrated whole. Many hybrid IEMs at this price have a slightly "assembled" quality to their sound; the VOID doesn't.

## Compared to

### vs. Truthear Nova ($149)

The direct competition, and a genuinely close call. The Nova runs 1DD + 4BA, is technically more impressive in measurable terms, and has a more extended treble. The VOID has a more natural timbre, better coherence, and a cleaner, more physical low end.

- **Nova wins**: detail retrieval, treble extension, classical, acoustic, and resolution-first listening
- **VOID wins**: timbre, coherence, vocal naturalness, long listening sessions, genres where musicality beats technical precision

If you listen primarily to rock, pop, jazz, R&B, or vocals, the VOID is the better fit. If you want maximum detail and listen to classical, acoustic, or electronic where microscopic resolution matters, the Nova is the call. Neither is wrong; they're genuinely different answers to the same price point.

### vs. Moondrop Aria 2 ($89)

The VOID is cleaner, faster, better extended in both the bass and treble, and significantly more resolving. The $60 premium is earned. The Aria 2 is still an excellent buy — the mids are roughly comparable and neither will fatigue you — but if you want the best single-DD Moondrop makes, the VOID is where you stop.

The Aria 2 is 80% of the way there at 60% of the price, which remains its strongest argument. If budget is the deciding factor, the Aria 2 doesn't embarrass itself at all. See the full [Moondrop Aria 2 review]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) for the detail.

### vs. Tanchjim Origin ($89)

Similar single-DD philosophy at a lower price. The Origin is warmer and more bass-forward; the VOID is more refined and more linear. Both are worth recommending in their price range — but the Origin at $89 and the VOID at $149 don't really compete directly. The VOID has noticeably better resolution and build quality. If you've heard the Origin and want the next step up that stays in single-DD territory, the VOID is a natural progression.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- Single-DD coherence that hybrid IEMs at $150 can't fully replicate
- LCP driver delivers fast, clean bass with excellent sub-bass extension
- Natural, forward vocal presentation with excellent timbre
- Swappable nozzles give real tuning flexibility without buying aftermarket tips
- Modular 3.5/4.4mm cable in the box
- Full metal build with no compromises at this price

**Cons**
- Treble is smooth to the point of being relaxed — not a set for treble-heads
- High sensitivity (126dB/10Ω) picks up hiss from noisy sources
- Stock eartips are fine, not great — budget for an upgrade
- Truthear Nova beats it on raw resolution and technical measurements

**Buy if:** you want the best single-DD IEM under $150, prefer natural timbre and coherence over hybrid precision, or want a non-fatiguing set that handles vocals and acoustic music exceptionally well.

**Skip if:** maximum detail retrieval is your priority (the [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) wins on that metric), or you're working with a budget closer to $89 (the [Aria 2]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) is 80% of the way there).

**Rating: 4.4 / 5** — A near-benchmark single-DD IEM, held from 4.5 only by the Nova's stronger technical performance at the same price.
