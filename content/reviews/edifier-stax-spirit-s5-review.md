Most wireless over-ear headphones chase the same target: cancel the world, sound "good enough," sell on convenience. The **Edifier STAX Spirit S5** ($499) does the opposite. It drops active noise cancellation entirely and spends the whole budget on a **planar magnetic driver, an 80-hour battery, and a full stack of hi-res Bluetooth codecs**. The pitch is simple and a little subversive: a wireless headphone that an actual audiophile would choose for the sound, not despite it.

After pulling together reviews from TechRadar, Headfonia, ecoustics, Neowin, and Stuff, the consensus is unusually strong — ecoustics scored it **9/10** and called the tuning "excellent" with "best-in-class detail retrieval." The catch is the one you already guessed: no ANC. Whether that's a dealbreaker is the whole question.

First, the name. Edifier owns the legendary **STAX** brand, but the Spirit S5 is **planar magnetic, not electrostatic** — you don't need a STAX energizer or a special amp. It's a normal (very good) closed-back planar that happens to be wireless.

The headline numbers that matter: **80 hours of battery** (you'll charge this monthly, not nightly) and a **codec list that leaves nothing out** — LDAC and aptX Lossless mean you're feeding the planar driver as much data as Bluetooth can physically carry.

## The dual-pad system — read this before you judge the bass

The S5 ships with **two earpad sets, and they meaningfully change the sound**. This isn't a cosmetic choice:

- **Lambskin leather pads** — tighter seal, warmer tonal balance, **more bass weight and better isolation**. The right pick for commuting, bass-forward genres, or anyone who found the stock tuning thin.
- **Mesh/fabric pads** — more breathable, cooler over long sessions, **airier presentation with a slightly leaner low end** and a touch more openness up top.

Most criticism of "not enough bass" or "too warm" online traces back to which pads the reviewer had on. **Swap pads before you form an opinion.** It's effectively two tunings in one box.

## Sound

Across reviews the S5's signature is described as **balanced, refined, and clean** — closer to a well-tuned wired planar than to a typical bass-boosted wireless can. ecoustics singled out **imaging and detail retrieval** as best-in-class for a wireless headphone.

### Bass

With the **leather pads**, low end has real weight and planar speed — fast, textured, controlled, never bloated. Sub-bass extension is good without the artificial shelf most wireless tunings lean on. With the mesh pads it tightens and lightens. Either way it's *accurate* bass, not *big* bass.

### Mids

The strongest region. Vocals and instruments sit at a natural level with the timbre planar drivers are prized for — no upper-mid shout, no plasticky coloration. This is where the S5 reads as "audiophile" rather than "consumer Bluetooth."

### Treble

Extended and clean with planar speed in the transients. Reviewers note it stays detailed without turning harsh or sibilant — cymbals shimmer, but long sessions don't fatigue. Slightly more air with the mesh pads.

### Soundstage and imaging

For a closed-back, the staging is **wide and precise** — repeatedly called out as a highlight. Instrument separation and depth are well beyond what wireless ANC flagships (Sony, Bose) manage, because those spend driver budget on noise cancellation circuitry the S5 simply doesn't carry.

### Wired mode

Plug in the 3.5mm cable and the S5 runs **passively as a wired planar**, no battery required — handy when it dies, or when you want to skip Bluetooth entirely off a dongle or desktop amp.

## Compared to

### vs. Sony WH-1000XM6 / Bose QC Ultra (wireless ANC flagships)

Different tools. The Sony and Bose win decisively on **ANC and call quality** — they're built for planes and open offices. The S5 wins decisively on **sound quality, detail, and soundstage**, plus crushes them on battery (80h vs ~30h). If you fly weekly, buy the Sony. If you want the best-*sounding* wireless headphone and isolate passively, the S5 is in another tier.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is the Edifier STAX Spirit S5 electrostatic?**
No. Despite the STAX name (Edifier owns the brand), the Spirit S5 is **planar magnetic**, not electrostatic. You don't need a STAX energizer or special amp — it works like any normal wireless/wired headphone.

**Does the STAX Spirit S5 have noise cancelling (ANC)?**
No. The S5 has **no active noise cancellation** — only passive isolation from the closed-back design and leather pads. This is its biggest trade-off versus Sony/Bose wireless flagships, and the budget instead goes into sound quality and battery.

**How long does the STAX Spirit S5 battery last?**
Up to **80 hours** per charge, with fast charging giving roughly 10 hours of playback from a 10-minute top-up. You charge it monthly, not nightly.

**Can you use the STAX Spirit S5 wired?**
Yes. It has a **3.5mm analog input** and runs passively as a wired planar — useful when the battery dies or you want to bypass Bluetooth from a dongle DAC or desktop amp.

**What's the difference between the two earpads?**
The **lambskin leather** pads give a tighter seal, warmer tone, more bass, and better isolation. The **mesh** pads run cooler and sound airier with a leaner low end. Swap them to taste — it's effectively two tunings.

**Which Bluetooth codecs does it support?**
Bluetooth 5.4 with **LDAC, LHDC, aptX Lossless / Adaptive / HD, AAC, and SBC** via the Qualcomm QCC5181 / Snapdragon Sound platform — a complete hi-res codec stack.
