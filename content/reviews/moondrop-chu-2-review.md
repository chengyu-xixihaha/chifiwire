When the original Moondrop Chu launched at $20 in 2022, it became the default first-IEM recommendation overnight. The follow-up — the **Chu 2** — keeps the price ($19.99) and the basic recipe but refines the tuning and tightens the build. The question is whether the changes are enough to make this *the* new $20 pick over the Truthear Gate, the 7Hz Salnotes Zero, and the rest of the crowded entry-level field.

Short version: **the Chu 2 is the most refined VDSF tuning Moondrop has shipped under $30**, but the fixed cable is a real drawback in 2026. Read on for who should buy it.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm dual-cavity dynamic |
| **Impedance** | 28 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 120 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 12 Hz – 25 kHz |
| **Cable** | **Fixed** (non-detachable), 3.5mm SE termination, in-line mic version available |
| **Shell** | Zinc alloy, mirror finish |
| **Tuning** | Moondrop VDSF (their Harman variant) |
| **Accessories** | 6 sets silicone tips (S/M/L × 2), sleeve pouch, no hard case |
| **Weight** | ~16 g per side |
| **MSRP** | $19.99 (no mic) / $22.99 (with mic) |

The standout: **zinc alloy shell at $20**. Most competitors use resin (Truthear Gate, Salnotes Zero). Moondrop's metal shell gives the Chu 2 a "premium" feel that's rare at this price.

The catch: **fixed cable**. If it breaks, IEM is dead. We'll come back to this.

## Who it's for

- **Your first audiophile IEM**, replacing Apple EarPods or random Amazon junk
- **Vocal music listener** (pop, K-pop, acoustic, ballads)
- **Gift buyer** for a friend who's curious about "real headphones"
- **Secondary pair** for travel / gym / backup
- **Long-session listener** who hates fatiguing treble

Skip it if:
- You want detachable cable (get Truthear Gate at $22 instead)
- You're a bass-head (sub-bass is polite — Truthear Zero Red for $55 is the bass IEM)
- You want analytical / detailed sound (the Chu 2 is smooth, not sharp)

## Build, fit, accessories

The Chu 2's **zinc alloy shell with mirror finish** is the visual standout. They look more expensive than they are. The shape is universal — small enough for smaller ears, ergonomic enough for larger ones, no pressure points on long wear.

Weight is ~16 g per side — noticeable but not heavy. Comfortable for 2-3 hour sessions; after that the metal warmth (or coolness, depending on your jacket) becomes a factor.

**Six sets of silicone tips** is generous for $20. Most competitors ship 3 sets. The variety covers most ear canal sizes well, though serious tip swappers will still buy Spinfit CP145 ($8) for the seal upgrade.

The sleeve pouch is **basic**. Most listeners will buy a $5 hard case from Amazon or just toss the IEMs in a jacket pocket (risky). No case is the only real omission for $20.

## The cable — the real consideration

**Fixed cable. Cannot be replaced. Cannot be upgraded to balanced.**

This matters in 3 ways:
1. **Longevity**: cable failures account for ~60% of IEM deaths. Fixed = total loss.
2. **No balanced upgrade**: if you eventually buy a DAP with 4.4mm output, you can't use it.
3. **No cable swap aesthetics**: niche but real for some buyers.

Counter-argument: **at $20, the IEM IS the cable**. Replacing the whole IEM if the cable dies is $20. That's less than a quality aftermarket cable for a detachable IEM. So the math isn't as bad as it looks.

Still, **if you can spare $2 more, the Truthear Gate gives you detachable cable + similar sound**. We covered this in [Chu 2 vs Truthear Gate](/vs/moondrop-chu-2-vs-truthear-gate/).

## Sound

The Chu 2 follows **Moondrop's VDSF target curve** — their Harman-derivative tuning that biases warmer in the mid-bass. The result: an easy-listening, vocal-forward sound that works for most music without offending anyone.

### Bass

Mid-bass is **tight and polite** — kick drums punch but don't rumble. Sub-bass extension is decent (12 Hz claimed, audible to ~30 Hz) but lacks the slam of bass-focused IEMs.

For electronic music or hip-hop where you want bass to dominate, **the Chu 2 will feel underweighted**. For everything else, the bass is well-balanced.

### Mids

**The standout**. Female vocals have an airy texture that sub-$50 IEMs rarely achieve — the VDSF tuning's slight upper-mid emphasis helps voices float above the mix. Male vocals have appropriate chest resonance. Guitars in a mix retain their texture.

If you came to ChiFi for vocals, the Chu 2 will satisfy.

### Treble

**Smooth, polite, non-fatiguing**. Cymbals are present but not sparkly. Hi-hats have definition but no harshness. Sibilance ("s" and "sh" sounds in vocals) is well-controlled.

Trade-off: **lacks excitement on treble-heavy genres**. If you want shimmer on jazz brushed cymbals or sparkle on classical strings, the Truthear Hexa ($79) is the upgrade.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Intimate stage**. Vocals and instruments feel close, not spread wide. Imaging is decent — you can place instruments roughly — but not pinpoint. For an IEM at this price, expected; don't expect concert-hall stage.

### Technical performance

Detail retrieval is class-appropriate. You hear the words; you don't always hear the breaths between them. Transient response is reasonable for a single DD. There's no obvious technical magic — just a competent driver with a thoughtful tune.

## Source — your phone is fine

At 28 Ω + 120 dB sensitivity, the Chu 2 is **easy to drive from anything**. Phone headphone jack, $9 Apple USB-C dongle, any source. Doesn't scale meaningfully with better sources — a $300 DAP won't make it sound twice as good.

This is part of the appeal: **plug into your phone, hear good sound, move on with life**.

## Compared to

### vs. Truthear Gate ($22)

The Gate is the **cable-detachable answer to the Chu 2**. Sound is slightly more energetic (more sub-bass, more treble sparkle). Build is resin (vs Chu 2 metal). **Get the Gate** if you want cable swap-ability.

[Read our full Chu 2 vs Gate comparison](/vs/moondrop-chu-2-vs-truthear-gate/).

### vs. 7Hz Salnotes Zero ($25)

Salnotes Zero is warmer with more sub-bass body. **Chu 2 wins for vocal-forwardness; Salnotes wins for body and weight**. Both are reasonable first-IEM picks.

### vs. Tangzu Wan'er S.G. ($20)

The Wan'er is slightly warmer than Chu 2 with stronger mid-bass. Build is plastic vs metal. **Chu 2 wins on build; Wan'er wins on warmth and bass body**.

### vs. Simgot EW200 ($40)

EW200 is 2x the price but **significantly more capable technically** — better treble extension, faster transients, more detail. Step up to EW200 once you've outgrown the Chu 2's polite presentation.

### vs. Moondrop Aria 2 ($89)

Aria 2 is 4.5x the price. **Don't skip the Chu 2 to jump straight to Aria 2** unless you have a clear preference. The Chu 2 is the audition. If you love its tuning, the Aria 2 is the natural upgrade path.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ The most refined VDSF tuning under $30
- ✅ Premium zinc alloy shell (rare at this price)
- ✅ Excellent vocal reproduction
- ✅ Smooth, non-fatiguing treble
- ✅ Easy to drive from any source
- ✅ Six sets of tips included

**Cons**
- ❌ **Fixed cable** — if it breaks, IEM is dead
- ❌ No balanced (4.4mm) upgrade path
- ❌ Bass-heads will find it polite
- ❌ Treble lacks excitement on cymbal-heavy genres
- ❌ No hard case included

**Buy if:** you want the most refined $20 IEM tuning, you listen primarily to vocals / acoustic / pop, and you don't mind the fixed cable (or accept "IEM = cable, $20 to replace if dead").

**Skip if:** you want detachable cable (Truthear Gate at $22 is the answer), or you want excitement / sub-bass slam (look at $50+ options).
