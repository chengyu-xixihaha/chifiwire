The original Hype 4 was a bass-forward, warm crowd-pleaser. The **Thieaudio Hype 4 MKII at $399** is not that IEM — it's a comprehensive redesign that swaps the resin shell for CNC-machined aluminum, drops the original's Sonion balanced armatures in favor of an all-Knowles array, and re-tunes the whole thing toward a cleaner, brighter, more technical presentation. The headline hardware is a 6-driver hybrid: two 8mm dynamic drivers running Thieaudio's second-generation IMPACT² subwoofer system, plus four Knowles BAs split across low-mids, mid-treble, and an ultra-tweeter parked in the nozzle, all fed through a 4-way crossover.

This is Thieaudio reaching upmarket without changing the price tag. The build now looks and feels like a flagship — Metalverse notes the bundled modular cable is "the same cable as the Thieaudio Valhalla," the brand's $2,000 flagship — and the tuning has shifted from "fun and warm" to "clean and energetic." The question this review answers is whether the MKII's new bright-leaning voice is an upgrade or just a different flavor, and where it lands against the original Hype 4 and Thieaudio's own [Oracle MKII]({{< ref "reviews/thieaudio-oracle-mkii-review" >}}) sitting $100 below it.

## TL;DR

- **Price:** $399
- **Drivers:** Hybrid — 2× 8mm dynamic (Gen 2 IMPACT² subwoofer system) + 4× Knowles BA (dual RAB low-mids, ED-33465 mid-treble, RAD-33518 ultra-tweeter), 4-way crossover
- **Sound signature:** Clean, U-shaped, neutral-with-bass-boost — leaner and brighter than the original, with far-extending treble and strong technicalities
- **Who it's for:** Detail-seekers and treble-heads who want flagship-grade resolution and staging around $400 in one box
- **Verdict:** A genuine technical step up over the original Hype 4, near the top of its price bracket — but the brighter tuning can run hot, so treble-sensitive listeners should audition first

## Sound signature

Across all three reviews, the through-line is the same: the MKII is a U-shaped, transparent, energetic tuning that trades the original's warmth for clarity. Headfonia summed it up as "neutral-with-bass-boost" with "a highly transparent core," while Metalverse called it "very clean with a generally bright and energetic sound."

### Bass

The dual-DD IMPACT² system anchors a sub-bass-led low end. Metalverse measured the sub-bass peaking around 50Hz with roughly 9dB of elevation, and noted a deliberate "slight tuck" in the mid-bass that "prioritizes clarity and prevents warmth from overwhelming the midrange." Headfonics describes the result as a low end with "a proper floor now," reaching "deep with real rumble" while staying "tight and controlled," with mid-bass that hits "with a clean punch" rather than a boosted thump. The consensus: bass texture is above average and the tonality natural, but it's noticeably more polite than the original Hype 4. Bass-heads who loved the first model's bloom may find this version reserved.

### Mids

Metalverse credits a "lush 400Hz hump" for adding body, and rates vocal reproduction as "exceptional across the spectrum" — male vocals "full-bodied and authoritative," female vocals clear and detailed. The caveat is positioning: Metalverse felt vocals "sit back with the instruments" slightly, while Headfonia heard a "clean and linear midrange" with "lively upper mids." So the mids are well-rendered and uncolored, but listeners who want vocals pushed forward and intimate (as the original delivered) won't get that here.

### Treble

This is where the MKII makes its biggest leap — and takes its biggest risk. The Knowles RAD-33518 ultra-tweeter in the nozzle delivers what Metalverse described as "extension that rivals electrostatic and planar magnetic implementations." Headfonics found the treble "energetic and lively" with "good sparkle and air" and strong upper separation, praising the lower-treble "crunch" where "snares crack just right and cymbals have actual bite." But both outlets flag the same trade-off: it can run hot. Metalverse warned that "brass, cymbal hits, or other very high frequency instruments can come across 'hot'" at higher volumes and that "treble-sensitive listeners will definitely want to be aware," and Headfonics echoed the caution about bright pairings. The detail is genuinely excellent; the headroom for fatigue is real.

### Soundstage & imaging

Unanimous strength. Metalverse called the stage "genuinely impressive, particularly for a closed-back IEM," with "substantial width that extends beyond the confines of your head" and "excellent" imaging precision for pinpointing instruments. Headfonia described the set as "agile, open, precise, and energetic," and Headfonics highlighted "strong imaging and a spacious soundstage." The combination of the bright tilt and the strong separation is what makes the MKII read as so technical.

## Build & comfort

The move to a CNC-machined, anodized aluminum shell is the most obvious upgrade over the resin original. Despite the metal, each earpiece weighs only around 6–7g, and Metalverse found the ergonomic shaping comfortable for long sessions with "admirable" passive isolation. The modular cable — ultra-pure copper litz with both 3.5mm and 4.4mm plugs in the box — has good ergonomics and minimal microphonics. At $399, including balanced termination out of the box is the right call, and the overall package presents well above its price.

## How it compares

**vs. the original Hype 4:** This is the clearest fork in the road. The original used Sonion BAs and was tuned bassier and warmer, with vocals pushed forward — a fun, "bass-head" set. The MKII switches to Knowles drivers throughout and re-voices toward a cleaner, brighter presentation, with measurable gains in detail retrieval (especially treble) and a wider soundstage. Metalverse framed it bluntly: the original suits bass-heads, the MKII appeals to treble-heads. If you bought the original specifically for its low-end warmth, the MKII is not a straight upgrade — it's a different IEM that happens to be more technically capable. Headfonia went further, calling the MKII a "mini Monarch MKIV" and "the budget-conscious man's Monarch" — high praise that frames it as the closest Thieaudio has gotten to its flagship line at this price.

**vs. the [Thieaudio Oracle MKII]({{< ref "reviews/thieaudio-oracle-mkii-review" >}}) ($299):** These two are aimed at different listeners. The Oracle MKII is a 1DD+4BA tribrid voiced neutral-warm with a Harman-adjacent tilt — it's the timbre-and-midrange specialist for vocal and acoustic music, and the safer pick if you're sensitive to bright treble. The Hype 4 MKII is the more aggressive, dual-DD, bass-and-treble-extended technical monitor: bigger stage, more sub-bass slam, more sparkle, more energy — at the cost of $100 more and some treble risk. If your library leans acoustic and you prize coherence, the cheaper Oracle MKII may genuinely serve you better; if you want excitement, slam, and resolution, the Hype 4 MKII justifies its premium. ## Who should buy it

- Detail-seekers who want flagship-grade resolution, air, and staging around $400 in a single box
- Treble-heads and former-original-Hype-4 owners who found the original too warm or too thick in the mids
- Listeners who want sub-bass slam from a dynamic driver but a clean, uncluttered midrange above it
- Anyone who values a premium aluminum build and a modular balanced cable included out of the box

## Who should skip it

- Treble-sensitive listeners — the upper treble can come across hot on bright tracks and at higher volumes
- Bass-heads who loved the original Hype 4's warm, forward low end (the MKII is leaner and more polite)
- Vocal-first listeners who want intimate, pushed-forward mids — the Oracle MKII is the better-tuned, cheaper choice here
- Anyone chasing maximum warmth and forgiveness rather than clarity and technical performance

## Verdict

The Hype 4 MKII does exactly what Thieaudio set out to do: it turns a warm, bassy crowd-pleaser into a cleaner, brighter, more technical monitor with a genuinely flagship-feeling build. The dual-DD IMPACT² bass is tight and deep, the all-Knowles BA array resolves beautifully, and the soundstage and imaging punch well above $399. Headfonia's "mini Monarch MKIV" framing isn't hyperbole — across all three reviews, the technical performance is the standout, and it's hard to find this level of agility and resolution at the price.

The one asterisk, and the reason this lands at 4.4 rather than higher, is the treble. The ultra-tweeter buys you electrostatic-like extension and air, but both Metalverse and Headfonics independently flagged that it can run hot — a real consideration for treble-sensitive ears that knocks it just short of a universal recommendation. If you know you like bright, energetic, detail-first tunings, the Hype 4 MKII is close to the best you can buy near $400. If you don't, audition it first or look at the warmer, cheaper Oracle MKII. Either way, this is a confident, well-executed redesign that earns its place near the top of the sub-$500 bracket.
