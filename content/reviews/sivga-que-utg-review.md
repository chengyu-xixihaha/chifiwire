Most sub-$100 single dynamic drivers chase the same warm, safe, crowd-pleasing tuning. The **Sivga Que UTG** ($89) takes a different bet: a **10mm flat glass diaphragm** — ultra-thin glass (UTG) sourced from Japanese specialist NEG — instead of the usual polymer or composite dome. The pitch is clarity and detail without the harshness that brings, in a price bracket where "detailed" usually means "bright and fatiguing."

Short version: **it works**. The Que UTG is one of the few sub-$100 IEMs aimed at the listener who wants resolution and stage over comfort-warmth, and it backs that up with a build and accessory set that look like they cost more.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | 10mm flat glass diaphragm dynamic (NEG ultra-thin glass) |
| **Magnetic system** | Single magnet, dual-cavity magnetic circuit |
| **Impedance** | 32 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 103 ± 3 dB |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 20 kHz |
| **Faceplate** | South American green sandalwood (real wood) |
| **Cable** | Detachable, **modular 3.5mm SE + 4.4mm balanced** plugs included |
| **Accessories** | Leather case, silicone tips (S/M/L, 2 pairs each), manual |
| **MSRP** | $89 (some retailers list ~$98) |

The headline is the **glass diaphragm**. Glass is stiff and light, which in theory gives you fast transients and clean treble without the ringing that plagues cheaper metal-coated drivers. Sivga pairs it with a flexible PU surround and a dual-cavity magnetic circuit to keep distortion down. It's genuinely uncommon tech at this price.

## Who it's for

- **You want detail and soundstage** more than a thick, warm low end
- **You've been let down by "bright" budget IEMs** and want clarity *without* the fatigue
- **You use both a phone and a balanced source** — the modular 3.5/4.4 cable means you don't pick one
- **You like a real-wood, grown-up aesthetic** instead of glittery resin

Skip it if:
- You want a bass-cannon V-shape ([Truthear Zero Red]({{< ref "/reviews/truthear-zero-red-review" >}}) or a planar like the Letshuoer S12 Pro are more your speed)
- You want the textbook-neutral Harman reference at all costs ([Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "/reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) is the safer measurement pick)

## Build, fit, accessories

This is where the Que UTG immediately reads as more than $89. The shells use a **CNC metal body with a real green sandalwood faceplate** — every pair has slightly different grain, which is the point. Fit is medium-sized and ergonomic; the nozzle isn't unusually long or wide, so most ears get a quick seal.

The accessory set is what genuinely surprises:

- **Modular cable with both 3.5mm single-ended and 4.4mm balanced plugs** — this is rare under $100. You get balanced output for a DAP or dongle *and* a phone-friendly SE plug, no aftermarket cable required.
- Leather carrying case (not the usual cheap pleather puck)
- Silicone tips in S/M/L, two pairs of each

Most IEMs at this price ship one plug and a drawstring bag. Sivga's bundle is closer to what you'd expect at $150.

## Sound

The Que UTG is tuned **balanced with a slight warm lean** — not strictly neutral, not V-shaped, not bright. It's the kind of tuning that's easy to listen to for hours but still resolves detail, which is a harder balance to strike than it sounds.

### Bass

The low end is **pronounced and muscular** with real sub-bass extension — it rumbles with authority when a track calls for it. What matters at this price is *control*: transients are quick, and the bass stays tight rather than bleeding into the mids. Impactful, not bloated, and never fatiguing on bass-heavy tracks.

### Mids

Mids combine **clarity, warmth, and natural tonal balance**. Vocals sit just forward enough to feel present and lifelike without dominating the mix, and there's no obvious dip or recession. Acoustic instruments and orchestral arrangements come through natural and resonant — this is a strong pick for vocal-forward and acoustic listeners. (If vocals are your priority, also see our [best ChiFi IEMs for vocals]({{< ref "/guides/best-chifi-iems-for-vocals" >}}).)

### Treble

This is where the glass diaphragm earns its keep. Highs are **sharp and detailed but articulate and restrained** — there's air and openness with a natural roll-off that keeps sparkle alive without etched brightness. The glass driver delivers the resolution you'd hope for from the material while sidestepping the harshness that usually comes with "detailed" budget tuning.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Soundstage is exceptional for the price** — width and depth that create a genuinely holographic sense of space, not the in-your-head presentation most sub-$100 single DDs settle for. Imaging is precise, letting you place subtle cues in the mix intuitively. This is the Que UTG's standout technical trait.

## Source — easy, but feed it balanced if you can

At **32 Ω and 103 dB**, the Que UTG is easy enough to drive from a phone dongle, but it's slightly less sensitive than ultra-easy IEMs like the Aria 2, so a proper dongle DAC (FiiO KA series, Moondrop Dawn) tightens the bass and opens the stage. The bundled 4.4mm balanced plug is there for a reason — if you have balanced output, use it; the Que UTG scales with cleaner power.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Aria 2 ($89)

Same price, very different goals. The [Aria 2]({{< ref "/reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) is warmer and more mid-forward — a "musical reference." The Que UTG is more about **resolution and stage**, with a livelier, airier top end thanks to the glass driver. **Pick by priority**: smooth musicality and comfort tuning → Aria 2; detail, air, and soundstage → Que UTG. The Que UTG also wins hard on accessories (modular cable, real wood, leather case).

### vs. Truthear Hexa ($79)

The [Hexa]({{< ref "/reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) is the safe measurement pick — a 1DD+3BA tuned close to neutral/Harman. The Que UTG is a **single DD** with more warmth, more sub-bass weight, and (to many ears) a more natural, coherent presentation without the BA timbre debate. The Hexa edges it for strict tonal accuracy; the Que UTG is the more engaging single-driver listen and the better-built package.

### vs. Tanchjim Origin ($120)

The [Tanchjim Origin]({{< ref "/reviews/tanchjim-origin-review" >}}) is the reference single DD if you want clean neutrality and don't mind spending more. The Que UTG undercuts it by ~$30, leans slightly warmer, and brings the modular cable the Origin doesn't. If budget is tight or you want a touch more low-end body, the Que UTG is the value play.

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Glass diaphragm delivers detail and air *without* fatiguing brightness
- ✅ Exceptional soundstage and imaging for sub-$100
- ✅ **Modular 3.5mm + 4.4mm cable included** — rare at this price
- ✅ Real green sandalwood faceplate + leather case feel like a $150 product
- ✅ Tight, muscular bass that doesn't bleed into the mids

**Cons**
- ❌ Slightly warm lean won't satisfy strict-neutral purists (Hexa/Origin)
- ❌ Not a bass-head V-shape — sub-bass is present but controlled
- ❌ A touch less sensitive than the easiest budget IEMs; benefits from a dongle

**Buy if:** you want resolution, air, and a wide stage at $89, you value a real-wood build and a balanced-ready cable, and you've been burned by harsh "detailed" budget IEMs before.

**Skip if:** you want textbook Harman neutrality (Hexa), a reference neutral single DD (Origin), or a bass-cannon V-shape.

The Que UTG is the rare sub-$100 IEM that bets on a genuinely different driver and *lands it*. For detail-and-stage listeners who don't want to pay for fatigue, it's one of the most distinctive picks in the price tier.

## Frequently asked questions

**What driver does the Sivga Que UTG use?**

**Is the Sivga Que UTG bright or fatiguing?**
No. Despite being detail-focused, it's tuned **balanced with a slight warm lean**, and the glass driver's natural treble roll-off keeps it articulate without etched brightness. It's one of the few "detailed" sub-$100 IEMs that stays comfortable over long sessions.
