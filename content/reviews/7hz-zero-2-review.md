The original 7Hz Salnotes Zero launched in 2023 and became the default $25 IEM recommendation for three years running. The **Zero:2** doesn't try to reinvent that formula — it refines it. A new 10mm PU+CNT composite diaphragm, tightened tuning closer to the Harman target, and the same lightweight resin shell. On paper: everything the original did right, sharper.

Short version: **the Zero:2 is the neutral-reference benchmark under $30 in 2026**. If you want honest, balanced sound that doesn't flatter or punish any genre, nothing else at this price touches it. Read on for who should buy it and who should look elsewhere.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver** | Single 10mm dynamic, **PU+CNT (carbon nanotube) composite diaphragm** |
| **Impedance** | 32 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 108 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 20 Hz – 20 kHz |
| **Cable** | Detachable 0.78mm 2-pin, 3.5mm SE termination |
| **Shell** | Plastic resin, lightweight universal fit |
| **Accessories** | 3 sets silicone tips, carrying pouch |
| **Tuning** | Neutral-reference, Harman-derived with slight mid-bass emphasis |
| **MSRP** | $24.99 |

The CNT (carbon nanotube) composite diaphragm is the headline upgrade over the original. The same driver family powers the Truthear Zero:Red — a more expensive, bass-heavy sibling. In the Zero:2, 7Hz tuned the driver for neutral-reference output rather than bass emphasis, and the result is a more technically capable baseline than the original polymer-diaphragm Zero.

## Who it's for

- **First audiophile IEM** — the zero-regret starting point in 2026
- **Neutral-preference listener** who finds most budget IEMs too bassy or too bright
- **Vocal and acoustic music fans** who want voices to sound natural, not hyped
- **Long-session listener** — easy, non-fatiguing tuning for hours of listening
- **Upgrader from earbuds** who wants "what does music actually sound like" before chasing flavour

Skip it if:
- You want bass emphasis or sub-bass slam (Truthear Zero:Red at $55 is the tool)
- You want the absolute sharpest treble detail (Simgot EW200 at $40 is the step up)
- Build quality is a priority — the plastic shell is fine but it's plastic

## Build, fit, accessories

The Zero:2's **plastic resin shell is featherlight** — wear them for an hour and you stop noticing they're there. The universal shape fits most ear geometries without pressure points. It's the same reliable form factor as the original Zero, which millions of listeners have confirmed works.

The detachable **0.78mm 2-pin cable** is standard across the budget tier — any aftermarket cable fits. The stock cable is a basic 4-strand twist, functional without being notable. If you ever want to upgrade to a balanced 4.4mm connection, the investment goes into the cable, not a new IEM.

Accessories are honest: 3 sets of silicone tips and a fabric pouch. No hard case, no wide-bore tip variants. Budget another $8 for Spinfit CP145 tips if you want to optimise the seal — the difference in bass and isolation is real.

The metal nozzle is worth calling out. Plastic nozzles degrade; metal nozzles don't. At $25 this is a detail that pays off over years of use.

## Sound

The Zero:2's tuning is **neutral-reference with a slight mid-bass lift** — very close to the Harman target but tuned to avoid the thin or analytical character that strict Harman adherents sometimes complain about. The CNT diaphragm brings more speed and control than the original's polymer driver, and you can hear it in cleaner transients and tighter bass.

### Bass

**Clean sub-bass with mild mid-bass warmth** — not a bass IEM, but not bloodless either. Kick drums have weight; bass guitars have body. The mid-bass emphasis is conservative enough that it never muddies the mids or clouds the lower registers.

What you won't get: the slam and rumble of a dedicated bass IEM. The Truthear Zero:Red has more sub-bass quantity by a wide margin. The Zero:2 is about accuracy, not emphasis.

### Mids

**One of the best midrange presentations under $30.** Vocals — male and female — sit naturally in the mix: present, clear, and uncoloured. The slight mid-bass warmth gives male vocals chest resonance without sounding thick. Female vocals are forward and textured without becoming thin or shrill.

Acoustic instruments translate well: guitar strings have overtones, piano notes have weight, strings have body. The midrange is where the Zero:2 earns its neutral-reference reputation — it doesn't flatter or subtract.

### Treble

**Well-extended without harshness.** This is not a small achievement at $25. Early budget IEMs in this price tier routinely introduced an 8 kHz peak that made "s" and "sh" consonants in vocals wince-inducing. The Zero:2 avoids that entirely. Cymbals have definition and shimmer; hi-hats have texture; the top end is present without demanding attention.

The trade-off: treble-heads who want sparkle and air above 10 kHz will find the Zero:2 polite. It's not dark — the extension is real — but it's not the forward, glittery treble of the Simgot EW200.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Better than you expect at this price.** The soundstage isn't wide — budget single-DD IEMs don't do concert-hall staging — but imaging is precise enough to place instruments meaningfully. Kick and bass sit centred, vocals sit slightly forward of centre, guitars and synths locate to the sides. More directional resolution than the Moondrop Chu 2 or original Salnotes Zero.

### Technical performance

The CNT diaphragm's benefit shows up here. Transient response is faster than the original Zero — sharp drum hits resolve cleanly, complex mixes stay organised. Detail retrieval is class-leading at $25: you hear the textural difference between a plucked and picked guitar string, vocal breath between phrases, layered synth elements.

It's not a technical champion against $50+ IEMs. The Simgot EW200's DLC diaphragm and the Truthear Hexa's tribrid configuration are meaningfully better. But at $25, nothing touches it.

## Source — your phone is fine

At 32 Ω and 108 dB sensitivity, the Zero:2 is **efficient enough for any source** — phone headphone jack, USB-C dongle, dedicated DAP. You'll set the volume slightly higher than you would on the Chu 2 (which is 120 dB), but no power requirement issues.

The Zero:2 doesn't scale meaningfully with source upgrades. A $300 DAP will not transform it. Buy the IEM; spend any remaining budget on better tips or a cable, not a source upgrade at this price point.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Chu 2 ($20)

The Chu 2 is $5 cheaper and has a premium zinc alloy shell. The Zero:2 has a detachable cable and the CNT-driver refinement. **Sound comparison**: Chu 2 is brighter and more vocal-forward via VDSF tuning; Zero:2 is more tonally balanced and more musical. Both are top-tier sub-$30 picks — Chu 2 for treble-heads and vocal focus, Zero:2 for neutral balance and cable longevity. The Chu 2's fixed cable is the dealbreaker for some. ### vs. Simgot EW200 ($40)

The EW200 at $40 is the technical upgrade. DLC diaphragm, better detail retrieval, wider soundstage, more treble extension. The Zero:2 is warmer and smoother — easier to listen to long-term, less demanding about source. **Spend the $40 if you can; stay at $25 if budget is the constraint**. The EW200's treble energy fatigues some listeners where the Zero:2 doesn't. [Read our EW200 review]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-ew200-review" >}}).

### vs. Truthear Zero:Red ($55)

These are different tools. The Zero:Red is a bass specialist — elevated sub-bass, more slam, more weight. The Zero:2 is neutral-reference. **Different tuning philosophies for different listeners**: EDM and hip-hop listeners who want bass elevation should go Zero:Red; everyone else who wants accuracy should go Zero:2. The $30 price gap is real and the Zero:2 is the better buy for most use cases. [Read our Zero:Red review]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-zero-red-review" >}}).
