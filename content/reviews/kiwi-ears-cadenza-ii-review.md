The Kiwi Ears Cadenza II is the kind of IEM that makes the sub-$50 bracket genuinely hard to shop. At **$49.99** it carries a single **10mm titanium dynamic driver** running Kiwi Ears' revised **KARS 2.0** (Kiwi Acoustic Resonance System) tuning — a proprietary internal acoustic chamber the company says is engineered for tighter sub-bass and lower distortion in budget models. On paper it's an unremarkable spec sheet. In practice, reviewers keep describing it the same way: mature, restrained, and a clear step up from the original Cadenza.

That word — *restraint* — is the through-line. Resonance Reviews flatly called it "the mature man's budget IEM," and the framing fits. Where a lot of cheap IEMs chase attention with a scooped V-shape or fake-detail treble spikes, the Cadenza II trades the warm, lush vocals of the first Cadenza for a clearer, more transparent midrange and a mild U-shaped tuning that doesn't fatigue. The question this review answers: is a $50 single-DD still the *smart* first buy in 2026, when the Truthear Gate costs $22 and the Moondrop Chu 2 costs even less?

## TL;DR

- **Single 10mm titanium-coated dynamic driver with KARS 2.0; $49.99.**
- **Mild U-shaped, warm-leaning tuning** — deep sub-bass, neutral-to-lush mids, smooth-but-airy treble. Easy to live with.
- **Midrange is the highlight**: male vocals have weight, female vocals stay clear without going shouty.
- **Treble offers real resolution without artificial sharpness** — no fake-detail sibilance.
- **Big soundstage and good imaging** for the price, per Headfonics.
- **Caveat**: it's a touch harder to drive than rivals, so it rewards a half-decent source/pairing.
- **Best for**: a first "proper" IEM where you want a clean, non-fatiguing, all-rounder over a hype-tuned cheapie.

## Sound signature

Overall, the Cadenza II lands as a **clean, mild U-shape** — bass and upper treble are both present and well balanced against each other, with a midrange that stays honest rather than hollowed out. It's engaging without being lively to the point of fatigue.

### Bass

The sub-bass is **deep and visceral**, with rumble that feels real and a reasonably controlled decay. Notably, Kiwi Ears pulled *back* on mid-bass versus the original Cadenza, so the low end is tight and punchy with average impact for the price rather than bloated. The result is weight where you want it and restraint where bloat usually creeps in.

### Mids

This is where reviewers agree the Cadenza II shines. The midrange is **neutral and accurate in note weight** — dense and lush yet clear. Male vocals carry enough body, female vocals stay clear without turning shouty, and there's a touch of warmth in the lower mids that blends into a forward (but not artificial) upper midrange. The upper mids are well-calibrated to give instruments presence without that harsh push.

### Treble

The lower treble is **clean and smooth**; the upper treble is **airy, extended, and accurate**. Crucially, it's "a smooth treble that still offers high resolution" — it avoids the artificial sharpness budget IEMs often use to fake detail, while still rendering good tonal contrast against the lower registers. Slightly elevated, never sibilant.

### Soundstage

Headfonics highlights a **big soundstage with good imaging** — generous for an entry-level single-DD, and a real part of why the listen feels transparent and "open" rather than congested.

## Build, fit & accessories

The Cadenza II carries Kiwi Ears' usual budget-tier presentation: a CNC aluminum faceplate on a compact, easy-to-wear shell. Reviewers consistently flag the **beautiful design at an entry-level price point** as part of the appeal. Fit is comfortable for typical ears, and the package leans on Kiwi Ears' broader ecosystem — a 1-year warranty, free worldwide shipping, and an online equalizer platform if you want to tweak the tuning yourself. The one practical asterisk on sound: the Cadenza II is a little **harder to drive** than some rivals, so finding a good pairing pays off.

## How it compares

### vs. Truthear Gate ($22)

The [Truthear Gate review]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-gate-review" >}}) covers the other end of the value spectrum — a $22 single-DD whose headline feature is a **detachable 2-pin cable** (and a mic option). The Gate is more than twice as cheap, future-proofs you on cabling, and is easier to drive. The Cadenza II answers with a **more refined, more transparent midrange**, deeper and more controlled sub-bass, and a bigger soundstage. If budget is the only constraint, the Gate is the smarter spend; if you want the more grown-up *sound* and don't mind paying for it, the Cadenza II is the upgrade.

### vs. Moondrop Chu 2 (~$20)

The [Moondrop Chu 2 review]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-chu-2-review" >}}) is the other budget darling — the consensus "first IEM," with a near-Harman, reference-oriented tuning. Against it, the Cadenza II has **better bass dynamics and more low-end quantity**, plus more warmth and musicality, making it the more emotionally involving listen for general music. The Chu 2 is cleaner and more clinical in places (and a fraction of the price), so it's the pick for a strict reference sound on the tightest budget. The Cadenza II is for the listener who's decided they want *engaging* over *clinical*.

## Who should buy

- **First-time IEM buyers** who want a clean, non-fatiguing all-rounder rather than a hype-tuned cheapie.
- **Listeners who prioritize vocals and mids** — male and female alike — without sibilance.
- **Anyone who values a smooth, mature U-shape** over an aggressive V-shape.
- **Buyers who appreciate a polished design** at the entry tier.

## Who should skip

- **Pure budget maximizers** — the Chu 2 and Truthear Gate deliver most of the way there for less than half the price.
- **Listeners who want only the cheapest source-free experience** — the Cadenza II is a touch harder to drive and rewards a decent pairing.
- **Bassheads** — sub-bass is deep, but mid-bass impact is deliberately restrained to "average for the price."
- **Anyone who needs a detachable cable as a must-have** — the Truthear Gate addresses that directly at $22.

## Verdict

The Cadenza II is a genuinely *smart* first budget IEM — not because it's the cheapest, but because it's the most grown-up sound in the bracket. Its philosophy is restraint: deep, controlled bass; a transparent, lush-yet-clear midrange; and a smooth treble that resolves real detail without faking it, all wrapped in a big soundstage. It's a clear sonic upgrade over the original Cadenza and an easy recommendation for anyone whose budget stretches to $50 and who wants something they won't grow tired of. The only reasons to look elsewhere are price (the Gate and Chu 2 are remarkable for the money) or a hard requirement like a detachable cable. **Rated 4.5/5.**
