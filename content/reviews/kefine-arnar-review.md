Most budget planars hand you one tuning and dare you to like it. The **Kefine Arnar** ($189) takes the opposite bet: a **14.5mm planar magnetic driver paired with a single Knowles balanced armature**, then three swappable steel nozzles that meaningfully move the sound from warm to balanced to bright. It's a planar hybrid that ships as three IEMs in one box, wrapped in an aviation-grade aluminum shell that looks like it costs more than $189.

Short version: **it lands.** The planar handles the heavy lifting — fast transients, a wide stage — while the Knowles BA adds top-end articulation that pure budget planars often smear. The nozzle system is the difference between "good tuning" and "the tuning *you* want."

The interesting part is the **driver pairing**. A 14.5mm planar is a large diaphragm for an IEM — the same size class as the [HIDIZS MP145 Pro]({{< ref "/reviews/hidizs-mp145-pro-review" >}}) — and it's the source of the Arnar's speed and stage. The Knowles BA isn't there for bass or mids; it exists to extend and articulate the treble, which is exactly where sub-$200 planars usually run out of resolution.

## Who it's for

- **You want one IEM that adapts** — the three nozzles cover warm, balanced, and bright without buying anything extra
- **You like planar speed and stage** but find pure-planar treble either dull or metallic
- **You use both a phone and a balanced source** — the modular 3.5/4.4 cable means you don't choose
- **You want a grown-up metal build** instead of glittery resin

Skip it if:
- You want the absolute cleanest pure-planar tone at this price ([Letshuoer S12 Pro]({{< ref "/reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) or [7Hz Timeless AE]({{< ref "/reviews/7hz-timeless-ae-review" >}}) are the pure-planar reference picks)
- You never want to swap nozzles and just want one fixed, dialed tuning out of the box

## Build, fit, accessories

The Arnar reads as more than $189 the moment you hold it. The shells are **five-axis CNC aluminum** — a real metal build, not painted resin — with a "Wish Window" faceplate patterned after traditional Chinese lattice windows. It's understated and adult in a category full of loud resin swirls. Fit is comfortable and medium-sized; the aluminum is dense but the shells aren't oversized, so most ears seal quickly and stay comfortable over long sessions.

The bundle is generous:

- **Modular cable with both 3.5mm single-ended and 4.4mm balanced plugs** — silver-plated OFC, 4-strand braid, 0.78mm 2-pin. Balanced output for a DAP or dongle *and* a phone-friendly SE plug, no aftermarket cable required.
- **Three pairs of swappable steel nozzles** (this is the headline feature — more below)
- Hard carrying case, 12 pairs of ear tips, two tip storage cases, manual and nozzle guide

That's a $150-tier accessory set on a $189 IEM.

## The nozzle system — three IEMs in one box

This is the Arnar's defining trait, so it gets its own section. The three stainless steel nozzles aren't a gimmick; each one shifts the response enough to change which music the IEM is best at.

- **Silver — balanced / natural.** Neutral bass, even upper-mids, smooth highs. The all-rounder and the one most people should start on.
- **Gold — clarity / transparency.** Emphasized upper-mids and the brightest, most detailed presentation. Best midrange definition of the three, with expressive vocals and tactile transients — but the treble gets noticeably brighter and some vocals can turn slightly shouty. Great for acoustic and detail listening, less ideal for already-bright recordings.
- **Black — warm / rich.** Thicker, more potent bass with a warmer low end and the smoothest treble. This is the "sit back and enjoy" nozzle, and the pick for bass-forward genres.

The practical value: you can tune the Arnar to the music instead of tuning your library to the IEM. That flexibility is rare under $200, and it's the strongest single reason to buy this over a fixed-tuning rival.

## Sound

Across all three nozzles, the Arnar's core character is **speed and space** — the planar signature — with the Knowles BA keeping the top end articulate rather than smeared.

### Bass

The planar delivers **fast, controlled bass** with good texture. On the black nozzle the low end thickens noticeably, with a more potent resonance that suits hip-hop and electronic; on silver and gold it's cleaner and more neutral. It's tight rather than thunderous — planar bass trades some slam for speed — but it extends well and never bleeds into the mids.

### Mids

Midrange is **clean and well-defined**, and it's where the nozzles matter most. Gold pushes the upper-mids forward for the most vocal presence and precision; silver keeps them even and natural; black pulls them back slightly for a warmer balance. Vocals sit forward enough to feel lifelike, and the BA-assisted upper range gives instruments tactile edge without the timbre debate that pure-BA sets invite.

### Treble

The Knowles BA is doing its job here. Treble is **detailed and extended** with real air — better articulated than most pure-planar sets under $200. The honest caveat: with the **gold nozzle it can get bright and even blaring**, and a few voices tip into shouty. Silver and black smooth it out considerably. This is the one area to be deliberate about nozzle choice, especially if you're treble-sensitive.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Soundstage is the Arnar's standout** — wide, immersive, with excellent instrument separation and a "studio-like" spatial precision that doesn't collapse into your head. It compares favorably to established budget planars like the Letshuoer S12, which is high praise at this price. Imaging is precise enough to place subtle cues intuitively.

## Source — it's a planar, so feed it current

At **13 Ω and 107 dB/Vrms**, the Arnar is a typical planar load: low impedance, current-hungry. A phone jack drives it to volume but sounds flat and slightly soft. A proper dongle DAC (FiiO KA series, Moondrop Dawn) or a DAP's **4.4mm balanced output** tightens the bass, widens the stage, and brings the treble to life — and the bundled 4.4mm plug is there precisely so you can use it. Planars scale with clean power more than efficient dynamic-driver IEMs do; the Arnar is no exception. (New to source pairing? ## Compared to

### vs. Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)

The [S12 Pro]({{< ref "/reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) is the pure-planar value benchmark: a fun, forgiving V-shape that sounds good off almost anything. The Arnar costs ~$30 more and answers a different question — **tuning flexibility and BA-assisted treble** instead of one fixed voicing. The S12 Pro is the easier out-of-box listen and the more accessible source-wise; the Arnar wins on treble articulation, build (metal vs. mostly-metal), and the fact that it can *become* three different IEMs. If you want one dialed planar and nothing to fiddle with, S12 Pro. If you want to tune to the music, Arnar.

### vs. Kiwi Ears Aether ($150)

The [Aether]({{< ref "/reviews/kiwi-ears-aether-review" >}}) is a single large planar in a light resin shell — smooth, airy, and famously comfortable for marathon sessions. The Arnar is denser (aluminum), more tunable, and its BA gives it a crisper top end. The Aether is the smoother, lighter, more laid-back pick; the Arnar is the more flexible and more articulate one. Comfort-first and warm → Aether; flexibility and treble detail → Arnar.

### vs. HIDIZS MP145 Pro ($169)

The [MP145 Pro]({{< ref "/reviews/hidizs-mp145-pro-review" >}}) uses a similarly large 14.5mm planar but is a **pure planar** — bigger, more forceful bass and a bold presentation. The Arnar is the more refined and adjustable of the two, with the BA adding top-end finesse the MP145 Pro doesn't have. Big, bold, single-driver planar → MP145 Pro; refined, tunable planar hybrid → Arnar.

- ✅ Knowles BA adds treble articulation most budget planars lack

**Cons**

**Buy if:** you want one IEM that adapts to your music, you like planar speed and stage, and you value a metal build with a balanced-ready cable at $189.

**Skip if:** you want the cleanest fixed pure-planar tone (S12 Pro, Timeless AE), or a big bass-forward V-shape.

The Arnar is one of the strongest sub-$200 planar hybrids right now precisely because it refuses to pick one tuning for you. Match the nozzle to the music and it competes with pure planars costing more — while doing something none of them can.

## Frequently asked questions

**What drivers does the Kefine Arnar use?**
A **14.5mm planar magnetic driver plus a single Knowles balanced armature** per side — a planar hybrid. The large planar handles bass through mids and provides the speed and soundstage; the Knowles BA extends and articulates the treble, which is where budget pure-planars usually run short.

**What do the three Kefine Arnar nozzles do?**
They change the tuning. **Silver** is balanced and natural (the all-rounder), **gold** emphasizes upper-mids and treble for the clearest, most detailed sound (but the brightest), and **black** warms the low end and smooths the treble for a richer, bass-forward signature. They swap in seconds via a steel thread.

**Is the Kefine Arnar bright?**
It depends on the nozzle. On the **gold nozzle the treble can get bright and occasionally shouty**; the **silver and black nozzles smooth it out** considerably. Treble-sensitive listeners should run silver or black. This tunability is the whole point of the three-nozzle system.

**Does the Kefine Arnar need an amp?**
It benefits from one. At 13 Ω and 107 dB/Vrms it's a current-hungry planar — a phone jack reaches volume but sounds flat. A dongle DAC or a **4.4mm balanced output** (the Arnar ships with a modular 4.4mm plug) tightens the bass and widens the stage. Budget for at least a decent dongle.

**Kefine Arnar vs Letshuoer S12 Pro — which should I buy?**
The [S12 Pro]({{< ref "/reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) is a fixed, fun V-shaped pure planar that's easy to drive and easy to love. The Arnar costs ~$30 more and offers **three tunings plus BA-assisted treble**. Want one dialed planar with no fuss → S12 Pro. Want to tune to the music and get crisper highs → Arnar.
