Some IEMs define a generation. The Moondrop Blessing 2: Dusk — a collaboration between Moondrop and reviewer/ranker crinacle — did exactly that. **From 2022 through 2024, the Dusk held "best IEM under $200" status in more consensus lists than any other single product in its tier.** It wasn't unanimous, but it was close.

The Blessing 3 has since arrived, and the $300 tier has matured. But in 2026, the Dusk still shows up used at $130–150, and the question is whether it still earns a recommendation. Short answer: **yes — but you need to understand what it is and what it isn't**.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Value |
|------|-------|
| **Driver config** | 1 DD (bass) + 4 BA (mids + highs) |
| **Impedance** | 22 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 117 dB/Vrms @ 1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 5 Hz – 35 kHz |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin, 3.5mm SE |
| **Shell** | Resin body, metal faceplate, semi-custom universal shape |
| **MSRP** | $159 (now commonly $130–150 used) |

The hardware is identical to the standard Blessing 2 — same shell, same driver array. The Dusk is a **retune**: crinacle applied a Diffuse Field (DF) target to the crossover, pulling the bass down, flattening the mids, and extending the treble further than Moondrop's stock tuning. Same chassis, different philosophy.

## Who it's for

- **Reference listeners** who want to hear recordings as they were captured, not as a tuning curve wants you to hear them
- **Treble enjoyers** — the Dusk's top-end is among the best in its price tier, full stop
- **Used-market shoppers** looking for a technically competitive IEM at $130–150
- **Anyone comparing DF-tuned vs Harman-tuned** IEMs — the Dusk is the cleanest single comparison point

Avoid it if you come from V-shaped IEMs, want elevated bass, or are treble-sensitive. The Dusk is deliberately bright. That's not a flaw — it's the point.

## Build, fit, accessories

The semi-custom universal shell is one of the Dusk's underrated selling points. The resin body is contoured for the concha in a way that most universals aren't — **fit is noticeably better than box-shaped plastic IEMs at the same price**. For most ear geometries, the Dusk disappears after a few minutes. Seal is good, passive isolation is above average.

The metal faceplate (smooth, unbranded on the Dusk version) feels premium for the price. The resin body is translucent with subtle colour variation — it looks more expensive than it costs.

The included cable is the Dusk's weakest point. The stock 0.78mm 2-pin 3.5mm SE cable is functional but nothing more — tangles easily, and the Y-split quality is basic. It's the expected compromise at $159. A cable upgrade (something braided, even $15–20) makes a meaningful ergonomic difference. The 0.78mm 2-pin connector is universal and inexpensive to replace.

Tip selection matters. The nozzle is wide. Moondrop's included wide-bore tips work, but Spinfit CP100+ or Final Type E can sharpen the stage and control brightness slightly.

## Sound

The Dusk targets a **Diffuse Field (DF) curve** — not Harman. Understanding this distinction is essential before you listen. Harman targets a mild bass shelf (+4–6dB sub-bass), forward upper mids, and controlled treble. DF is flatter: neutral bass from top to bottom, slightly recessed pinna compensation, and longer treble extension. If you've been listening to Harman-tuned IEMs (Nova, Aria 2, most Moondrop products), the Dusk will initially sound bass-light and bright. That's the tuning doing its job.

### Bass

The single dynamic driver handles everything below ~800Hz. Bass quantity is **neutral-flat by design** — the DF target doesn't add the Harman shelf. Sub-bass extension is solid: 30–40Hz content has presence and body, not absence. There's no mid-bass bloom, no warmth bleeding into the lower midrange. Kick drums are tight. Bass guitar has note definition.

For listeners used to elevated bass: the Dusk will sound lean. For listeners used to BA-only IEMs: the DD sub-bass extension may actually feel more realistic. For DF-preference listeners: this is correct.

### Mids

Forward, clear, and slightly bright in the upper registers. **Vocals are present and prioritised** — the flat lower midrange means no muddiness under singers, and the forward upper mids put voices front-and-centre. Male vocals are clear without being thin. Female vocals are detailed and present, though some tracks push them toward sharp on bright recordings. Instruments separate cleanly; guitar harmonics, piano upper registers, and strings have texture without blur.

The midrange is the Dusk's most divisive region. For neutral-preference listeners, it sounds accurate and honest. For Harman listeners, the extra upper-mid energy reads as a mild brightness. Both descriptions are correct.

### Treble

**The Dusk's defining characteristic, and its clearest competitive advantage at this price.** Treble is extended, airy, and controlled in a way that most sub-$200 IEMs don't manage. Cymbals have real shimmer and decay — not a compressed spike, but extension through the tail of the transient. Hi-hats are textured. String overtones resolve clearly.

The Dusk doesn't harshness out on well-recorded material. The elevation is real but it's balanced armature-clean — detail without grain. On badly mastered or brick-walled recordings, the treble will expose the mastering. That's the cost of honesty.

This is the IEM to buy if treble quality is your benchmark.

### Soundstage and imaging

Wide and deep for the price, and **the shell geometry contributes directly to this**. The semi-custom fit creates a consistent, deep seal — and seal quality has a measurable effect on perceived soundstage. The Dusk sounds larger than most universals at this price because it fits more consistently.

Imaging is precise: instruments land at specific points in the field, not smeared across a region. Layering is distinct — you can track individual elements in dense passages. The Dusk doesn't quite reach the Blessing 3's surgical three-dimensional imaging, but at half the price, it's the strongest imaging performance in the sub-$200 tier.

### Technicalities

This is why the Dusk held "best under $200" for years. **The technical ceiling is genuine**. Detail retrieval is high — low-level information in the mix (room ambience, micro-dynamics, the texture of acoustic instruments) is resolved rather than smoothed over. Transient response is fast and clean. Nothing sounds processed or artificially enhanced; the Dusk's version of detail is transparency, not hyper-etching.

## Source pairing

At 22Ω / 117dB, the Dusk is easy to drive — any phone or DAP will reach listening volume. But it is not entirely source-indifferent. A cleaner DAC (even a $30 USB dongle like the Moondrop Dawn or JCALLY JM20) audibly reduces any grain in the upper treble. The Dusk will be honest about your source's weaknesses, partly because its own frequency response exposes them.

For portable use, any clean 3.5mm output works. For desktop or critical listening, a neutral DAC/amp chain is preferable to a warm-coloured one — the Dusk's tuning is already DF; adding warmth at the source moves it toward neutral-warm, which may actually suit more listeners who find pure DF too lean.

## Compared to

### vs. Moondrop Blessing 3 ($319)

The Blessing 3 uses the same driver count and shell lineage but targets Moondrop VDSF (Harman-derived) and has a newer, improved driver implementation. **The Blessing 3 is warmer, more dynamic-sounding, and technically superior** — better bass texture, tighter driver coherence, better cable. The Dusk is brighter, more analytical, and half the price (new) or a third the price (used). If you prefer DF tuning, the Dusk remains the better fit regardless of budget. If you prefer Harman and want the full picture, the Blessing 3 is the upgrade path. See the [full Blessing 3 review]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-blessing-3-review" >}}).

### vs. Truthear Nova ($149)

The closest market comparison. Both are competitive sub-$200 hybrids with strong technical fundamentals. **The Nova is Harman-tuned: warmer mids, more bass, easier treble.** The Dusk is DF-tuned: flatter bass, brighter mids, more top-end extension. Neither is technically superior — they're different tuning philosophies at similar price points. Pick by target preference. If you don't know which you prefer, the Nova is the safer first choice; the Dusk rewards you if you already know you like DF. See the [Truthear Nova review]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}).

### vs. Thieaudio Oracle MKII ($299)

The Oracle MKII is warmer and more musical — a more forgiving listen with a larger, airier presentation. It costs twice the Dusk's current used price. **The Dusk wins on dollar-for-dollar technical performance at its used price** — the Oracle MKII's advantages are real, but whether they justify the price gap depends on how much the warmer tuning appeals. See the [ThieAudio Oracle MKII vs Blessing 3 comparison]({{< ref "vs/thieaudio-oracle-mkii-vs-moondrop-blessing-3" >}}).

## Verdict

**Pros**
- ✅ Best treble presentation in the sub-$200 tier
- ✅ Excellent fit — semi-custom ergonomic shell
- ✅ Wide, deep soundstage for the price
- ✅ Genuinely transparent and technically honest
- ✅ Outstanding used-market value at $130–150

**Cons**
- ❌ DF tuning reads as "bass-light" to Harman listeners — not for everyone
- ❌ Stock cable is mediocre
- ❌ Treble can be demanding on poorly mastered recordings
- ❌ 3.5mm SE only (no balanced cable option in box)

**Buy if:** you want the reference DF tuning experience, you prioritise treble quality and staging, or you're hunting the used market for a technically competitive IEM under $150.

**Skip if:** you want elevated bass, you're treble-sensitive, or you prefer Harman tuning — the Nova is the better fit for that preference at the same price.

The Dusk is a classic for a reason. It defined what analytical tuning could sound like in the sub-$200 tier, and a generation of listeners learned what DF sounded like because of it. **The Blessing 3 has moved the benchmark, but the Dusk hasn't been made obsolete — it's just become the best argument for buying used.**
