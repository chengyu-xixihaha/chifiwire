DUNU has been making in-ear monitors for 22 years, and the **Falcon Ultra** feels like the kind of product that only comes out of that much accumulated experience — a single dynamic driver IEM that doesn't chase a gimmick, just nails the fundamentals. It pairs a 10.7mm second-generation **ECLIPSE-architecture dynamic driver** (DUNU markets this "Meteorite Titanium" version's diaphragm accordingly; the underlying dome is a lithium-magnesium alloy with an external "Tesla-level" ring magnet assembly) with a glossy stainless-steel shell in a striking deep **Klein Blue** finish. At **$239** for the standard edition — with the newer Meteorite Titanium variant landing closer to **$219** — it slots squarely into the most competitive bracket in ChiFi.

What's made the Falcon Ultra one of the most recommended single-DDs of the year isn't a single headline feature — it's the *whole experience*. The package is genuinely absurd for the price, the tuning is warm and inoffensive, and there are two swappable nozzles to dial the top end. AudioNotions went so far as to call it "probably the best beginner-friendly IEM in the market today," and "the best IEM to get right now" if you want something safe-sounding, well-built, and impeccably accessorized. That's a big claim. Let's see if it holds.

## TL;DR

- **Single 10.7mm dynamic driver** with DUNU's 2nd-gen ECLIPSE architecture and ring-type magnet assembly — no hybrid complexity, no crossover compromises.
- **Warm, bassy, detailed** tonality with a lush midrange — Headfonics called the mids the standout, with a "firm grasp on timbre and natural note weight."
- **Two tuning nozzles** (stainless steel = brighter; gold = warmer/balanced) plus a modular cable with 3.5mm and 4.4mm plugs.
- **One of the best-value packages in IEM history** — four+ tip sets, premium case, modular DUW-02 Pro cable, cleaning kit, adapters.
- **Easy to drive** at 16Ω / 108dB, but scales nicely with a decent dongle DAC.
- **The catch:** if you want a huge sub-bass shelf or a holographic stage, this isn't a basshead cannon — it's an all-rounder.

## Sound signature

The headline from every outlet is consistency. Headfonics summed it up as "great all-around" — warm and full, but resolving. The two nozzles let you nudge the balance: the **stainless-steel nozzle** is brighter with extra energy in the upper mids and treble, while the **gold nozzle** is the safer, warmer tuning with a touch less top-end bite.

### Bass

The Falcon Ultra leans warm without becoming a bass cannon. Headfonics described "just enough oomph to add a cozy warmth" with an "agile" response, noting only that sub-bass texture depth is a touch limited. The Headphone List was more enthusiastic, praising a "wholesome yet well controlled lower end" with "very good dynamics and control," strong sub-bass rumble, and a "slightly fuller" mid-bass with a "very good area of impact." If you want the bass louder, the warmer gold nozzle pushes it forward — but this is fundamentally a tasteful, controlled low end, not a basshead's first choice.

### Mids

This is where the Falcon Ultra earns its reputation. Headfonics called the midrange the strongest aspect — a "firm grasp on timbre and natural note weight" with "articulate" vocals that lack the "sharp edges" of older DUNU models. The Headphone List went further: "simply lovely for the details seekers," with "superb note height," "excellent transparency and energy," male vocals that avoid throatiness, and female vocals that come across as "superbly accurate." Whichever nozzle you pick, vocals sit forward and sound natural.

### Treble

The top end is detailed without being mean. Headfonics noted "good strike" and "sparkle" — "delicate, stylish, and crisp" — while keeping a "richness" that avoids brittleness. The Headphone List rated it "one of the most detailed IEMs one can buy under $300" with "excellent accuracy with body," but flagged the one caveat: there's "extra bit of height and energy," so listeners who prefer a smoother, darker treble may find the stainless nozzle a little hot. That's exactly what the gold nozzle is for.

### Soundstage & imaging

Here the two outlets diverge. Headfonics found the stage "rather ordinary, neither expansive nor holographic," though with strong instrument separation. The Headphone List heard it more generously — "much bigger expansion in every direction," "one of the best X-axis width" in class, with notable Z-axis depth and imaging delivering "superb accuracy and precision." The truth is probably in between: separation and imaging precision are clearly strengths; raw stage width is good-to-very-good depending on your ears and tips, not a defining feature.

## Build, package & comfort

This is the Falcon Ultra's not-so-secret weapon. The shell is polished stainless steel in that glossy Klein Blue, and it looks far more expensive than $239 — though it is on the heavier side. Comfort is largely excellent: Prime Audio called the fit "extremely comfortable and secure" with good isolation, while The Headphone List did note some "stability inside the ear" challenges across multiple tips. Bring your own favorite tips if you have picky ears.

The accessories are the talking point. The Headphone List and AudioNotions both describe one of the **best-value packages ever bundled with an IEM** — reviewers were "stunned and overwhelmed" by the contents. You get:

- The **DUW-02 Pro** 4-core silver-plated copper Litz cable with a **modular plug system** (3.5mm single-ended and 4.4mm balanced in the box), described as supple and low on microphonics.
- **Multiple ear tip sets** across several types and sizes (including SpinFit-style and DUNU's own white/reversed/blue-core tips).
- Both **tuning nozzles** (steel + gold).
- A **premium zippered case** plus a separate tips pouch and a fabric cover.
- **Cleaning brush, cloth**, and a **6.35mm (6.5mm) adapter**.

For a beginner, opening this box is a genuine "this is what audiophile gear feels like" moment — and that experience is a real part of the value proposition.

## Driveability

At **16Ω and 108dB**, the Falcon Ultra is easy to drive — it'll get plenty loud off a phone dongle or even a laptop. The Headphone List confirmed it performs adequately straight from mobile phones, but noted it **scales with source quality**, getting "excellent details and coherency" off a cleaner DAC like the Hidizs S8 Pro. You don't *need* a desktop stack, but a $50–100 dongle DAC is a worthwhile pairing.

## How it compares

**vs. the [Simgot EA1000 (Fermat) review]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-ea1000-fermat-review" >}}) ($219):** The EA1000 is the obvious cross-shop — another premium single-DD with swappable nozzles, but Simgot adds a passive radiator for bass extension and ships three nozzles. The Headphone List felt the Falcon Ultra offered "much bigger expansion" and "more even density" in its presentation. Broadly: the EA1000 is the slightly more reference-leaning, technically precise pick; the Falcon Ultra is the warmer, more forgiving listen with the more lavish accessory set.

**vs. the [Moondrop Aria 2 review]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}):** The Aria 2 is the value-king single-DD — Harman-tuned, lighter, and roughly half the price. If you want the cleanest path to a "correct" tuning on a budget, the Aria 2 is hard to beat. The Falcon Ultra justifies its premium with build, the modular cable, the tuning flexibility, and a more refined, richer top end. You're paying for the whole experience, not just the sound.

## Who should buy

- **First-time audiophiles** who want one box that delivers the full experience — great sound, great accessories, no rabbit hole required.
- Listeners who want a **warm, detailed, fatigue-free** all-rounder for long sessions.
- Anyone who values **modular cables and tuning options** without buying them separately.
- People who appreciate a **premium-feeling build** and a striking look.

## Who should skip

- **Bassheads** who want a massive sub-bass shelf — the Falcon Ultra is controlled, not thunderous.
- Treble-sensitive listeners chasing a **dark, smoothed-over** signature (the gold nozzle helps, but there's still some energy up top).
- Anyone who only cares about **raw soundstage width** — separation is great, but the stage isn't holographic.
- Shoppers chasing **absolute value** — the [Moondrop Aria 2 review]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) gets you most of the sound for much less.

## Verdict

The DUNU Falcon Ultra is one of those rare products where the marketing hype and the reviewer consensus actually line up. Headfonics rated it 4.5/5 as a "great all-around" IEM; The Headphone List handed it a "Recommended" badge and called it "the one to get under $300" if you want detail without sharpness; AudioNotions called it possibly the best beginner-friendly IEM on the market. The picture is consistent: a warm, resolving, beautifully built single-DD with a package that punches well above its price.

It's not the basshead's cannon and it's not the absolute cheapest path to good sound — but as a *complete* sub-$300 single dynamic that you can hand to a newcomer or keep as a daily driver for years, it's about as easy a recommendation as ChiFi makes. **Rating: 4.5/5.**
