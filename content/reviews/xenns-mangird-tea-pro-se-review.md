The Xenns (Mangird) Tea line has been a reference-tuned mid-fi favorite for years. The **Tea Pro SE** ($449) is the most refined version yet — a **2 dynamic driver + 6 balanced armature hybrid** with upgraded Knowles tweeters, a re-worked crossover, and one of the cleaner reference tunings in the sub-$500 tier. It's not a reinvention; it's the Tea formula sharpened to a point.

Short version: **it's a technically strong, reference-leaning hybrid that stays musical instead of clinical.** Eight drivers per side deliver wide staging and precise imaging, the reworked bass is tighter than past Teas, and the extended ultra-treble adds real air. At $449 it competes with the best hybrids in its class — the knock is simply that "reference" means it won't flatter you the way a warmer, more colored set will.

## Specs at a glance

| Spec | Xenns Mangird Tea Pro SE |
|---|---|
| **Driver config** | 2 DD + 6 BA hybrid (8 drivers per side) |
| **Notable drivers** | Dual custom dynamic drivers + Knowles BAs + Knowles SWFK 31736 ultra-treble tweeters |
| **Impedance** | 13 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 104 dB SPL |
| **Crossover** | Multi-way, 150 Hz sub/low-mid split |
| **Shell** | Precision metal housing + handcrafted resin faceplate, CNC-machined |
| **QC** | Individually matched driver units |
| **Cable** | 8-core mixed-metal modular, 0.78 mm 2-pin, interchangeable 3.5 mm + 4.4 mm plugs |
| **Cable length** | 1.2 m |
| **Price** | $449 |

At 13 Ω / 104 dB the Tea Pro SE is easy to drive — it plays cleanly from a dongle — but that low impedance means it's **source-picky about hiss**, so a clean output helps. The modular cable shipping with both 3.5 mm and 4.4 mm plugs is the expected courtesy at this price, and here it's a genuinely nice 8-core.

## Sound signature

**Bass.** The dual dynamic drivers handle the low end, and the SE tightens it up versus older Teas — **punchier, more controlled sub-bass** with a sharper 150 Hz crossover that keeps the low mids clean. It's not a basshead tuning; it's weight and texture in service of a reference balance. Physical enough to satisfy, disciplined enough to stay out of the mids.

**Mids.** A gentle rise around 400 Hz adds warmth so the midrange doesn't go sterile, which is the SE's smartest tuning move. Vocals and instruments sit natural and full — reference-neutral with just enough body to stay engaging. This is where the "musical, not clinical" verdict comes from.

**Treble.** The Knowles **SWFK 31736** ultra-treble tweeters are the headline. Core treble is smoothed to avoid fatigue, while the **extended +20 kHz ultra-treble** opens up air and clarity without harshness. It's a detailed, airy top end that resolves micro-detail cleanly — one of the SE's clearest upgrades over the standard Tea Pro.

**Stage & imaging.** Eight drivers and a well-sorted crossover pay off here: **wide soundstage, precise imaging.** Reviewers consistently call the staging and placement standout traits. It images with the confidence you'd expect from a well-executed multi-BA hybrid.

## Build & accessories

The build is flagship-appropriate: a **precision CNC metal housing** with a handcrafted green-and-gold marbled resin faceplate that looks the part, plus **individually matched driver units** for channel consistency. The 8-core mixed-metal modular cable with interchangeable 3.5/4.4 plugs rounds out a package that feels worth $449.

## How it compares

- **Moondrop Blessing 3 (~$320, 1DD+4BA):** the reference-hybrid benchmark below it. The [Blessing 3](/reviews/moondrop-blessing-3-review/) is the value pick for a neutral Harman-ish tuning; the Tea Pro SE adds a second DD, more BAs, better ultra-treble extension, and a more refined stage — you're paying ~$130 more for technicalities and air, not a different philosophy.
- **Thieaudio Oracle MKII (~$589, tribrid):** the step up in driver ambition. The [Oracle MKII](/reviews/thieaudio-oracle-mkii-review/) brings a tribrid config and a slightly more colored presentation; the Tea Pro SE is the more straightforwardly reference option for less money.
- **Kiwi Ears Orchestra II (~$349, all-BA):** the all-BA alternative. The [Orchestra II](/reviews/kiwi-ears-orchestra-ii-review/) trades the Tea's dynamic-driver bass slam for all-BA coherence and class-leading isolation. Choose the Tea Pro SE if you want real DD bass weight; the Orchestra II if you want coherence and isolation.
- **Kiwi Ears Halcyon (~$259, tribrid):** the budget-flagship spoiler. The [Halcyon](/reviews/kiwi-ears-halcyon-review/) gets you near-neutral tuning and MEMS treble for less; the Tea Pro SE is the more complete, better-built package if you have the budget.

## Who should buy it

- Listeners who want a **reference tuning that stays musical**, not analytical
- Anyone chasing **airy, extended treble** and precise imaging from a proven Knowles setup
- Buyers who want **real dynamic-driver bass** in a technical hybrid, not BA-only bass
- People who value **build, QC, and a modular balanced-ready cable** at $449

## Who should skip it

- **Bassheads and warmth-lovers** — the SE is reference-leaning, not colored or thick
- **Budget buyers** — the Blessing 3 delivers ~80% of the philosophy for ~$130 less
- Anyone sensitive to **source hiss** — 13 Ω is revealing of a noisy output
- Listeners who want a single fun, forgiving signature — a warmer set fits better

## Bottom line

The Xenns Mangird Tea Pro SE is a proper step up for the Tea series: tighter bass, cleaner mids, and a genuinely airy Knowles-driven top end, all wrapped in a flagship-feeling build with a modular cable. It's reference done musically — technically excellent without being cold. If you want colored fun it's the wrong pick, but if you want a resolving, well-staged, reference-leaning hybrid at $449, the Tea Pro SE is one of the strongest options in its class.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does the Tea Pro SE need an amp?** No — at 13 Ω / 104 dB it's easy to drive from a dongle. But that low impedance makes it **sensitive to source hiss**, so a clean output matters more than raw power.

**Tea Pro SE vs Moondrop Blessing 3 — which should I buy?** The [Blessing 3](/reviews/moondrop-blessing-3-review/) (~$320) is the value reference hybrid; the Tea Pro SE (~$449) adds a second DD, more BAs, and better ultra-treble extension for more air and technicalities. Same reference philosophy, more resolution for the money up.

**Is it a tribrid?** No — it's a **hybrid** (2 dynamic + 6 balanced armature). If you specifically want a tribrid at this level, look at the Thieaudio Oracle MKII or, cheaper, the Kiwi Ears Halcyon.

**What's the difference from the standard Tea Pro?** The SE brings upgraded Knowles tweeters, a sharper 150 Hz crossover for cleaner low-mid separation, tighter bass, and extended +20 kHz ultra-treble — a refinement of the Tea Pro's reference tuning, not a new sound.
