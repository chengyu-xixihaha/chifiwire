Two Sennheiser open-backs, two different philosophies. The HD 600 is a 1997 legend that has outlasted entire product categories. The HD 550 is Sennheiser's 2024 answer to a simpler question: what if a great open-back didn't require a dedicated amp stack?

Both are worth your attention. Neither is obviously better. Which one belongs on your head depends on how you listen.

**TL;DR:**
- **HD 600** wins on midrange texture, long-term tonal accuracy, and the classic Sennheiser warmth audiophiles reach for in a proper desktop setup
- **HD 550** wins on value, wider soundstage, easier driveability, and more modern ergonomics
- Neither is a mistake — the gap comes down to your source and listening habits

## Sound

Both headphones share Sennheiser's core open-back DNA — spacious, natural, non-fatiguing — but they diverge in character in meaningful ways.

The HD 600 carries a classically lush low-mid body. There is warmth in the upper bass that gives vocals and acoustic instruments a richness that many listeners describe as euphonic rather than analytical. The treble is filled-in through the mid-treble range, which keeps it smooth and even rather than peaky. For genres with natural decay — orchestral, jazz, acoustic singer-songwriter — this tuning rewards long sessions. On aggressive rock or metal, that warmth rounds off harder-edged recordings rather than exposing them.

The HD 550 leans more neutral. Its bass profile is flatter and extends further into sub-bass, which removes the low-mid push of the 600-series and tightens the low end considerably. The midrange is slightly recessed around 3.5 kHz compared to the HD 600, which avoids the forwardness some listeners find fatiguing in the older model. The result is a more clinical presentation — cleaner through the mids, with a broader soundstage. Where the HD 600 stages sound with a focused, intimate "three-blob" imaging characteristic of the 6XX line, the HD 550 opens up laterally and sounds notably wider.

Neither headphone is wrong. The HD 600 gives you warmth and texture; the HD 550 gives you space and clarity.

## Build and Comfort

The HD 600 wears its age well. The 6-series chassis is plastic but feels solid — the material has a density and confidence to it that the HD 550's lighter 5-series frame does not fully match. The iconic speckled finish is distinctive enough that it still stands out on a desk in 2026. Clamp force runs medium-tight out of the box, which can be eased by gently bowing the metal headband arms. Ear chamber depth is generous, and the headband padding holds up over long sessions.

The HD 550 is the lighter, more contemporary-looking headphone. It ships with a 3.5mm cable and adapter rather than a bare quarter-inch, which reflects its positioning as a more portable-friendly product. The ear pads run slightly shallower than the HD 600's, which can push the driver closer to the ear — a fit preference that splits users. The clamp is looser, which some will prefer immediately but others may find feels less secure.

For all-day wear, the HD 600's combination of pad depth and headband padding edges out the HD 550 for most listeners with average or larger head sizes.

## Driveability

This is the sharpest practical difference between the two.

The HD 600 is 300 ohms. It will technically produce sound from a phone or laptop headphone jack, but you are leaving significant performance on the table — output will be quiet, bass will thin out, and dynamics will compress. A dedicated DAC/amp — even a budget dongle like the FiiO KA13 or a desktop entry like the Schiit Magni/Modi stack — unlocks the HD 600 properly. This is not optional if you want to hear what it actually does.

The HD 550 at 150 ohms is easier to drive. It still benefits from amplification, but the delta between phone and amp is smaller. Many users report satisfying results from a mid-tier dongle DAC or even a higher-powered laptop output. For anyone who wants open-back sound without committing to a desktop DAC/amp, the HD 550 is the pragmatic pick.

## Who Should Buy Which

**Buy the HD 600 if:**
- You already own or plan to buy a DAC/amp
- You want the reference midrange that mixing engineers and mastering engineers have trusted for nearly 30 years
- You prefer a warmer, more intimate soundstage for vocal-centric and acoustic listening
- Longevity matters — replacement pads, headbands, and cables are still widely available

**Buy the HD 550 if:**
- You don't have a desktop DAC/amp and aren't sure you want one yet
- You prefer a wider soundstage and a more neutral bass response
- Budget matters — the HD 550 consistently sells $100–150 lower than the HD 600
- You want a more modern-looking headphone that ships with a 3.5mm cable

## Verdict

The HD 600 remains one of the best open-back headphones at any price when paired with appropriate amplification. Its midrange texture, long-term comfort, and proven reliability are hard to argue with, and there is a reason it has never been discontinued.

The HD 550 is not a compromise version of the HD 600 — it is a different headphone with a different target. It offers a wider soundstage, a cleaner low end, and meaningfully lower barriers to entry. For anyone new to open-back listening or unwilling to build a dedicated desktop setup, it is the better starting point.

If you own a DAC/amp or are about to buy one, stretch to the HD 600. If you are not there yet, the HD 550 will show you what you have been missing — and may be all you ever need.
