If you searched "Sennheiser HD 550" and ended up here, there is a good chance you are not entirely sure what you are looking for. That is not a criticism — the HD 550 exists in a confusing historical gap. It was a well-regarded open-back released around 2001, discontinued by roughly 2005, and largely forgotten outside of vintage audio circles. But its name still surfaces in search, and the question underneath that search is usually the same: *is it worth buying used, or should I just get something modern?*

The modern something is the HD 560S. At $199 new, it sits at Sennheiser's current entry point for audiophile open-backs. It is also, by nearly every objective measure, the better headphone. Here is why — and what the HD 550 still gets right.

**TL;DR:**
- **HD 560S** wins on tuning accuracy, comfort, driveability, and reliability
- **HD 550 (vintage)** has warmth and history, but is harder to drive, harder to find in good condition, and sonically behind by modern standards
- **Verdict:** If you have $200, buy the HD 560S. The HD 550 is for collectors and the genuinely curious, not daily drivers.

## Sound

The HD 550 was designed in an era when "audiophile tuning" broadly meant warmth and lushness. It has a prominent low-mid body, a slightly recessed treble, and a relaxed, slightly veiled character in the upper frequencies that contemporary reviewers sometimes praised as smooth but that modern ears tend to read as lacking air and extension. Against current measurement standards, the HD 550 shows elevated bass shelf rolloff, a mid-treble dip, and limited high-frequency extension past 10 kHz. It is not harsh. It is not offensive. But it is clearly a product of its time.

The HD 560S represents where Sennheiser's open-back tuning philosophy landed after two decades of refinement. The bass is controlled and extends cleanly into sub-bass without the warmth hump of older designs. The midrange is even and linear through the critical 1–4 kHz presence range. The treble is where the 560S most visibly departs from its predecessor — it extends openly past 12 kHz, giving cymbal transients and sibilants more energy and air. Some listeners find this brightness fatiguing over long sessions; others consider it the correct target for a neutral reference headphone. Either way, measured frequency response flatness has improved substantially.

For critical listening, mixing reference, or simply hearing a recording as it was intended, the HD 560S is the more accurate tool. The HD 550's warmth, by contrast, flatters certain recordings — older jazz, vinyl rips, acoustic folk — in a way that some listeners genuinely prefer. That preference is real, but it is coloration, not fidelity.

## Build and Comfort

The HD 550 was built to the standard of early 2000s Sennheiser production: solid plastic chassis, velour pads, and a headband design that the brand recycled across the entire HD 5xx line of that era. Units in good condition still feel substantial. Units in poor condition — cracked plastic, compressed pads, degraded foam — are common on the used market and will affect both comfort and sound. Buying blind from auction listings is genuinely risky.

The HD 560S is lighter (240g vs. approximately 280g) and uses a revised headband with better weight distribution. Ear pad depth is sufficient for most ears, and the low clamp force suits extended sessions. At 240g, it is among the lighter options in the open-back mid-fi tier. The cable terminates in 3.5mm with a 6.35mm adapter included, which makes it laptop and dongle DAC friendly out of the box.

If comfort over multi-hour sessions matters to you, the HD 560S is the easier choice — and you are not gambling on the physical condition of a 20-year-old pair.

## Driveability

At ~300 ohms, the HD 550 was never meant for portable use. Early 2000s audiophile headphones assumed a dedicated headphone amplifier, and the HD 550 reflects that assumption. Without amplification, volume is low and the low end thins noticeably. A proper amp is essentially required.

The HD 560S at 120 ohms is substantially easier to drive. It works well from a quality dongle DAC (FiiO KA13, Qudelix 5K, Apple USB-C adapter at a stretch) and scales upward with a dedicated desktop amp without needing exotic output power. For someone who does not want to invest in a full DAC/amp stack, this is a meaningful practical advantage.

## The Used Market Reality

A clean, fully functional HD 550 in good cosmetic condition runs $100–150 on eBay or Headfi classifieds in 2026. A rough-but-working pair might show up for $50–80. This sounds like a bargain against the HD 560S's $199 new price, but the math only works if you factor in condition risk, no warranty, potential pad replacement cost ($20–40 for aftermarket equivalents), and the near-certainty that you are getting a headphone that objectively underperforms the HD 560S.

There is also the simpler issue of scarcity. The HD 550 is not always available. When it appears, listings move quickly or the condition is disappointing. The HD 560S is in stock at Amazon, B&H, and Sennheiser's own site today.

## Verdict

Buy the HD 560S.

That is the short answer, and it applies to almost everyone reading this in 2026. It is better measured, more comfortable, easier to drive, under warranty, and not contingent on finding a 20-year-old pair in acceptable condition. The $199 price is competitive for what it delivers, and within the Sennheiser lineup it sits in a sensible spot before the HD 600 becomes the obvious next step.

The HD 550 is not a bad headphone. In its era, it was genuinely good, and the warm, slightly forgiving tuning still has listeners who prefer it to anything more modern. But if you are choosing between hunting down a vintage pair and buying a new HD 560S, the hunting is only worth it if vintage audio is a hobby for you — not just a means to get sound out of your ears.

For $200 and clear intent to listen to music, the HD 560S is where you should stop.
