The [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) at $79 is one of the most recommended sub-$100 IEMs in ChiFi — neutral-leaning, technically capable, and hard to fault at the price. The [Simgot SuperMix 4]({{< ref "reviews/simgot-supermix-4-review" >}}) costs $109 and adds a planar tweeter to the tribrid formula. Both run 1DD + BA drivers in a hybrid configuration; the SuperMix 4 takes it further with that extra planar element.

The question is simple: does the $30 premium and the extra planar driver actually buy you something meaningful?

**TL;DR**:
- **SuperMix 4** wins on bass impact, treble extension, and excitement factor — it's the "fun" pick
- **Hexa** wins on midrange accuracy, coherence, and value — it's the "correct" pick
- The $30 premium gets you the planar tweeter; it's worth it if treble extension and bass slam matter to you

## Specs side by side

| | **Simgot SuperMix 4** | **Truthear Hexa** |
|---|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Driver config** | 1 DD + 2 BA + 1 planar tweeter | 1 DD + 3 BA |

| **Shell** | Resin | Resin |
| **MSRP** | $109 | $79 |
| **Price gap** | — | $30 cheaper |

## Sound — by frequency

### Bass

**SuperMix 4 wins.** The single dynamic driver in the SuperMix 4 is tuned with V-shaped intent — more sub-bass rumble, more mid-bass punch. If you want to feel the kick drum or the 808 in your ears, the SuperMix 4 delivers that in a way the Hexa doesn't.

The Hexa's dynamic driver is tighter and cleaner. Sub-bass is present but not elevated; mid-bass is controlled rather than punchy. For critical listening and accuracy this is correct behavior. For genre listening — electronic, hip-hop, R&B — it can feel restrained.

The SuperMix 4 doesn't become muddy despite the added bass; there's enough driver control to keep textures legible. But it is unambiguously a bassier IEM.

### Mids

**Hexa wins.** The neutral-analytical tuning keeps the mids forward and accurate. Vocals are clearly placed, instruments have body, and the presentation doesn't feel scooped.

The SuperMix 4's V-shape means the midrange pays the price. Vocals take a step back relative to the bass and treble shelves. It isn't harsh or unpleasant — Simgot has tuned the V tastefully — but if you listen to vocal-led music or acoustic recordings, the Hexa's mids feel more honest and more engaging.

This is the most important trade-off. The midrange is where a lot of music lives, and the Hexa simply keeps more of it in front of you.

### Treble

**SuperMix 4 wins on extension and air.** The planar tweeter is the whole point of the SuperMix 4's spec sheet, and it delivers. High-frequency extension reaches further than the Hexa's BA-only treble, adding genuine air and sheen to cymbals, strings, and acoustic detail.

The Hexa's BA treble is good — it's one of the reasons the Hexa benchmarks well — but it doesn't reach as high. Above 12–14 kHz there's a rolloff that the planar driver crosses comfortably.

The trade-off: the SuperMix 4 can be brighter on harsh recordings. Sibilant-heavy tracks or aggressive mastering will expose the elevated treble shelf. Treble-sensitive listeners should factor this in. The Hexa is more forgiving with poor recordings.

### Technicalities

**Very close; each wins somewhere.** The SuperMix 4 has better treble resolution — the planar tweeter resolves micro-detail in the top end that the Hexa's BAs don't fully surface. Soundstage is marginally wider on the SuperMix 4, consistent with its V-shaped staging.

The Hexa wins on **overall coherence**. Multi-driver IEMs always carry crossover risk; the Hexa's 1DD + 3BA integration is well-implemented and sounds unified. The SuperMix 4's four drivers are mostly seamless, but the planar tweeter occasionally draws attention to itself on certain material — a subtle handoff artifact rather than a flaw.

## Cable and accessories

This is a meaningful practical difference. The SuperMix 4 ships with a **modular cable** that includes both 3.5mm SE and 4.4mm balanced terminations in the box. You can plug into a balanced source immediately without buying a cable upgrade.

The Hexa's stock cable is **3.5mm SE only**. If your DAP or dongle has a 4.4mm output — which is now the norm — you'll need a separate cable to use it balanced. Budget $15–25 for that if you buy the Hexa.

Adjust your price comparison accordingly. The SuperMix 4 at $109 includes what you'd need; the Hexa at $79 + a balanced cable narrows the gap.

## Who should buy each

### Get the **Simgot SuperMix 4** if:
- You want **more bass impact** and listen to electronic, hip-hop, pop, or R&B
- **Treble extension and air** matter to you — the planar tweeter is the real differentiator
- You're plugging into a **4.4mm balanced source** and don't want to buy an extra cable
- You want an exciting, fun IEM rather than a clinical one
- You're coming from the Hexa and want something noticeably different

### Get the **Truthear Hexa** if:
- You want a **neutral reference point** — accurate, Harman-adjacent, analytical
- **Vocal presence and mids** are priorities (singer-songwriter, jazz, classical)
- You're **treble-sensitive** and prefer a more forgiving top end
- You're **phone-only** or don't yet have a dedicated source chain
- You want **maximum value** at the sub-$100 tier — the Hexa per-dollar is hard to beat

## Verdict

The $30 premium on the SuperMix 4 isn't arbitrary. You're paying for the planar tweeter and the V-shaped tuning that deploys it. If those are things you want — more treble air, more bass slam, a more exciting presentation — the upgrade is real and worthwhile.

If you don't specifically want those things, the Hexa is the smarter buy. It's more accurate, more coherent, and more versatile across genres and source pairings. It's the better reference IEM. The SuperMix 4 is the better listening-for-fun IEM.

Neither is wrong. They're built for different priorities. Know which listener you are.
