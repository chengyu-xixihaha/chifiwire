Two Sennheisers people cross-shop constantly, but for the wrong reason: they think it's an "upgrade" decision. It isn't. The **HD 560S** ($200) is a bright open-back neutral monitor. The **HD 480 Pro** ($399) is a smooth closed-back pro-tier tracking headphone. Doubling the price gets you sealed isolation, no treble peak, and a warmer studio tuning — not "more" of the same headphone.

Here's the direct answer: **buy the HD 560S if you mix in a quiet room and want the cheapest way into honest sound; buy the HD 480 Pro if you need isolation, treble that survives long sessions, or one headphone that does tracking *and* casual listening.** If you already own the HD 560S and only listen for enjoyment, the 480 Pro isn't an upgrade — it's a different tool.

**TL;DR:**
- **HD 560S** wins on: price, weight, open-back airiness, imaging for the money, "true neutral" reference for critical listening.
- **HD 480 Pro** wins on: isolation, smoother fatigue-free treble, tighter sealed bass, versatility (tracking + monitoring + casual), build quality.
- **Different jobs.** The 560S is a $200 neutral open-back; the 480 Pro is a $399 closed-back studio can. Neither is "better."

Impedance-wise both are approachable — 120 Ω vs 130 Ω is a rounding error. Neither is a phone-out headphone (you'll get low volume and thin sound), but any audio interface (Focusrite Scarlett, MOTU M2, UA Volt) or dedicated headphone jack drives them cleanly. So driveability is not a decision factor here, unlike the [HD 600 vs HD 480 Pro comparison]({{< ref "vs/sennheiser-hd600-vs-hd480-pro" >}}) where the 300 Ω HD 600 genuinely needs a real amp.

## Sound comparison

### Bass

The **HD 480 Pro's sealed low-frequency chamber gives it noticeably more sub-bass** and tighter mid-bass than the HD 560S. The 560S has extended-but-not-emphasized bass that rolls off gently below 40 Hz — flat is correct, but flat is not physically weighty. If you mix EDM, hip-hop, or anything where sub-bass matters, the 480 Pro shows you what's actually happening in the bottom octave. If you mix folk, classical, or acoustic, the 560S's honest roll-off is fine. **Advantage: HD 480 Pro** for sub-bass presence.

### Midrange

Very close. Both have accurate, well-separated mids without coloration. The 480 Pro sits vocals slightly forward with a mild warm tilt — pleasant for tracking singers who don't want to hear themselves in ruthless detail. The 560S is flatter through the mids with a hair more presence in the upper mid range. Neither is muddy, neither is scooped. **Advantage: HD 480 Pro for tracking; HD 560S for critical mid-range analysis.**

### Treble

The clearest split. The **HD 560S has a measured peak in the upper treble (8–12 kHz)** that's the source of most "is my HD 560S defective?" threads — it isn't defective, it's [tuned that way on purpose]({{< ref "reviews/sennheiser-hd-560s-review" >}}) to expose recording detail. Great for finding sibilance and mix problems; fatiguing over 4-hour sessions with lossy or harsh material.

The **HD 480 Pro's treble is smooth and essentially peak-free** — reviewers repeatedly called it fatigue-free over 6-hour sessions. The trade-off: you'll miss top-end problems more easily. If your job is mixing and hunting problems, the 560S surfaces them faster. If your job is tracking or long-form monitoring, the 480 Pro doesn't punish you. **Advantage depends on the job.**

### Soundstage & isolation

Different worlds. The **HD 560S is open-back**: wider, airier stage, better imaging separation, and it runs cool over long wear — but zero isolation and it leaks into every open mic in the room. The **HD 480 Pro is closed-back** with a stage that's surprisingly wide for a sealed can, plus real isolation for tracking booths, noisy spaces, or open-plan offices.

**If any live mic is near you, the 560S is disqualified and the 480 Pro is the only option.** If you're mixing alone in a quiet apartment, the 560S's openness is more natural to listen to for hours.

## Build and comfort

Both are plastic-heavy — Sennheiser puts budget in the drivers, not the housings. The HD 480 Pro feels slightly more substantial and has a proper mini-XLR cable connection (industry standard for studio use) instead of the 560S's less-robust 3.5mm jack on the left cup. Neither creaks or feels flimsy.

Comfort is a wash. Both have soft velour pads, minimal clamp, and low weight (240 g vs 272 g). Multi-hour sessions with either are painless. The 560S runs slightly cooler because it's open-back and doesn't trap heat.

## Use case matrix

| Job | Pick |
|---|---|
| Mixing in a quiet, treated room | HD 560S (honest, cheaper, treble surfaces problems) |
| Mixing where noise leakage matters | HD 480 Pro (closed, smoother) |
| Tracking vocals or acoustic instruments | HD 480 Pro (isolation is non-negotiable) |
| Casual music listening, hours per day | HD 480 Pro (smoother treble, less fatigue) |
| Detail-hunting / critical A/B | HD 560S (upper treble peak surfaces detail) |
| Gaming / open-plan office | HD 480 Pro (isolation both ways) |
| First "real" headphone, budget matters | HD 560S ($200 gets you 80% of the way) |
| Sub-bass presence for EDM / hip-hop production | HD 480 Pro (sealed low-frequency chamber) |

## When the extra $200 is worth it

- **You track anything with a mic** — isolation makes the 480 Pro table-stakes, not upgrade.
- **You listen for 4+ hours a day** — the 480 Pro's smooth treble is worth the money in avoided fatigue.
- **You need one headphone for everything** — mixing, tracking, and casual listening all work on the 480 Pro; the 560S is a mixing-first tool.
- **Sub-bass matters to your work** — the 480 Pro's sealed LF chamber genuinely gives you more low end to reference.

## When to stay with the 560S

- **You mix in a quiet room and don't track** — the 560S is more honest for mix reference and cheaper.
- **You already own an HD 600 or similar warm open-back** — the 560S is a valuable second reference at a different tuning; the 480 Pro overlaps too much.
- **Your budget is tight** — $200 saved is a headphone amp, an interface, or a chunk of your DAC budget.
- **You genuinely like bright treble** — some listeners prefer it, and the 560S delivers more of it than the 480 Pro.

## Which should you buy?

If you're spending money for the first time on a serious headphone and you have a quiet room: **HD 560S**. It's the cheapest ticket into honest neutral sound and it will teach you what your music actually contains.

If you'll ever put a mic in the same room, spend all day in a headphone, or need one can that does everything: **HD 480 Pro**. It's twice the price for a reason — the sealed design and smoother tuning solve real problems the 560S doesn't try to solve.

Do not upgrade from the 560S to the 480 Pro for "more resolution" or "better sound" — that's not the trade. Upgrade for isolation, fatigue-free listening, or sub-bass presence. If none of those describe you, keep the $200.
