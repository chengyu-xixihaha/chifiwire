If you're shopping under $100 for IEMs in 2026, two names keep coming up: the **Truthear Hexa** at $79.99 and the **Moondrop Aria 2** at $89.99. They're a $10 apart, they both target the audiophile crowd, and they both have huge community backing. But they're not redundant — they take fundamentally different approaches to the same problem, and which one is "better" depends entirely on what you want from an IEM.

Short version: **the Hexa is the technical pick, the Aria 2 is the musical pick**. Both are excellent. Read on for which fits you.

## Specs side by side

| | **Truthear Hexa** | **Moondrop Aria 2** |
|---|---|---|
| **Price** | $79.99 | $89.99 |
| **Driver config** | 1DD + 3BA tribrid (10mm LCP DD + 2 composite BA + 1 high BA) | Single 10mm dynamic, TiN ceramic-coated dome |
| **Impedance** | 20.5 Ω | 33 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 120 dB/Vrms @1kHz | 122 dB/Vrms @1kHz |
| **Frequency response** | 8 Hz – 40 kHz | 16 Hz – 22 kHz (IEC61094) |
| **Shell** | Resin with metallic matte finish | CNC zinc alloy + brass nozzle |
| **Cable termination** | 3.5mm SE (modular cable add-on $25+) | **Modular 3.5mm + 4.4mm in box** |
| **Target tuning** | Harman-derivative, neutral with slight sub-bass lift | Moondrop VDSF (warmer Harman variant) |
| **MSRP delta** | -$10 vs Aria 2 | +$10 (but includes balanced cable) |

The headline difference: **Hexa is a 4-driver hybrid, Aria 2 is a single dynamic**. That single fact drives 80% of the sound difference below.

## Sound — round by round

### Bass

- **Hexa**: Tight, fast, controlled. The 10mm LCP DD has decent sub-bass extension but the focus is precision, not slam. Bass guitars are articulate; kick drums punch but don't rumble.
- **Aria 2**: Meatier mid-bass, more body, more "fun". Sub-bass is competent but politer than the Hexa. Bass guitars have more weight and warmth.

**Winner depends on genre**: Hexa for classical, jazz, complex prog. Aria 2 for rock, pop, vocals, acoustic.

### Mids

- **Hexa**: Clean, slightly forward, very accurate. The BAs handle the mids and they're transparent — instruments sound like instruments. Some find it slightly cold or "studio monitor" in flavor.
- **Aria 2**: Warmer, more lush. Female vocals especially shine — there's an airy texture that the Hexa lacks. Male vocals have appropriate chest resonance.

**Winner for vocals**: Aria 2, no contest. **Winner for instrument timbre**: Hexa edges it for orchestral / classical, Aria 2 wins for acoustic / rock.

### Treble

- **Hexa**: Bright, extended, detailed. The high BA gives you sparkle on cymbals and detail in upper harmonics. Can be slightly fatiguing on harsh recordings or with poor source. A few percent of users find it sibilant.
- **Aria 2**: Comfortable, smoother, lacks the last bit of sparkle. Won't fatigue you over long sessions. Hi-hats and crash cymbals are present but politer.

**Winner**: Hexa for detail / shine, Aria 2 for fatigue-free long listening.

### Soundstage and imaging

- **Hexa**: Surprisingly wide for an IEM, imaging is precise. The tribrid arrangement helps separate instruments cleanly.
- **Aria 2**: Stage is more intimate, depth is good but width is narrower than Hexa.

**Winner**: Hexa, clear advantage.

### Technical performance

- **Hexa**: Higher resolution, better instrument separation, more detail retrieval. Punches well above $80.
- **Aria 2**: Class-appropriate; Moondrop's low-distortion engineering shows but the single DD has a ceiling.

**Winner**: Hexa for sheer technical chops.

### Source synergy

- **Hexa**: At 20.5 Ω + 120 dB, it's easy to drive but **noticeably benefits from a clean source**. A dongle DAC (Apple, Moondrop Dawn Pro) takes it from "good" to "great".
- **Aria 2**: At 33 Ω + 122 dB, it sounds polished out of any source — phone headphone jack, basic dongle, anything.

**Winner for picky source**: Aria 2 (more forgiving). **Winner for scaling with better gear**: Hexa.

## Build, fit, accessories

**Hexa**:
- Resin shell, lightweight, comfortable for hours
- Stock cable is plain (4-core, fixed 3.5mm) — modular cable is $25 extra
- Stock tips adequate, decent case
- Build feels good for the price but not premium

**Aria 2**:
- Zinc alloy shell, real heft, premium feel
- **Modular cable with 3.5mm AND 4.4mm plugs in the box** — saves you $25+
- Stock tips are a downgrade from prior Aria SE (no Spring Tips)
- Replaceable brass nozzle for durability

**Winner**: Aria 2 — the modular cable in box is genuine value. Hexa's build is competent but Aria 2 feels like the more expensive product.

## Verdict

### Pick the Truthear Hexa if...

- You prioritize **detail, separation, and soundstage** over warmth
- You listen to **classical, jazz, orchestral, or complex genres**
- You have (or plan to use) a **dedicated source** like a dongle DAC
- You don't need a balanced cable
- You'd rather save $10 and **buy better tips with the difference**

### Pick the Moondrop Aria 2 if...

- You prioritize **musicality and easy listening** over technicality
- You listen to **rock, pop, vocals, acoustic genres**
- You want something that **sounds great from your phone** with no extras
- You **want a balanced (4.4mm) cable included** — saves $25
- You value **better build quality** and don't mind paying $10 for it

### The honest take

Most people will be happier long-term with the **Aria 2**. The Hexa is more impressive on first listen, especially if you A/B with cheap IEMs — but the brighter treble can wear thin over months, and not everyone has a dongle DAC to feed it properly. The Aria 2 is the "set and forget" pick.

**Audiophiles who already own multiple IEMs and want a clean reference** should grab the Hexa.
