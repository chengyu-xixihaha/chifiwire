This is the classic "should I upgrade my Hexa or get a planar?" question. The [Letshuoer S12 Pro]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-s12-pro-review" >}}) at $160 is **the most-recommended budget planar on r/iems**. The [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) at $79 is **the most-recommended sub-$100 hybrid**. Both are reference picks at their tiers.

This guide breaks down the actual differences — sound, source needs, who picks which — so you don't drop $160 only to realize you preferred the $79 sibling.

**TL;DR**:
- **S12 Pro** wins on sub-bass extension, speed, sound coherence — if you have a good source
- **Hexa** wins on phone usability, treble extension via BA, value per dollar
- Same listener can prefer either — there's no objectively-better pick

## Specs side by side

| | **Letshuoer S12 Pro** | **Truthear Hexa** |
|---|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Driver** | 14.8mm planar magnetic | 1 DD + 3 BA hybrid |
| **Diaphragm** | Single planar film | 10mm LCP DD + 3 BAs |
| **Impedance** | 16 Ω | 20 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 102 dB/Vrms | 120 dB/Vrms |
| **Shell** | Aluminum | Resin + metal nozzle |
| **Cable included** | 0.78mm 2-pin, 4-core silver-plated | 0.78mm 2-pin, basic |
| **Tip selection** | Wide foam + silicone | Silicone only |
| **MSRP** | $159.99 | $78.99 |
| **Source needs** | Dongle DAC minimum | Phone-only acceptable |

## Sound — by frequency

### Sub-bass

**S12 Pro wins decisively.** The 14.8mm planar diaphragm moves so much air at deep frequencies that it feels almost subwoofer-like. Extension is flat to 20 Hz with authority.

The Hexa's 10mm DD is good — flat to 30 Hz — but the planar's physical advantage at deep frequencies is obvious. For electronic / cinema / hip-hop, the S12 Pro is a real upgrade.

### Mid-bass

**Tie** — different character. S12 Pro mid-bass is fast and clean (planar speed). Hexa mid-bass is articulated but slightly forward. Neither is "better" — pick by genre.

### Lower mids

**S12 Pro is slightly fuller**. The planar coherence makes lower-mid instruments (cellos, low piano, electric bass) feel more "real". Hexa's tucked mid-bass means lower mids feel slightly thin by comparison.

### Vocals

**Pure preference**. S12 Pro vocals are smoother and more present. Hexa vocals are slightly recessed but more detailed (BA articulation).

For warm-voiced singers (Adele, jazz vocalists), S12 Pro is more emotionally engaging. For analytical listening (technical death metal, prog), Hexa's detail wins.

### Upper mids / lower treble

**Hexa wins on detail; S12 Pro on naturalness.** The BAs deliver every breath, every fret slide, every cymbal stick hit. The S12 Pro is smoother — less articulation but more "real" instrument timbre.

### Treble extension

**Hexa extends further with BAs.** The S12 Pro has the known "8 kHz peak" — sharp without proper EQ. The Hexa's BA treble is forward but feels more controlled. Treble-sensitive listeners struggle with either; warm-tip rolling helps both.

### Soundstage

**S12 Pro is bigger** in all three dimensions. Lateral width, front-to-back depth, height — all marginally larger. The Hexa stages well for a hybrid but doesn't compete with the planar's intrinsic spaciousness.

### Imaging

**S12 Pro wins on imaging precision.** Single-driver coherence beats multi-driver crossover handoffs. The Hexa imaging is good; the S12 Pro is better.

### Speed and transients

**S12 Pro wins**. Planar transient response is exceptional — fast attack, fast decay. The Hexa is no slouch but the planar's physical advantage is real.

## Source requirements

This is the most important practical difference.

**S12 Pro needs a real source.** At 102 dB/Vrms, phone alone is underpowered. A dongle DAC ($30-50) is the minimum; a DAP ($150+) unlocks the full performance.

**Hexa works from phones.** 120 dB sensitivity + 20 Ω = standard phone outputs drive it adequately. You can grow into a dongle DAC later.

**Practical implication**: if you don't have a dongle DAC, the Hexa is a $79 IEM that gets 90% performance. The S12 Pro is a $160 IEM that gets 50% performance from a phone. Total cost to compare honestly is $79 (Hexa) vs $160 + $50 dongle = $210 (S12 Pro setup).

## Genre fit

| Genre | S12 Pro | Hexa | Winner |
|-------|---------|------|--------|
| **EDM / Electronic** | Excellent sub-bass | Clean but less impact | **S12 Pro** |
| **Hip-hop / Trap** | Sub-bass slam | Tucked, controlled | **S12 Pro** |
| **Rock / Alternative** | Good but slightly smooth | Detailed, lit-up | **Tie / preference** |
| **Metal (death / black)** | Speed + coherence | BA detail, but mid-bass tight | **S12 Pro** for blast beats |
| **Jazz** | Natural timbre | Detail-forward | **S12 Pro** for trumpet |
| **Classical / Orchestral** | Stage + coherence | Layered detail | **S12 Pro** for symphonic |
| **Pop / Vocal-led** | Warmer vocals | More articulate | **Tie / preference** |
| **Acoustic** | Natural | Slightly bright | **S12 Pro** |

If you mostly listen to electronic, metal, or classical: S12 Pro wins.
If you mostly listen to detail-heavy / studio-produced music with complex arrangements: Hexa is excellent at half the price.

## Build, fit, comfort

**S12 Pro**: aluminum shell, premium feel. Heavier — some long-session users feel ear fatigue. Comes with a wider variety of tips (foam + silicone), making fit easier.

**Hexa**: lightweight resin. More comfortable for long sessions. Tips selection is more limited; many users tip-roll to find the right fit.

## Cable and accessories

S12 Pro ships with a notably-better cable (4-core silver-plated). The Hexa cable works but feels cheaper. If you're cable-sensitive, the S12 Pro feels more premium out-of-box.

## Where each falls short

### S12 Pro weaknesses
- Treble peak (around 8 kHz) — sharp without EQ
- Needs a real source — phone alone is underpowered
- $160 is real money for many buyers
- Some users find planar timbre slightly "wrong" on acoustic instruments

### Hexa weaknesses
- Vocals slightly recessed (preference)
- Treble is real treble — sensitive listeners struggle
- Less sub-bass slam than competitors at this tier
- BA detail isn't always "musical"

## Final verdict — which to pick?

### Get the **S12 Pro** if:
- You **already have a dongle DAC or DAP**
- You listen to **electronic, classical, metal** primarily
- You want **planar speed and sub-bass extension**
- You're upgrading from a $50-80 IEM
- You're willing to **EQ down the 8 kHz peak**

### Get the **Hexa** if:
- You're **phone-only** or just starting your source chain
- You want a **reference / detail-focused IEM** for analytical listening
- You're **on a $100 budget** with no room for source upgrades
- You're **new to audiophile listening** and want one IEM to learn
- You listen to varied music — not specifically genres that favor planar

### Get **both** if:
- You're **established in the hobby** and want to explore both topologies
- You have **different use cases** (commute vs home listening)
- You're a **collector / curator** building a reference library

## Where this guide leaves you

There's no "winner" — both are reference picks for different reasons. The S12 Pro is the better IEM for power users with sources. The Hexa is the better IEM for everyone else.

**My honest take**: start with the Hexa unless you're certain you have a source ready to feed the S12 Pro. The Hexa's 90% performance from a phone is more practical than the S12 Pro's 50% performance from a phone.

If you have a source already? Get the S12 Pro. It's the better IEM at full power.
