The [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) is the answer to "what should I buy if I want a proper IEM for $150." The [Moondrop Blessing 3]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-blessing-3-review" >}}) is the answer to the question you ask six months later: "is there something better, and does it matter?"

Both target Harman-derived tuning. Both use a 1DD + 4BA driver split. Both have received broad critical consensus as the reference point in their respective price tiers. The $170 between them is where the upgrade debate lives — and it's one of the most common arguments in ChiFi communities right now.

Here's the direct answer: **the Nova is the better value. The Blessing 3 is the better instrument.** The gap is audible but not enormous. Whether it matters depends on what you're coming from, what you're listening through, and what you actually want from an IEM.

## Specs side by side

| | **Truthear Nova** | **Moondrop Blessing 3** |
|---|---|---|
| **Price** | $149.99 | $319.99 |
| **Driver config** | 1 DD + 4 BA tribrid | 1 DD + 4 BA hybrid |
| **Tuning target** | Harman IE 2019 V2 | Moondrop VDSF (Harman-derived) |
| **Impedance** | 14.8 Ω | 22 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 123 dB/Vrms | 117 dB/Vrms |
| **Shell** | Acrylic | Resin |
| **Cable** | 3.5mm SE only | Modular (3.5mm + 4.4mm both included) |
| **Price difference** | — | +$170 |

One number stands out: the Blessing 3 is 6 dB less sensitive than the Nova. That's a meaningful spec — you'll need more gain from your source, and it means the Blessing 3 rewards upstream investment more than the Nova does.

## Sound comparison

### Bass

**Blessing 3 wins.** Both IEMs get this right, but the Blessing 3 does it better. The 1DD handling bass in the Nova is capable and clean; the Blessing 3's DD is faster, better-defined, and more textured below 40Hz.

On electronic and orchestral music with real low-end content — sub-bass rumble, bass guitar body, double bass resonance — the Blessing 3 has a physical quality that the Nova approximates but doesn't fully achieve. It's not about bass quantity; both are Harman-tuned and tasteful. It's about how the bass is *resolved*. The Blessing 3 is cleaner, tighter, and has more texture on complex bass passages.

**Verdict**: Blessing 3, clearly.

### Mids

**Very close — edge to Blessing 3 on technical quality, preference-dependent on presentation.**

The Nova's midrange is one of its best attributes: vocals sit at the right level, guitar and piano have appropriate body, nothing is nasal or recessed. For $150, it's exceptional.

The Blessing 3's four BAs resolve more — guitar body resonance, vocal chest resonance, the breath under a held note. It's not a tuning difference so much as a resolution difference: the Blessing 3 renders the same notes with more of what's actually on the recording.

Neither is forward or recessed in a way that makes one "better for vocals" categorically. If you want more vocal presence, neither is the pick — the Truthear Hexa or Aria 2 will push vocals forward. These are both reference-tuned, and both handle vocals with the same neutrality.

**Verdict**: Blessing 3 on resolution; tie on presentation preference.

### Treble

**Nova is safer. Blessing 3 is more extended — and more demanding.**

The Nova's treble is polite. Extended to around 14kHz, clean, no harshness, controlled sibilance. Its chief criticism is that it can sound *too* polite — some listeners want more sparkle and air, and the Nova's treble just won't give it to them.

The Blessing 3 targets Moondrop's VDSF curve — a Harman derivative with more upper treble extension. Cymbals shimmer more, air frequencies are more present, and the top end is genuinely detailed. The trade-off: the Blessing 3 will tell you if a recording is badly mastered or if your source chain has issues. On well-recorded material, it's excellent. On harsh recordings or bright sources, it can tip toward sharp.

Treble-sensitive listeners: the Nova is the right choice here, and that's not a knock — it's a design intent difference.

**Verdict**: Nova for safety and forgiveness; Blessing 3 for extension and resolution. Know your sensitivity.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Blessing 3 wins, and this is where the price gap is most audible.**

The Nova has a wide stage with precise imaging — it's a genuine strength at $150. The Blessing 3 makes the Nova's stage look two-dimensional. Instruments are placed with surgical accuracy in three dimensions; front-to-back depth is clearly resolved, not inferred. On orchestral recordings, section positioning, soloist placement, and hall reverb all land distinctly.

This isn't a small difference. If you A/B them back-to-back on a complex classical or jazz recording, the Blessing 3's staging is immediately apparent. It's the single most convincing argument for the $170 premium.

**Verdict**: Blessing 3, clearly, and by a wider margin than any other category.

## Build comparison

Neither will make you feel great about your purchase if you're thinking per-dollar.

The Nova uses an **acrylic shell** — lightweight, comfortable, universally fitting, and exactly what you'd expect from a $150 IEM. The glittery faceplate is divisive; the fit is reliable for most ears.

The Blessing 3 uses a **resin shell** — better-finished than the Nova, with a semi-translucent faceplate and Moondrop's gradient colorway, but still not metal. For $320, that stings a little. The competition at this price includes metal-shelled alternatives.

One real advantage the Blessing 3 has: the **modular cable includes both 3.5mm and 4.4mm plugs in the box**. That's not a small thing. At $320, Moondrop gives you everything you need to use balanced output immediately. The Nova ships with 3.5mm only — to run balanced, you're buying an aftermarket cable.

The Blessing 3's shell is also larger than the Nova's. If you struggled with the Blessing 2's fit, expect the same with the 3. The Nova's acrylic shell is more accommodating of smaller ears.

**Verdict**: Blessing 3 on cable inclusion; Nova on fit comfort and reliability for a wider range of ear sizes.

## Source requirements

This is where the upgrade decision gets practical.

**The Nova drives from anything.** At 14.8Ω / 123dB, your phone will run it fine. You won't hear it struggling. It scales up with better sources, but it doesn't need them.

**The Blessing 3 rewards a real source.** At 22Ω / 117dB, it's still technically easy to drive — but it's less sensitive, and it responds more noticeably to source quality. Running the Blessing 3 from a phone is leaving performance on the table. A dongle DAC (FiiO KA17, Moondrop Dawn Pro, iFi Go Blu) unlocks the stage and bass definition. Balanced output from a DAP widens the gap with the Nova further.

If you don't have a dongle DAC or DAP, the practical upgrade order is: **phone + Nova → phone + dongle DAC → dongle DAC + Blessing 3**. Skipping the source upgrade and jumping straight to the Blessing 3 underutilizes the IEM.

## Who should upgrade to the Blessing 3

**Get the Blessing 3 if:**

- You've already owned the Nova for a while, you know what it does, and you want to hear what comes next
- You have a dongle DAC or DAP already — or you're buying one at the same time
- Soundstage and imaging precision matter to you (jazz, classical, acoustic music where placement is meaningful)
- You want a one-IEM-for-everything solution at the level where diminishing returns genuinely plateau
- You're not treble-sensitive

The Blessing 3 is not an emotional purchase — it won't excite you with bass rumble or sparkle. It will reveal things in music you didn't hear before, and do it consistently across every genre. That's the value proposition.

## Who should stay with the Nova

**Stick with the Nova if:**

- You're coming from budget IEMs and haven't maxed out what the Nova offers yet — the gap between $79 IEMs and the Nova is larger than the gap between the Nova and the Blessing 3
- You listen on a phone or without a dedicated source — the Nova is more efficient, and you'll close the gap without a real DAC/amp upstream
- You're treble-sensitive — the Nova is significantly more forgiving, and that's a real reason to stay, not a consolation prize
- You'd rather spend $170 on a [dongle DAC]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-iems-under-200-2026" >}}) — which is the correct move if you don't already have one
- You listen to heavily compressed or harshly mastered music — the Nova is more forgiving of bad recordings

The Nova at $150 with a $50 dongle DAC will outperform the Blessing 3 run from a phone. That's the honest comparison that the upgrade question requires.

## Final verdict

The Blessing 3 is better. Audibly, measurably, and across every technical metric that matters. But "better" isn't the full answer when the price gap is 2× and the upgrade requires a source chain to hear the difference.

**Buy the Blessing 3** if you've heard the Nova, you have a decent source, and you know you want more. The soundstage improvement alone is worth the price for the right listener.

**Buy the Nova** if you're starting out at this price tier, you're source-limited, you're treble-sensitive, or you'd rather apply that $170 to a better DAC and tips. In most of those scenarios, the Nova + source upgrade sounds better than the Blessing 3 alone.

The answer to "is the upgrade worth it?" is: **yes, if you're ready for it. No, if you haven't finished hearing what you already have.**
