The [Moondrop Aria 2]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) at $89 is the IEM people keep describing as "just sounds right." The [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) at $149 is the Harman-tuned tribrid that has become the default sub-$200 recommendation for neutral-preference listeners. The $60 gap between them is real — but so is the question of whether technical improvement always means better for *your* listening.

**Short answer**: the Nova is the more capable IEM on paper. The Aria 2 is the one many listeners enjoy more. Your music and preferences decide which matters.

## Sound — head to head

### Bass

**Character split: Aria 2 warm, Nova clean**

These two take different approaches, and neither is objectively better — they serve different preferences.

The Aria 2's single LCP dynamic driver delivers mid-bass warmth and body. Kick drums have weight. Acoustic bass has a natural fullness. It is "analog-feeling" bass — round, textured, satisfying on warm genres. Sub-bass is present but secondary to the mid-bass character.

The Nova flips the balance. Sub-bass extends further and hits harder below 40 Hz, while mid-bass is deliberately restrained in line with its Harman target. Electronic music, modern hip-hop, and anything with sub drops benefit from Nova's more physical low end. On acoustic genres, that same restraint can feel slightly lean compared to the Aria 2.

**Pick**: Aria 2 for warmth and body. Nova for sub-bass extension and cleaner low-end separation.

### Midrange

**Winner: Aria 2 for timbre, Nova for resolution**

Both IEMs handle mids well, but the texture is meaningfully different.

The Aria 2's single DD midrange is where it earns its reputation. Vocals — especially female vocals, acoustic guitar, and piano — have a naturalness that multi-driver designs chase but often can't quite replicate. There is no crossover point in the midrange. The sound is cohesive, warm, and forgiving of poorly mastered material. If you listen to a lot of jazz, acoustic, or classic rock, this matters more than any spec.

The Nova's BA-assisted midrange is noticeably more detailed and resolving. It picks apart layered arrangements, finds low-level reverb tails, and renders complex harmonies with more clarity. The BA texture in the mids is slightly more "electronic" — not unpleasant, but you lose a fraction of the single-DD organicness. For critical listening or dense modern production, that trade is worth it.

**Pick**: Aria 2 for vocal music and naturalness. Nova for resolution and complex arrangements.

### Treble

**Winner: Nova for extension, Aria 2 for smoothness**

The Aria 2 rolls off earlier in the treble. Cymbals have presence and air, but fine shimmer and micro-details in the upper registers are softened. The result is never harsh, never fatiguing — it is a very easy signature to live with for long sessions.

The Nova extends further and delivers more BA sparkle. Cymbals have cleaner definition, and high-frequency instrument separation is noticeably better. The trade is that on bright recordings or with bright sources, the Nova's treble can become forward. It is not sibilant by default, but it is less forgiving than the Aria 2.

**Pick**: Nova for treble detail and extension. Aria 2 for treble-sensitive listeners or long listening sessions.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Winner: Nova**

The Nova's tribrid configuration produces a wider, more three-dimensional soundstage. Instruments sit in distinct positions, depth separation is clear, and complex orchestral or electronic music retains coherence under pressure. Imaging precision is noticeably better than the Aria 2.

The Aria 2 has a pleasantly open, enveloping presentation, but individual elements blend more cohesively rather than separating sharply. That cohesion is part of its musical character — it works beautifully for genres where immersion matters more than analytical placement.

**Pick**: Nova for gaming, classical, and anything where instrument positioning matters.

### Detail retrieval

**Winner: Nova**

The four BAs in the Nova's tribrid configuration retrieve micro-detail that the Aria 2's single driver cannot match. Low-level breath sounds, hall reverb, subtle room character — all more present on the Nova. This is the clearest technical gap between the two.

## Build and accessories

**Winner: Aria 2 for build quality, Nova for included case**

This is the one area where the $60 premium works backwards. The Aria 2's aluminum shell feels genuinely premium — dense, solid, and cool to the touch. At $89, it builds better than most IEMs at twice the price.

The Nova uses an acrylic shell. It is well-finished and comfortable, but it feels lighter and less substantial than the Aria 2's metal. For a $149 IEM, you expect better.

The cable situation also favors the Aria 2. Its modular 2-pin system ships with both 3.5mm SE and 4.4mm balanced terminations — you can use balanced output from a dongle DAC or DAP without buying an extra cable. The Nova ships with a 3.5mm SE cable only. If you want balanced on the Nova, you are buying a separate cable.

The Nova does include a soft carrying case; the Aria 2's packaging is more basic. Both use standard 0.78mm 2-pin connectors.

## The $60 question

The gap is not obvious on all music. Put on a well-recorded jazz trio, an acoustic singer-songwriter session, or classic rock from the '70s — the Aria 2 holds its own so well that many listeners would prefer it. The single-DD coherence and warmth are genuinely harder to quantify but easy to hear.

Switch to dense modern production — layered electronic, complex orchestral, hip-hop with heavy sub-bass work — and the Nova clearly pulls ahead. More detail, cleaner separation, deeper sub-bass, wider staging. The tribrid technology justifies itself on technically demanding material.

The $60 matters most if you already have a source that reveals the Nova's extra resolution. Phone-only listeners are hearing less of what separates them. With a dongle DAC (Fosi DS2, FiiO KA17), the Nova scales noticeably; the Aria 2 scales too, but the gap widens.

## Who should buy each

### Buy the Moondrop Aria 2 if you:
- Listen primarily to vocal-led music, jazz, acoustic, or classic rock
- Value natural, organic sound over analytical resolution
- Want a metal build that feels premium at the price
- Use 4.4mm balanced and don't want to buy an extra cable
- Prefer smooth, fatigue-free treble for long listening sessions
- Are a casual listener who cares more about enjoyment than technical measurements

### Buy the Truthear Nova if you:
- Want the Harman reference tuning in a tribrid form
- Listen to a wide range of genres, especially electronic, hip-hop, or orchestral
- Are upgrading from a sub-$50 IEM and want the biggest technical leap
- Already have a dongle DAC or DAP (so the Nova can show what it does)
- Prefer wider soundstage and better imaging precision
- Are a detail-oriented listener who notices micro-resolution differences

## Final verdict

The [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) is the technically superior IEM. Better sub-bass extension, more detail retrieval, wider soundstage, more accurate Harman tuning. If you want maximum technical performance under $200, the Nova is the right answer.

The [Moondrop Aria 2]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}) is the more enjoyable IEM for a specific listener. Single-DD timbre, warm natural mids, a metal build that punches above the $89 price point, and a modular cable that future-proofs your investment. On the right music, many experienced listeners will reach for the Aria 2 over the Nova.

**Edge: Nova** — for most listeners upgrading from entry-level gear, the technical gains across bass extension, detail, and soundstage make the $60 worth it. But do not write off the Aria 2 if warm, natural, vocal-centric sound is what you actually listen to. Buy the Nova for versatility. Buy the Aria 2 because you know what you want.
