Two Sennheisers, one price, opposite jobs. The **HD 600** is the 1997 open-back that became the default reference for mixing, and it has outlasted whole categories of gear around it. The **HD 480 Pro** is Sennheiser's April 2026 closed-back studio headphone, built on the HD 490 Pro's 38mm driver but sealed and retuned for tracking. Both land at **$399 street**, which turns "which is better" into a real either/or instead of a budget question.

Here's the direct answer: **the HD 600 is the more honest mixing tool; the HD 480 Pro is the more versatile studio tool.** If your room is treated and you mostly mix, the HD 600 still wins. If you track vocals, work somewhere noisy, or need isolation, the 480 Pro is the smarter single buy.

**TL;DR:**
- **HD 600** wins on: reference honesty, treble detail for hunting sibilance/harshness, open soundstage, running cooler over long sessions.
- **HD 480 Pro** wins on: isolation, smoother fatigue-free treble, tighter sealed bass, weight (272 g), driveability, and doubling as a tracking headphone.
- **Same $399.** The choice is open-back-mix-reference vs closed-back-does-everything, not price.

## Specs side by side

| | **Sennheiser HD 600** | **Sennheiser HD 480 Pro** |
|---|---|---|
| **Price** | ~$399 street | $399 ($439 Pro Plus) |
| **Type** | Open-back, over-ear | Closed-back, over-ear |
| **Driver** | 40mm dynamic | 38mm dynamic, dedicated LF chamber |
| **Impedance** | 300 Ω | 130 Ω |
| **Isolation** | None (open) | Yes (sealed) |
| **Treble** | Brighter, more present | Smoother, no 6–8 kHz peak |
| **Midrange** | Dry, unflattering, honest | Slight warm tilt, pleasant |
| **Weight** | ~260 g | 272 g (no cable) |
| **Released** | 1997 (still current) | April 2026 |
| **Best for** | Mix reference, critical listening | Tracking, monitoring, noisy rooms |

The impedance line tells the story. The HD 600 at **300 Ω** genuinely wants a dedicated headphone amp to reach its potential — off a laptop it sounds thin and quiet. The HD 480 Pro's **130 Ω** is the awkward middle: too high for clean phone-out, but happy off any audio interface (Focusrite Scarlett, UA Volt, MOTU M2). That difference alone points at two different desks.

## Sound comparison

### Bass

The 480 Pro's sealed low-frequency chamber gives it **tighter, weightier, more extended sub-bass** than the open-back HD 600 — it reaches past 30 Hz without the usual closed-back 80–120 Hz hump. Headphones.com even preferred its bass to the open-back HD 490 Pro it shares a driver with. The HD 600's open-back bass is more natural in decay but rolls off earlier and never hits with the same physical weight. **Advantage: HD 480 Pro** for anyone who needs to hear the bottom octave.

### Midrange

This is the HD 600's home turf. Its midrange is famously **dry and unflattering** — it will expose a harsh vocal, a muddy guitar, or a mix problem that gentler headphones smooth over. That is exactly what you want for mix reference and exactly what you don't want tracking a nervous vocalist. The 480 Pro's slight warm tilt makes vocals sit forward and *pleasant*. Pleasant is correct for tracking, suspect for ruthless mixing. **Advantage: HD 600 for mixing, HD 480 Pro for tracking.**

### Treble

The clearest split. The HD 600 is deliberately **brighter** up top, and that brightness is a working tool — it surfaces sibilance in a vocal take and cymbal harshness in a master before your listeners hear them. The 480 Pro's treble is **smoother and essentially peak-free** (reviewers repeatedly called it fatigue-free over 6-hour sessions). The trade-off is real: the 480 Pro won't show you top-end problems as readily. **Advantage depends on the job** — HD 600 to hunt problems, HD 480 Pro to survive long sessions.

### Soundstage & isolation

The HD 600 is open-back: wider, airier stage, and it runs cooler on your ears — but zero isolation and it leaks into every open mic in the room. The 480 Pro is closed-back with a stage that competes better than most sealed cans, plus real isolation for tracking and noisy environments. **If a live mic is anywhere near you, the HD 600 is disqualified and the 480 Pro is the only option.**

## Driveability: the deciding practical factor

At **300 Ω the HD 600 needs a real amp.** A [Topping E50 II]({{< ref "reviews/topping-e50-ii-review" >}}) stack, a dedicated headphone amp, or a capable interface headphone-out is the entry point; a phone or basic laptop jack will leave it thin and underpowered. The **130 Ω HD 480 Pro** drives cleanly off any audio interface and even portable players (with 6–10 dB less headroom). If you don't own an amp and don't want to, that alone can make the decision.

## Which should you buy?

- **Buy the HD 600 if** you mix in a treated room, want the most honest reference tuning, already own a headphone amp, and don't need isolation. It's still the benchmark 29 years later for a reason.
- **Buy the HD 480 Pro if** you track vocals or instruments, work in an untreated or noisy space, want one headphone that does monitoring *and* casual listening, and would rather not buy a separate amp. Full breakdown in our [Sennheiser HD 480 Pro review]({{< ref "reviews/sennheiser-hd-480-pro-review" >}}).
- **Get both, eventually,** if you're building a serious studio — they cover different phases (track on the closed 480 Pro, mix on the open HD 600). That's the classic two-headphone studio setup, and at $399 each it's an accessible one.

The "best studio headphones, period" headline the HD 480 Pro launched with is defensible **for closed-back work** — but it doesn't retire the HD 600 for open-back mix reference. They're not competing for the same seat. Pick by the job in front of you.
