If you've decided your next IEM is a 14.5mm planar and you have roughly $200 to spend, two names dominate the shortlist in 2026: the **Hidizs MP145 Pro** (~$209) and the **7Hz Timeless II** (~$229). They land within twenty dollars of each other, both run the same headline driver size, both ship with swappable tuning nozzles, and both demand a real source to sing. On a spec sheet they look like twins separated at birth — which is exactly why so many people end up comparing them with two carts open.

Look closer, though, and they're built around different philosophies. The Timeless II is the latest evolution of 7Hz's iconic coin-shaped puck — tiny, light, and detail-obsessed, with four nozzle pairs that swing the treble from smooth to sparkly. The MP145 Pro is the opposite shape entirely: a large, heavy aluminum shell housing an upgraded magnet circuit, tuned for a fuller, more natural body. This comparison is less about which is "better" in the abstract and more about which trade-offs fit your ears, your library, and your source.

## Short version / TL;DR

- **Buy the 7Hz Timeless II** if you want a featherweight, disappear-in-your-ear fit, the widest tuning range (four nozzles), and the airiest, most detail-first treble — and you have a strong source to feed it.
- **Buy the Hidizs MP145 Pro** if you want a more natural, full-bodied tonality, a slightly easier load to drive, and you don't mind (or actively prefer) a big shell. Its larger housing fills bigger ears better than the tiny 7Hz puck.
- **Both** are excellent 14.5mm planars with swappable nozzles and balanced-ready cables in the box. There is no wrong answer here — only a better fit.
- **Tie-breakers:** small ears and detail-chasing → Timeless II. Big ears, natural timbre, and a touch less source fuss → MP145 Pro.

The headline difference is physical: the MP145 Pro weighs **roughly twice** as much per side and is a substantially larger shell. The other is electrical — the Hidizs is a higher impedance (30 Ω) but the 7Hz's voltage-spec sensitivity makes both genuine "wants power" planars, just in different ways.

## Sound

These two are closer in tonality than their shells suggest, but the character diverges in the details.

**Bass.** Both deliver the fast, articulate, never-sluggish low end that good planars do best. The **MP145 Pro** leans mid-bass-forward for a fuller, slightly warmer foundation, with clean sub-bass when the track calls for it — punchy and quick rather than thick. The **Timeless II** surprises for the category with genuine sub-bass lift and slam that Headfonics rates "one of the best heard from a planar IEM" — it's the more sub-focused of the two, the Hidizs the more mid-bass-bodied.

**Mids.** The MP145 Pro's midrange is its calling card: described as both lush and clear, with accurate note weight that's neither thin nor thick, and vocal presence without shout. The Timeless II is more **neutral and analytical** — clean, articulate, detail-rich, but drier and less romantic. If vocals and natural body matter most, the Hidizs edges it; if you want clarity-first precision, the 7Hz.

**Treble.** Both are airy and extended with no harsh planar dryness. The **Timeless II** treble is its defining strength — sparkly, revealing, and (on the right nozzles) non-fatiguing, the more overtly detail-forward top end. The **MP145 Pro** extends well too but plays it slightly smoother and more natural rather than chasing maximum sparkle.

**Stage.** Both throw a wide, spacious, above-average stage. The Hidizs is praised for roughly equal width/height/depth and a natural atmosphere; the 7Hz is wider and airier but only average in front-to-back depth. Call it natural-and-rounded (MP145 Pro) versus wide-and-airy (Timeless II).

## Tuning flexibility & fit

This is the most practical differentiator, and it's more nuanced than "nozzles vs no nozzles" — **both** ship with swappable nozzles. The difference is range and shape.

The **Timeless II** gives you **four** nozzle pairs (silver/gold, flat/bullet) that primarily reshape the **treble and upper mids** — from the smooth silver-flat to the sparkly silver-bullet. It's the wider tuning swing, closer to having several treble voicings in one IEM. The **MP145 Pro** offers **three** pneumatic filters (High / Balanced / Low) that shift the broader balance — the silver High nozzle sharpens separation for orchestral and busy tracks, the rose-gold Balanced pushes vocals forward with warmth. Slightly fewer options, but each one moves more of the signature.

Fit is where they truly split. The **Timeless II** is a ~5g coin that sits flush and disappears over long sessions — ideal for small and average ears, and the safer blind buy if you've struggled with bulky IEMs. The **MP145 Pro** is a large ~9.5g shell; reviewers note that despite the size it's ergonomically shaped and comfortable for long listening, and a big shell fills **bigger ears** better than a small puck ever will. If you have larger concha or have found tiny IEMs never seal right, the Hidizs may actually fit *better* — but if you have small ears, the 7Hz is the safer pick.

## Driveability & source pairing

Neither of these is a phone-dongle throwaway, but they ask for power differently. The **MP145 Pro** (30 Ω / 104 dB) is the more conventionally "moderately easy to drive" of the pair — reviewers call it surprisingly efficient for a planar and genuinely enjoyable off portable DAC/amps, while still scaling with a strong DAP or desktop amp for extra dynamics and control. The **Timeless II** quotes 108 dB **per volt** (not per mW) at 15.2 Ω, which means it plays loud off a dongle but truly opens up only with real voltage behind it — a weak source leaves it sounding flat and quiet.

Practical guidance: a good dongle (Shanling UA6, FiiO BTR-class) is the floor for either, and a proper DAP or desktop amp tightens both. If your source is on the weaker side, the **MP145 Pro** is the more forgiving choice. If you already own a capable DAP or amp, the **Timeless II** rewards it more dramatically. ## Who should buy which

### Buy the MP145 Pro if

- You want a **natural, full-bodied tonality** with lush, present mids over clinical precision.
- You have **larger ears** or have struggled with tiny coin-shaped IEMs sealing properly.
- Your source is modest and you want a planar that's **easier to drive well**.
- You value mid-bass warmth and an even, rounded soundstage.
- A heavier shell doesn't bother you (and the Titanium limited edition tempts you).

### Buy the Timeless II if

- You want the **lightest, most disappearing fit** — small or average ears, all-day comfort.
- You're a **detail-and-treble chaser** who wants the airiest, sparkliest top end.
- You want the **widest tuning range** — four nozzles is the bigger swing.
- You already own a **capable source** that can feed a voltage-hungry planar.
- You prefer punchy sub-bass slam and a neutral, analytical mid presentation.

## Verdict

There's no loser here. The **7Hz Timeless II** is the detail-first, feather-light, maximum-flexibility pick — the better choice for small ears, treble lovers, tinkerers, and anyone with a strong source already in hand. The **Hidizs MP145 Pro** is the natural-bodied, easier-driving, big-shell alternative — the better choice for fuller mids, larger ears, and modest sources, and it usually sells for a touch less. Both are among the best 14.5mm planars money buys near $200.

If we had to break the tie for a typical buyer with an average dongle and average ears, the slight nod goes to the **MP145 Pro** for its more forgiving drive and natural timbre — but a small-eared detail-chaser with a good DAP should buy the **Timeless II** without hesitation. Match the IEM to your ears and your source, not to the spec sheet.
