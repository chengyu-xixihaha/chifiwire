The **7Hz Timeless AE ($219)** and the **Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)** are the two most-recommended planar magnetic IEMs under $250. Both use large-diameter planar drivers, both have strong community reputations, and both compete in the same "best planar for the money" conversation. But they are tuned very differently and aimed at very different listeners.

The $59 price gap is real — but so is the tuning gap. Here is what you are actually buying.

## TL;DR

- **7Hz Timeless AE ($219)**: neutral-bright, excellent detail retrieval, tight sub-bass, controlled treble extension. The more technically refined planar. Rewards good sources.
- **Letshuoer S12 Pro ($160)**: V-shaped, elevated bass and treble, slightly recessed mids. More fun and energetic. More forgiving of average sources.

If you only read one line: **buy the AE if you want the most technically accomplished planar under $250; buy the S12 Pro if you want an energetic, fun sound and want to keep $59 in your pocket.**

Both are easy-ish to drive by planar standards — a decent dongle DAC handles both. The AE's slightly higher sensitivity figure does not tell the full story: its neutral-bright tuning and planar transient character respond noticeably to source quality in a way the S12 Pro does not.

## Tuning & sound signature

### 7Hz Timeless AE: neutral-bright, technically refined

The Timeless AE sits in neutral-bright territory. Bass is present but linear — sub-bass extension is excellent, mid-bass is controlled without the extra slam that V-shaped tunings add. Mids are clear and well-positioned: vocals and instruments have accurate presence, no recession. Treble extends confidently with good control — you get genuine air and detail without the harshness that less careful treble tunings produce.

The overall character is clinical in the best sense. Every element of the recording is audible. Layering and imaging are sharp. If a track has texture, you hear it. If a recording is bright or thin, the AE will not hide it — **it is a revealing listen, not a flattering one**.

### Letshuoer S12 Pro: V-shaped, energetic, fun

The S12 Pro takes a different path. Bass is elevated with mid-bass slam that the AE does not attempt — it gives kick drums and bass lines physical weight and presence. Treble is bright and extended, lending energy and sparkle to cymbals and high-frequency transients. The trade-off: mids are slightly recessed, sitting back behind the bass and treble peaks in the V.

The result is immediately engaging and likeable on most genres — electronic, pop, rock, hip-hop all benefit from the extra slam and top-end energy. The S12 Pro **sounds fun to almost everyone on first listen**. The caveat is that the elevated treble can tip into sharpness on poorly recorded or compressed tracks, and some listeners find the mid recession tiring over long sessions.

## Sound comparison by region

### Bass

The S12 Pro hits harder. Mid-bass slam is clearly higher, and the V-shape makes bass the dominant feature of the presentation. Sub-bass extension is good but takes a back seat to the midbass elevation.

The AE's bass is tighter and more linear — sub-bass reaches deep and cleanly, but mid-bass is controlled rather than emphasized. Less fun on bass-heavy music; more accurate on everything else.

**Winner for quantity/slam**: S12 Pro. **Winner for linearity/control**: AE.

### Mids

The AE wins here without contest. Vocals are accurate, forward enough to command attention, and properly positioned relative to instruments. The AE's midrange is where its "neutral-bright" character earns its reputation — it sounds like a well-mastered recording sounds.

The S12 Pro's mids are functional but noticeably recessed. They work; they do not impress. Vocal-heavy music — acoustic, jazz, folk — sounds better on the AE.

**Winner**: 7Hz Timeless AE.

### Treble

Both IEMs have elevated treble, but with different execution. The AE's treble is extended and controlled — you get detail and air without obvious peaks that cause listener fatigue. It is bright but disciplined.

The S12 Pro's treble is brighter in a less controlled way. On well-recorded tracks it adds excitement. On poorly recorded or compressed tracks — streaming-quality EDM, some K-pop production — it can become sharp and fatiguing. The S12 Pro asks you to curate your listening more carefully.

**Winner for control**: AE. **Winner for sheer top-end energy**: S12 Pro (if that's what you want).

### Technicalities: imaging, detail, soundstage

The AE is the clear winner. Imaging is more precise — instruments sit in more defined positions in the soundstage, and the left-right layering is more coherent. Detail retrieval is better: micro-textures in recordings are more audible, and the AE resolves complex passages with less congestion.

The S12 Pro is not a poor performer technically — it is a strong technical IEM for its price. But the AE is at a higher tier. The 15% refinement gap that the $59 price difference represents is mostly here.

**Winner**: 7Hz Timeless AE.

## Source pairing

The AE's technical character means it **scales with source quality**. Running it off a high-quality balanced dongle (4.4mm) — something like a Fosi DS2 or Qudelix 5K — is audibly better than a basic 3.5mm smartphone output. If you already own or plan to own a decent DAC/amp, the AE rewards the investment.

The S12 Pro is meaningfully more forgiving. It sounds engaging off a basic USB-C dongle, a laptop headphone jack, or a mid-tier smartphone. If your source chain is whatever is already in your pocket, the S12 Pro meets you there. The AE does not.

Both IEMs are easy to drive by planar standards — neither needs a desktop amp. But if you want to get the most out of the AE, **budget for a proper dongle DAC** if you do not already own one.

The AE also includes a modular cable with both 3.5mm and 4.4mm terminations in the box, which is a meaningful accessories advantage — balanced output ready out of the package.

## Build & fit

Both are metal-shell IEMs in the same general form factor. The AE has a titanium-coated shell with a magnetic faceplate — it feels premium, sits firmly, and looks the part. The S12 Pro has a lightweight metal shell that is comfortable for long sessions without the same premium feel.

Neither shell raises ergonomic red flags. Both should fit a wide range of ear shapes comfortably.

The AE's modular cable (included) is a genuine build advantage over the S12 Pro's standard cable — you get balanced output without a separate purchase.

## Who should buy the 7Hz Timeless AE

- You want **the best technical planar under $250** — imaging, resolution, distortion
- You value **neutral-accurate tuning** over colored or fun signatures
- You listen to **acoustic music, jazz, classical, or vocals** where midrange accuracy matters
- You already own or plan to buy a **decent dongle DAC or source**
- You want balanced output **out of the box** (modular cable included)
- The $59 premium over the S12 Pro is within budget

## Who should buy the Letshuoer S12 Pro

- You want **fun, energetic V-shaped sound** — elevated bass slam, bright top end
- You listen to **electronic, pop, hip-hop, or rock** that benefits from extra bass presence
- Your source is a basic dongle, smartphone, or anything mid-tier — the S12 Pro is **source-forgiving**
- You want to spend **$160 instead of $219** and put the $59 toward tips, cable, or another purchase
- You find purely neutral signatures "boring" and want the sound to have personality on first listen

## Honest recommendation

The $59 gap buys real improvement — but only in specific areas. The AE's technical advantages are genuine, and for neutral-accurate listeners or those investing in a quality source chain, **the AE is worth every cent of the premium**.

But the S12 Pro is not a compromise pick. It is a very good planar IEM at $160 with a tuning character that a large portion of listeners actively prefer. If you know you like V-shaped signatures and your source chain is nothing special, the S12 Pro delivers exactly what it promises at a price that is hard to argue with.

**AE for technical best-in-class. S12 Pro for fun at a fair price.** Both are easy recommendations — the question is what you are optimizing for.
