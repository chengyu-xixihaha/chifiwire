The [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) at $79 has been **the default sub-$100 audiophile IEM recommendation** for nearly three years. The [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) at $150 is the bigger sibling — same brand, more drivers, refined Harman tuning. Is it worth 2× the price?

**Short version**: the Nova **is** measurably and audibly better. But the Hexa already does a lot right. Whether the upgrade makes sense depends on what you already have, what you listen to, and whether you're a "diminishing returns" believer.

## Sound — by frequency

### Sub-bass

**Tie, edge to Nova on extension.** Both deliver clean sub-bass extension to 25-30 Hz. Nova has slightly more authority below 40 Hz; Hexa is tucked but tight.

For electronic / hip-hop, Nova feels more physical. For most other music, the difference is small.

### Mid-bass

**Both restrained — Nova marginally more present.** Neither IEM has the bass-bloom problem that plagues cheap hybrids. Hexa's mid-bass is conservative (which some call "tucked"); Nova adds a touch more warmth without crossing into "boomy" territory.

### Lower mids

**Nova wins.** This is where the extra BA pays off. Cello, low piano, electric bass — all sound richer and more present on Nova. Hexa's lower mids feel slightly thin by comparison.

This matters if you listen to **classical** or **acoustic music with low strings**.

### Mids / vocals

**Different character.**
- **Hexa**: vocals sit half a step back, instruments compete equally — analytical staging
- **Nova**: vocals are more present, slightly forward — more "musical" presentation

For vocal-led music (pop, R&B, indie rock), Nova is more engaging. For complex arrangements where you want to hear individual instruments separately, Hexa's stage gives you better separation.

### Upper mids / lower treble

**Nova smoother, Hexa more articulated.** Both have real treble extension. Hexa's BAs are slightly more "lit up" in the 5-8 kHz range — you hear every breath, every cymbal stick. Nova tames this region without losing detail.

For **treble-sensitive listeners**, Nova is significantly more forgiving. For analytical listening, Hexa's edge is preferred.

### Upper treble / air

**Both extend well.** Nova has slightly more air; Hexa has slightly more sparkle.

### Soundstage and imaging

- **Width**: Nova marginally wider
- **Depth**: Nova clearly better (more front-to-back separation)
- **Imaging precision**: Tie

Nova's stage is "more 3D"; Hexa's is wider but flatter.

### Speed and transients

**Both are fast.** No meaningful difference for most music. Edge cases (death metal blast beats, complex orchestral) — both handle competently.

### Coherence

**Hexa wins slightly here.** With 3 BAs to manage, the crossover is cleaner. Nova's 4 BAs introduce a touch more "multi-driver" character, but it's only audible side-by-side.

## Source requirements

Both work fine from phones (high sensitivity, low impedance). Both scale up with dongle DACs and DAPs.

Realistic comparison: pair both with a **[Fosi DS2 dongle]({{< ref "guides/best-dongle-dacs-2026" >}})** ($40) for the truest evaluation. Without a real source, the differences narrow.

## Build, fit, accessories

- **Shells**: Both resin + metal nozzle. Nova slightly more refined finish.
- **Comfort**: Tie. Both have moderate-sized shells. Small-ear users — see [small ears guide]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-iems-for-small-ears" >}}).
- **Cable**: Nova ships with a noticeably better cable. Hexa cable works but feels generic.
- **Tips**: Both come with standard silicone selection. Tip rolling helps both.
- **Case**: Nova includes a soft case; Hexa includes a basic pouch.

## When the upgrade is worth it

### Get the **Nova** if:
- You **already have** a dongle DAC or DAP (so you can hear the difference)
- You listen to **vocal-led music** primarily
- You're treble-sensitive (Nova is more forgiving)
- You want **the safer "I want one IEM" pick** at $150
- You can spend the extra $71 without thinking about it

### Get the **Hexa** if:
- You're **first-time audiophile** — Hexa already delivers 80% of Nova performance
- You're **phone-only** — the difference shrinks without a source
- You listen to **analytical / detail-focused music**
- You'd rather save $71 for a **[dongle DAC]({{< ref "guides/best-dongle-dacs-2026" >}})** + tips
- You're budget-conscious

### Get **both** if:
- You're a collector
- You want to A/B compare topology variations (same brand, scaled-up driver count)

## The honest answer

For **80% of people**, the Hexa is sufficient. For analytical listeners and detail-focused use cases, it's actually preferred over the Nova. The upgrade is real but on **diminishing returns**.

If your budget is $150 total, **buy the Hexa + a [Fosi DS2 dongle DAC]({{< ref "guides/best-dongle-dacs-2026" >}})** ($79 + $40 = $119). You'll get more sonic improvement than the Hexa→Nova jump alone.

If your budget is $200+ and you already have a source: Nova makes sense.

## The "smart" upgrade path

1. Start with [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}) ($79) or [Pure]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-pure-review" >}}) ($90) depending on taste
2. Add a [dongle DAC]({{< ref "guides/best-dongle-dacs-2026" >}}) (Fosi DS2 $40, or FiiO KA17 $90)
3. THEN consider Nova or planar (S12 Pro)

That's the value-maximized path. Skipping straight to Nova because "it's the better IEM" misses the bigger picture.
