At exactly $149.99, the [Moondrop VOID]({{< ref "reviews/moondrop-void-review" >}}) and the [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) occupy the same shelf, target the same buyer, and do almost everything differently. One is a single dynamic driver in a full metal shell with swappable tuning nozzles and a balanced cable in the box. The other is a 1DD + 4BA tribrid chasing the Harman target with better resolution and a wider stage.

Same price. Opposite philosophy. This is the cleanest head-to-head at the $150 mark.

## Specs side by side

| | **Moondrop VOID** | **Truthear Nova** |
|---|---|---|
| **Price** | $149.99 | $149.99 |
| **Driver config** | 1 × 10mm LCP dynamic driver | 1DD + 4BA tribrid |
| **Tuning target** | VDSF neutral-warm | Harman IE 2019 V2 |
| **Shell** | Full metal | Acrylic |
| **Cable included** | Modular (3.5mm + 4.4mm) | 3.5mm SE only |
| **Nozzle tuning** | Two swappable nozzles | Fixed |

The cable situation is a real differentiator at this price. The VOID ships with both terminations ready to go; to run the Nova balanced, you are buying an aftermarket cable.

## Sound comparison

### Bass

**Edge to VOID for texture; edge to Nova for linearity.**

The VOID's single DD delivers mid-bass with warmth and body — there is a slight elevation that makes kick drums and bass guitar feel physical and immediate. It suits rock, R&B, and vocal music where a little low-end weight feels natural. The sub-bass rolls off gently rather than slamming.

The Nova's bass is more linear and controlled. The dedicated DD for low-end keeps things clean — sub-bass extension is better, and there is less mid-bass bloat. On electronic music or anything with deep rumble, the Nova tracks lower and tighter.

**Verdict**: VOID for bass feel and texture; Nova for technical bass accuracy and extension.

### Mids

**VOID wins here, and it is not subtle.**

This is the VOID's defining strength. A single driver with no crossover means the midrange is seamless — vocals, acoustic guitar, piano, strings all have a naturalness and tonal rightness that multi-driver designs struggle to match. There is no handoff between drivers, no BA timbre coloring the upper mids. The VOID's midrange just sounds like music.

The Nova's midrange is detailed and correctly placed — this is not a poorly tuned midrange by any measure. But it can carry a faint BA character in the upper mids that trained listeners will notice. The resolution is there; the organic quality is not, at least not to the degree the VOID achieves it.

If you listen to vocals, acoustic genres, jazz, or classical music, the VOID's midrange coherence is the strongest argument for buying it over anything in the $150 category — not just the Nova.

**Verdict**: VOID, clearly. Single-driver coherence is a genuine advantage.

### Treble

**Nova extends further; VOID is more forgiving.**

The Nova reaches further into the upper registers, with more cymbal shimmer and air above 12kHz. That extension is a real asset on well-recorded material — there is a sense of openness and sparkle that the VOID does not fully match.

The VOID's treble is smoother and more forgiving. The VDSF curve keeps peaks controlled; sibilance is minimal, and harshness on brighter recordings is a non-issue. This is the safer pair for longer listening sessions and less-than-perfect masters.

Treble-sensitive listeners should go to the VOID without hesitation. Listeners who want air and detail retrieval at the top end should note that the Nova delivers it.

**Verdict**: Nova for extension and sparkle; VOID for forgiveness and fatigue-free long sessions.

### Soundstage and imaging

**Nova wins the technical categories.**

The Nova's stage is wider and more precisely imaged. Instruments have defined positions; front-to-back depth is present and audible on well-recorded orchestral and jazz material. For a $150 IEM, the imaging is a genuine strength.

The VOID presents a more intimate and centered stage — not claustrophobic, but not wide either. Where it compensates is in how coherent the image feels. Nothing smears or loses positional integrity because there is only one driver managing the full range.

**Verdict**: Nova for staging width and imaging precision; VOID for coherence within a smaller but seamless presentation.

## Build comparison

The VOID has a meaningful build advantage. Full metal construction feels premium in hand, and the two swappable nozzles add real versatility — the alternate tuning nozzle shifts the sound signature enough to meaningfully alter how you hear it with different music. Combined with the included balanced cable, the VOID gives you more ways to configure it out of the box.

The Nova's acrylic shell is lightweight and comfortable, and the fit is accommodating for most ear sizes. It does not feel cheap, but it does not feel like a $150 purchase in the same way the VOID does. No balanced cable means an additional spend if you plan to use balanced output — which you probably should at this price.

**Verdict**: VOID on build quality, cable inclusion, and hardware flexibility.

## Who should buy which

**Buy the Moondrop VOID if:**

- Vocals, acoustic music, jazz, or classical music make up a significant portion of your listening
- Natural timbre and single-driver coherence matter more to you than raw technical performance
- You want balanced output capability without buying a second cable
- You prefer a forgiving, fatigue-free sound over analytical detail retrieval
- You value premium build quality and want IEMs that feel like the money you spent

**Buy the Truthear Nova if:**

- You genre-hop and want a technically capable, neutral reference IEM that holds up across everything
- Detail retrieval, soundstage width, and imaging precision are priorities
- You are coming from budget IEMs and want a measurable technical step up
- You want the Harman target done well at $150
- You will be using these for critical listening or as an analytical reference

## Final verdict

The VOID and Nova split the $150 category in two. They are not "better" and "worse" — they are built for different priorities, and neither is a wrong answer.

The **VOID** is the pick if music listening is what you care about — if naturalness, timbre, and coherence matter more than resolution numbers and stage width. The single-DD presentation is something the Nova cannot replicate regardless of driver count, and the included balanced cable is a real-world advantage that saves money and hassle.

The **Nova** is the pick if you want the most technically capable IEM at this price and you are willing to run 3.5mm or buy a cable. It resolves more, stages wider, and is a more versatile reference tool.

Same price. Two very different answers to the same question.
