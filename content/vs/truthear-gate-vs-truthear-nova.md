Truthear has built a reputation for making the price-to-performance argument uncomfortable for competitors. The Gate at $22 and the Nova at $79 are both single dynamic driver IEMs from the same team — but they're aimed at very different buyers. The question isn't whether the Nova is better (it is), but whether the difference justifies spending 3.6x more.

## Quick Specs

| | Truthear Gate | Truthear Nova |
|---|---|---|
| Price | ~$22 | ~$79 |
| Driver | 10mm dynamic | 10mm + 6mm dual DD |
| Impedance | 16Ω | 32Ω |
| Sensitivity | 108dB/mW | 102dB/mW |
| Cable | 0.78mm 2-pin | 0.78mm 2-pin |

One correction: the Nova actually uses a dual dynamic driver configuration — a 10mm woofer and a 6mm tweeter — rather than a single DD. That distinction matters for the comparison.

## Sound: Where They Differ

**Bass.** The Nova has more controlled sub-bass extension and a tighter punch. The Gate has slightly elevated mid-bass that makes it feel warmer and fuller on casual listening — some find this more engaging, others find it adds muddiness on complex tracks. For bass-heavy genres, the Gate's coloration works in its favor. For acoustic music, the Nova's cleaner low end wins.

**Midrange.** This is where the price gap is most audible. The Nova's dual-driver crossover allows it to handle the critical 1kHz–5kHz region with more resolution. Vocals have more texture, and instruments in the upper midrange — acoustic guitars, piano, strings — are rendered with better separation. The Gate's midrange is competent but sounds somewhat flattened by comparison on complex arrangements.

**Treble.** The Gate has a slightly elevated lower treble that can read as "detail" on first listen but becomes fatiguing over long sessions. The Nova is smoother through the upper octaves without being rolled-off — a more mature tuning. If you're treble-sensitive, the Nova is the safer pick.

**Soundstage.** The Nova has a wider and deeper image. The Gate is average for its class — a reasonable head-width stage, not particularly deep. Neither IEM images exceptionally well, but the Nova does noticeably better.

## Build and Fit

Both use similar shell geometries, but the Nova's shell is slightly larger and heavier. Most users with average to large ears will find the Nova more stable during movement; smaller-eared users may actually prefer the Gate's compact fit.

The cable situation is identical: both ship with a 0.78mm 2-pin cable that is functional but not particularly premium-feeling. Upgrading the cable on either is straightforward with the standard connector.

The Gate's cable comes with an inline microphone on the included version — useful for voice calls. The Nova does not.

## Who Should Buy Which

**Buy the Gate if:**
- You're new to IEMs and want to understand what good sounds like at minimal cost
- You want a daily commute pair you won't be devastated to lose
- You need a mic for calls
- You're buying a backup or gym pair

**Buy the Nova if:**
- You're using these as your primary IEM for serious listening
- You notice midrange texture and imaging in your regular listening
- You frequently listen to acoustic music, vocals, or complex orchestral arrangements
- You've heard the Gate and want to know what "more" sounds like

## The Honest Answer

If you've never spent more than $30 on IEMs, the Gate will genuinely impress you and there's no need to go straight to the Nova. If you've already been around the block with Chi-Fi and you're spending $79, the Nova delivers a meaningfully better result that holds up against $100-$120 competitors from Moondrop and Simgot.

The gap between them is real. Whether it's worth the price difference depends entirely on how critically you listen and how much you care about midrange resolution.
