If you've decided your next IEM is going to be a planar, two names dominate the 2026 shortlist: the **Kiwi Ears Aether** at around $169 and the **Letshuoer S12 Pro** at around $110. Both ditch the dynamic and hybrid crowd in favor of a single planar-magnetic diaphragm, the technology prized for its speed, low distortion, and clean transients. But they arrive at the format from opposite directions — and that $60 gap buys you a genuinely different listening experience, not just "more of the same."

The Aether is the ambitious newcomer, built around a 15.3mm planar driver that's reportedly one of the largest ever fitted into an in-ear monitor, tuned to be smooth and spacious enough to win over people who've been burned by harsh budget planars. The S12 Pro is the established benchmark — a refined take on the IEM that put affordable planars on the map back in 2022, now widely called the measurement reference for mid-range planar performance. We've reviewed both in full, so this is the head-to-head: which one deserves your money, and why.

## Short version / TL;DR

- **Buy the Kiwi Ears Aether** if you want a smooth, spacious, fatigue-free planar with headphone-like bass authority and the widest soundstage in the class — and you've been let down by harsh planars before.
- **Buy the Letshuoer S12 Pro** if you want maximum technical detail, speed, and deep V-shaped sub-bass for the least money, you have a real source, and you don't mind some treble bite.
- **Both need power** to sound their best — neither is a phone-only IEM. Budget a dongle DAC/amp either way.
- **The price gap is real:** the S12 Pro is the value champion at ~$110; the Aether asks ~$169 for a smoother tuning, a bigger driver, and a more refined presentation.

Two takeaways jump out of the table. The Aether has the bigger driver and a smoother tuning on paper; the S12 Pro counters with a fully **modular cable** — three plugs in the box, including 4.4mm balanced — at two-thirds the price.

## Sound

**Bass.** Both planars move serious air, but they spend it differently. The Aether's 15.3mm driver delivers what reviewers call "headphone-like bass authority" — strong sub-bass extension and a punchy, textured mid-bass that stays clean and controlled rather than meaty. The S12 Pro goes harder on sub-bass specifically: its V-shaped tuning pushes deep, rumbling, textured low-end (cue up "Strobe" or "Limit to Your Love") while keeping mid-bass tight. If you want slam and rumble for the money, the S12 Pro; if you want bass that's authoritative but never the headline, the Aether.

**Mids.** This is the clearest split. The Aether's midrange is smooth, natural, and largely free of aggression — vocals are present and unforced, with only a slight lower-midrange recession that can occasionally sound a touch thin. The S12 Pro's mids are **recessed by design**: clear but set back, with female vocals lacking some air and rock guitars sitting slightly distant. If you live in the mids — vocals, jazz, folk, chamber music — the Aether is the easy pick.

**Treble.** The Aether is the gentler of the two: smooth, refined, with good extension and only minor sibilance on the odd snare or cymbal hit at volume. The S12 Pro is brighter and more detailed up top — hi-hats glisten and micro-detail is exceptional — but it carries a presence-region peak around 7–8 kHz that can turn sharp on harsh recordings. Treble-sensitive listeners will prefer the Aether; detail-chasers who'll EQ will be happy with the S12 Pro.

**Soundstage & imaging.** Both image well, but the Aether owns this category. Its soundstage is unusually wide with real front-to-back depth and layering that most IEMs in the class can't match. The S12 Pro is no slouch — wide, deep, and better-imaging than most sub-$200 rivals thanks to those fast planar transients — but the Aether's sense of space is its signature trait.

## Driveability & source pairing

Here's the rule that applies to **both**: planars need power, and neither of these is a "plug into a phone and call it done" IEM. The Aether (14 Ω, 105 dB/mW) isn't brutally hard to drive and will play loud off a phone, but you'll lose sub-bass depth and texture without more juice. The S12 Pro is the more demanding of the two — at 102 dB/mW its sensitivity is on the low side, and under-driven it sounds thin and flat. In fact, the single biggest reason people return the S12 Pro is trying it on a phone, being unimpressed, and refunding before it ever got proper power.

The fix is cheap. A decent USB-C dongle DAC/amp in the $20–$50 range unlocks the Aether; the S12 Pro really wants ~150 mW+ and rewards a balanced output — and conveniently, its modular cable includes a 4.4mm plug, so you can run balanced out of the box with no extra purchase. The Aether ships single-ended only.

One more way to read the price gap: the S12 Pro costs ~$110, but if you don't already own a capable source, factor a dongle into the total for either IEM. The Aether's higher asking price partly buys you a tuning that's more forgiving of a modest source, where the S12 Pro is less so.

## Build & comfort & accessories

The two go about build differently. The Aether uses smooth, rounded **medical-grade resin** with no sharp edges — comfortable for long sessions, but noticeably **larger than average** (a direct result of housing that 15.3mm driver), with a wide ~6mm nozzle that can be fiddly to seal. Smaller ears should demo first. Isolation is only average, since the ventilation that keeps it comfortable also lets outside noise in.

The S12 Pro takes the metal route: a **CNC-machined aluminum shell** with a sandblasted matte finish and 5-axis ergonomics that genuinely match the ear. It's compact, light, and the build feels above its price.

On accessories, the Aether wins on tip variety — nine pairs plus a leather case — but its non-modular 4-core copper cable is the weak link in an otherwise premium package. The S12 Pro's headline accessory is that **modular cable** (2.5mm + 3.5mm + 4.4mm plugs, all included), letting you A/B single-ended versus balanced in seconds, alongside three tip sets and a case. For source flexibility, the S12 Pro's bundle is hard to beat at the price.

## Who should buy which

### Buy the Aether if

- You've tried a budget planar before and found it harsh, metallic, or thin — the Aether is built to change that impression.
- You want the widest, most layered soundstage in the class and bass with genuine authority.
- You do long sessions and prioritize a smooth, fatigue-free top end.
- You value smooth, natural mids and don't want a scooped, V-shaped presentation.
- You're willing to add a modest dongle but want a planar that's forgiving of a modest source.

### Buy the S12 Pro if

- You want maximum technical detail, speed, and separation for the least money — it's the value benchmark.
- You like a V-shape with deep, rumbling sub-bass and bright, sparkly treble.
- You have a real source (DAP or dongle DAC) and ideally a balanced output to use that 4.4mm plug.
- You want a compact aluminum shell and a fully modular cable in the box.
- You're saving ~$60 versus the Aether and would rather put that toward a better source.

## Verdict

These are two of the best budget planars of 2026, and the right answer depends on what you're optimizing for. The **Letshuoer S12 Pro** is the value and technicality champion — at ~$110 with a modular cable, deep sub-bass, and reference-flat detail, it remains the benchmark, provided you feed it power and tolerate a V-shape with recessed mids and a treble peak. The **Kiwi Ears Aether** is the more refined, more grown-up listen: smoother mids, a gentler top end, headphone-like bass authority, and the widest soundstage here — for which you pay roughly $60 more and accept a larger shell and a fiddlier seal.

Put simply: if you're chasing the most performance per dollar and you have a proper source, the S12 Pro wins. If you want the smoothest, most spacious, most fatigue-free planar and the budget allows, the Aether is worth the premium. Neither is a wrong answer — they're just tuned for different listeners.
