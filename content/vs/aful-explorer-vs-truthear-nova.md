Two 1DD+4BA hybrids. $40 apart. Both ChiFi's current answer to "what should I buy around $100-150?" But they sound nothing alike — and the right answer depends entirely on what you're optimizing for.

The [AFUL Explorer]({{< ref "reviews/aful-explorer-review" >}}) ($109) is the analytical pick: treble-forward, resolving, and a little clinical. The [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) ($149) chases Harman IE 2019 V2 accuracy — sub-bass presence, natural mids, smooth top end. Knowing which camp you're in makes this decision straightforward.

## Specs at a glance

| | **AFUL Explorer** | **Truthear Nova** |
|---|---|---|
| **Price** | $109 | $149 |
| **Driver config** | 1 DD + 4 BA hybrid | 1 DD + 4 BA hybrid |
| **Tuning target** | Neutral-bright, analytical | Harman IE 2019 V2 |
| **Shell** | Metal faceplate + resin | Full resin/acrylic |
| **Impedance** | 22 Ω | 15 Ω |
| **Sensitivity** | 109 dB/mW | 124 dB/Vrms |
| **Cable** | 0.78mm 2-pin | 0.78mm 2-pin |

Same driver count, fundamentally different implementation and philosophy.

## Build and fit

The Explorer has metal faceplates and a noticeably premium in-hand feel for the price. If you care about the unboxing moment or desk aesthetics, AFUL wins this one. The Nova uses an acrylic shell — perfectly fine, durable, and well-finished, but it doesn't carry the same gravitas. AFUL's metal construction at $109 is genuinely impressive.

Fit is comparable between the two. Both use standard 0.78mm 2-pin cables with serviceable stock options. Tip rolling will help both — the Explorer particularly benefits from wider-bore tips to bring down some of the treble energy if needed.

## Sound comparison

### Bass

Nova edges ahead here, by design. Its Harman tuning includes a meaningful sub-bass boost: you get better rumble and weight on electronic, hip-hop, and anything with a lot of low-end texture. The Explorer's bass is tighter and faster — better transient control — but sits lower in overall level. Neither one is bass-heavy by any measure, but if you want your bass to be *present*, Nova delivers it more readily.

Edge: **Nova** (more sub-bass presence); **Explorer** for pure tightness and speed.

### Mids

This is where the biggest audible gap sits, and it's Nova's clearest win.

The Nova's midrange is more coherent, more natural, and more pleasant on vocals. Male and female vocals both have realistic timbre — instruments sound like instruments. The Harman target handles the upper midrange carefully, preventing harshness without dulling the presence region.

The Explorer's upper mids are elevated and can tip into "forward" territory on certain recordings. Analytical listeners will appreciate the additional clarity and micro-detail. But on strings, piano, and acoustic guitar, the Nova simply sounds more like the real thing. If you listen to a lot of vocal music — pop, jazz, indie, singer-songwriter — this will be the difference you notice most.

Edge: **Nova**, clearly. Better vocal timbre and midrange coherence. Not close.

### Treble

Explorer is the brighter, more energetic IEM in the high frequencies. Cymbal detail, air, and upper-frequency sparkle are all more present. If you like the sensation of space and shimmer in your music, the Explorer's treble character is genuinely fun and engaging.

The Nova is smoother and more forgiving. Sibilance is better controlled. Poorly mastered tracks are easier to sit with. You give up some sparkle but gain long-term listenability.

Edge: **Explorer** for detail and air; **Nova** for smooth, fatigue-free treble.

### Soundstage and imaging

Soundstage width is roughly equivalent — both present a moderately wide field without being exceptional. Nova has a clearer sense of depth, giving orchestral and live recordings more convincing layering. Explorer's imaging is precise and surgical, which suits its analytical character well.

Edge: slight **Nova** on depth; slight **Explorer** on imaging precision.

### Technical performance

At this price tier, both perform well and neither will leave you feeling underserved. The Explorer has a narrow but real edge in treble detail retrieval — it genuinely resolves more upper-frequency information. The Nova has better driver coherence across the DD/BA crossover region; the transition sounds more seamless, which contributes to that more natural midrange character.

If you're measuring on a rig, the Explorer might show more detail. If you're just listening, the Nova sounds more like a unified IEM rather than a multi-driver stack.

Edge: **Explorer** (treble detail); **Nova** (driver coherence).

## Who should buy the Explorer

The Explorer at $109 is the better buy if:

- You're chasing detail and resolution — it's genuinely excellent at separating and layering complex passages
- You listen to classical, metal, or anything where treble energy and instrument separation are priorities
- You're treble-sensitive in the other direction (you find Harman's sub-bass bump sounds bloated, or you want more air in your music)
- You're saving the $40 price difference for tips, a cable, or putting toward another IEM down the line
- You prefer an analytical "tools for listening" approach over a colored, musical presentation

## Who should buy the Nova

Spend the extra $40 on the Nova if:

- Vocals matter to you — pop, jazz, acoustic, singer-songwriter, R&B. This is the clearest use-case win.
- You want Harman-accurate tuning for a reference baseline that translates well across sources
- You're treble-sensitive and need a smoother top end that doesn't fatigue on long sessions
- You care about midrange coherence and want instruments to sound natural rather than anatomized
- You're less interested in "maximum detail" and more interested in "sounds right"

## The $40 question

The Nova costs $40 more — about 37% over the Explorer. That's not nothing. But it's not a trivial difference in sound either.

The Explorer isn't a worse IEM in absolute terms; it's a different tool. But for most listeners, the Nova's midrange naturalness and Harman tuning represent a more immediately satisfying and broadly useful sound signature. The Explorer's analytical character is genuinely valuable if that's your preference, but it's the more specific taste — not the safer default.

Default recommendation: **Nova**, unless you know you're the analytical-treble listener.
