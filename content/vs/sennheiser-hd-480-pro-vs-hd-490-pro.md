Sennheiser built two studio headphones on the same 38mm dynamic driver chassis and then sealed them differently. The **HD 480 Pro** is the closed-back one — a $399 tracking and monitoring can with a low-frequency chamber that pressurizes the cup. The **HD 490 Pro** is the open-back sibling — also around $399, but with metal mesh grilles, a wider stage, and two swappable ear-pad sets aimed at producers and mixers. Same neodymium driver, same 130-ohm impedance, same lightweight frame language. The hardware is closer than any spec sheet makes it look.

So this comparison isn't really about which headphone "sounds better." It's about a single decision you've probably already half-made before you got here: **closed-back or open-back.** That one choice cascades into everything — isolation versus soundstage, tracking versus mixing reference, a vocal booth versus a treated room. Both cans cost the same. Both are excellent at the job they were built for and compromised at the other. Pick the wrong sealing for your room and no amount of tuning will save it. Let's make the call properly.

## Short version / TL;DR

- **Buy the HD 480 Pro (closed-back)** if you track vocals or live takes, work in an untreated or shared room, need isolation, or want one headphone that does tracking *and* casual reference.
- **Buy the HD 490 Pro (open-back)** if you mix in a quiet, treated room, want the widest soundstage Sennheiser ships at this price, and value the two-pad system that lets you tune for producing or mixing.
- **They share a driver and impedance** (38mm dynamic, 130 ohm), so source requirements are nearly identical — both want a real interface or amp, not a phone jack.
- **Headphones.com actually preferred the closed 480 Pro's bass and treble smoothness** to the open 490 Pro — an unusual upset, and worth knowing if you were assuming open-back automatically wins.
- **Only buy the 490 Pro for the right room.** Open-back leaks; if a mic can hear it or a roommate can complain, that's the 480 Pro's lane.

## Closed-back vs open-back: what it means for you

This is the whole comparison, so spend a minute here before you read about treble.

A **closed-back** headphone (the 480 Pro) seals the back of the driver. That seal does two things: it keeps outside noise out and your audio in (isolation), and it lets the cup build up air pressure, which is why closed-backs can deliver bass with weight and authority. The cost is soundstage — sealed cups bounce sound around inside, so the stereo image tends to feel like it's happening *inside your head* rather than out in a room. For **tracking** — recording a singer, capturing a live take, anyone wearing the cans next to a hot microphone — that seal is non-negotiable. An open-back leaks monitor audio straight back into the mic.

An **open-back** headphone (the 490 Pro) has a vented, mesh-grille rear. Air moves freely through the driver, which kills the internal reflections and produces a wider, more natural, more out-of-head soundstage — the single biggest reason engineers reach for open-backs when **mixing**. You hear where instruments sit in the stereo field more like you would on monitors. The cost is total: zero isolation. The headphone leaks sound out, lets the room in, and is useless next to a live mic or on a noisy train. RTINGS frames the 490 Pro as the more comfortable, more flexible studio tool *for a quiet room* — and that qualifier is the entire point.

So: if your work involves a microphone or a shared space, the 480 Pro's seal decides it. If your work is mixing in a room you control, the 490 Pro's openness is the upgrade. The driver underneath is the same.

## Sound

Because the platform is shared, the **family resemblance is strong** — both lean neutral with a touch of warmth, both have a smooth-ish presentation, neither is a bass cannon or a treble spike machine. The differences come from the sealing and, on the 490, from which pads you fit.

**Bass.** Here's the surprise. The closed 480 Pro's sealed low-frequency chamber gives it tight, weighty sub-bass that extends past 30 Hz without the usual closed-back mid-bass hump — and **Headphones.com explicitly preferred the 480 Pro's bass to the open 490 Pro's.** The 490 Pro's bass depends entirely on pads: SoundGuys notes the velour "producing" pads boost everything below 500 Hz by a couple of decibels for a warmer feel, while the fabric "mixing" pads are flatter and more linear. The Headphones.com reviewer found the producer-pad bass "thin" and "tubby" and far preferred the mixing pads' tighter integration. So out of the box, the closed can arguably has the more confident low end.

**Mids.** Both share a slightly warm midrange. The 490 Pro's mixing pads, per Headphones.com, deliver "my favorite midrange presentation from Sennheiser behind the HD 600 and HD 650" — high praise — while the producer pads recess the upper mids and sound warmer/duller. The 480 Pro's mids sit forward and pleasant, which is ideal for tracking vocals but less ruthless for mix reference.

**Treble.** The 480 Pro's treble was the star of its review — smooth, fatigue-free, no harsh 6–8 kHz peak. The 490 Pro is more contentious: SoundGuys measured a 4–7 kHz lift, and Headphones.com found both pad sets a touch "crunchy/glarey" up top, describing "papery snare drums." If long-session smoothness matters most, the closed 480 Pro is the gentler listen.

**Soundstage.** This is the 490 Pro's home turf. Open-back venting gives it the wider, more out-of-head image that makes panning and depth decisions easier — Sweetwater calls it "extremely wide, dimensional." The closed 480 Pro images precisely but stays bounded inside your head, as all sealed cans do. For mixing, stage is the 490 Pro's single biggest advantage.

**On the swappable pads:** treat them as two voicings of one headphone. Velour/producing = warmer, comfier, more soundstage-flavored; fabric/mixing = flatter, cooler, more neutral. Most reviewers land on the mixing pads for serious work. Note that swapping takes real force against the plastic latches.

## Build, comfort & accessories

The two share a chassis, so build and comfort are close. Both are featherweights — 272 g for the 480 Pro, 260 g for the 490 Pro — light enough for all-day sessions, with replaceable pads and detachable cables. Both have deep-enough pad channels that reviewers singled out for glasses-wearers.

The **480 Pro's** standout build detail is a microphonic-damping spring at the earcup cable entry that kills cable-rub thump — genuinely useful when you're tracking and wearing the cans live. The **490 Pro's** standout is the box contents: two full pad sets, a 1.8m cable with both 3.5mm and 6.3mm connectors, an adapter, and (per SoundGuys) a bundled dearVR MIX-SE plugin. SoundGuys did flag the 490 Pro's ear cups as slightly shallow for some, and Headphones.com noted there's no suspension strap. The 480 Pro's sealed pads run warmer over long sessions; the 490 Pro's open design stays cooler on the ears.

## Driveability

This is the one place there's almost no daylight between them: both are **130-ohm** dynamics. That impedance is the awkward middle — too high to drive cleanly from a phone, too low to demand a flagship desktop amp. The intended source for both is an audio interface (Focusrite Scarlett, UA Volt, MOTU M2) or any modest headphone amp, which drives them comfortably. Desktop stacks are overkill but fine; portable players work with 6–10 dB less headroom. If you already power the 480 Pro adequately, the 490 Pro needs nothing different.

## Who should buy which

### Buy the HD 480 Pro if

- You **track vocals, instruments, or live takes** and need isolation next to a microphone
- You work in an **untreated or shared room** where open-back leakage is a problem
- You want **one headphone** for both tracking and casual reference listening
- You prioritize **smooth, fatigue-free treble** and confident, weighty closed-back bass
- You need to block out a roommate, an office, or a commute

### Buy the HD 490 Pro if

- You **mix and master in a quiet, treated room** and never need isolation
- **Soundstage width and out-of-head imaging** are central to how you make decisions
- You want the **two-pad system** to tune between a warmer producing voice and a flatter mixing reference
- You value the bundled accessories (extra pads, dearVR MIX-SE plugin)
- Open-back airiness and cooler long-session comfort matter to you

## Verdict

There's no winner here — there's a room and a workflow. The **HD 480 Pro** is the better tracking and general-purpose tool: it isolates, its sealed bass is tighter (Headphones.com preferred it outright), its treble is smoother for long sessions, and it doubles as a do-everything can. The **HD 490 Pro** is the better dedicated mixing reference *if* your room earns it — wider stage, two-pad flexibility, and a mixing-pad midrange one reviewer ranked just behind the legendary HD 600/650.

If you can only own one and you ever sit near a microphone or share a wall, buy the closed-back 480 Pro — it's the more versatile $399. If you have a quiet, treated space and you mix more than you record, the open-back 490 Pro's soundstage is the reason it exists, and it's worth the trade. Same driver, same price, opposite jobs. Choose the seal, not the spec sheet.
