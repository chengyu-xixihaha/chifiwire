Truthear has had one of the fastest rises in ChiFi. The brand launched in 2022, partnered with Crinacle to tune the Hexa, and within a year became the default recommendation at every price point they touched. By 2026 they have five IEMs on the market — spanning $55 to $149 — and at least two of them are on nearly every "best of" shortlist. The brand's consistency is rare: no obvious dud in the lineup, no cynical repackage, and pricing that's honest about what you're actually getting.

This guide covers all five Truthear IEMs. The goal is to save you from buying the wrong one, or from buying two when one would do.

If you're overwhelmed, skip to the Nova. If $149 is too much, read the Gate vs Hexa section. If you want bass without spending more than $60, the Zero:Red is a clear call.

## Truthear Zero:Red — $55

{{< ref "reviews/truthear-zero-red-review" >}}

The Zero:Red is the entry-level IEM Truthear designed specifically for listeners who want impact. It's a single dynamic driver with a bass shelf that's noticeable from the first track — elevated sub-bass, a slightly recessed midrange, a safe treble that doesn't bite. Compared to the original Zero (white), the Red trades some neutrality for fun, and the trade is worth it if bass is what you're after. Technicalities are limited at this price, but the low-end texture is better than you'd expect for $55.

This one is for the listener who knows they want warmth and punch, doesn't care about flat reference tuning, and isn't spending more than $60. It's also a good gift IEM — plug it into a phone with no EQ and it sounds lively immediately. Skip it if you listen to acoustic, classical, or anything where you want to hear the midrange clearly; the Gate does that job better for the same budget tier.

## Truthear Gate — $79

{{< ref "reviews/truthear-gate-review" >}}

The Gate is the IEM that often gets overlooked because the Hexa gets more press, but it's the better choice for a lot of listeners. Single dynamic driver, warm-neutral tuning, generous low-end without the V-shape exaggeration of the Zero:Red. Vocals sit forward, instruments sound natural, and the treble is smooth enough that you can listen for hours without fatigue. It's the kind of IEM that disappears — you stop thinking about the gear and just hear the music.

Who it's for: listeners who want something that sounds like a well-mastered speaker setup, not an audiophile tool. If you stream pop, R&B, jazz, or indie and just want things to sound right without any EQ fiddling, the Gate does that. It's also the pick if you've tried neutral IEMs and found them clinical. The Gate is warmer and more forgiving without being a bass-head IEM.

## Truthear Hexa — $79

{{< ref "reviews/truthear-hexa-review" >}}

The Hexa is where Crinacle's fingerprints are most visible. It's a 1DD+3BA tribrid tuned to a modified Harman target, and it sounds like what audiophiles mean when they say "reference" — flat-ish bass, clear mids, extended treble that reveals detail without sounding sharp. At $79, it was the benchmark for sub-$100 neutral IEMs when it launched and it still holds that position. Separation is excellent, imaging is precise, and it scales well with better source gear.

The tradeoff is that the Hexa isn't flattering. EQ out of necessity is non-optional if you want warmth; unequalised, it's lean in the low-end and the upper mids can have slight edge on some tracks. This makes it the right pick for mixing or critical listening, and the wrong pick if you're going cable-and-phone with no EQ. At the same $79 as the Gate, the choice is simple: know what you're optimising for. Analytical accuracy → Hexa. Musical enjoyment → Gate.

---

## Truthear Pure — $99

{{< ref "reviews/truthear-pure-review" >}}

The Pure sits in an interesting spot: $20 more than the Gate and Hexa, 1DD+1BA hybrid, and tuned for warmth and vocals specifically. Where the Hexa leans neutral and the Gate leans warm-neutral, the Pure commits to a musical presentation. The upper midrange is elevated in a way that brings vocals to the front of the mix — singers feel present and immediate. Bass is full but controlled. Treble is rolled off enough to be non-fatiguing over long sessions.

It's the pick for vocal-focused listeners: singer-songwriter, acoustic, K-pop, classical voice. If most of your library centres on a human voice and you want that voice to sound real and close, the Pure does that better than anything else in the lineup. The downside is that it gives up some detail retrieval compared to the Hexa, and bass heads may find the low-end warmth doesn't extend to genuine impact. At $99, it competes with the Nova value proposition, which makes the step-up question relevant — but if vocals are the priority, the Pure is tuned more specifically for that than the Nova is.

---

## Truthear Nova — $149

{{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}

The Nova is Truthear's flagship and it earns that title. 1DD+4BA tribrid, tuned to Harman 2019 V2 — which Crinacle considers the best version of the Harman curve — and executed with a level of polish that makes every other model in the lineup feel like a warmup. Bass has genuine extension and weight while staying controlled. The midrange is clean and present. Treble is detailed without grain. Imaging places instruments with confidence. At $149, it competes with IEMs from brands charging $200–$250 for comparable performance.

The Nova is the answer if you want one Truthear that does everything well and don't want to think about it again. It doesn't specialise like the Pure or the Hexa — it's the all-rounder built to a high ceiling. Listeners upgrading from any of the other four models will hear the improvement immediately in the low-end extension and the treble resolution. It's the endgame for this lineup.

## Should you step up?

**Zero:Red → Gate or Hexa ($24 more):** Yes, if you're flexible on sound preference. The jump in technical performance is real. The Zero:Red is for committed bass heads; if there's any doubt, go Gate or Hexa.

**Gate → Pure ($20 more):** Only if vocals are your primary concern. The Gate is not a downgrade from the Pure — it's a different tuning for different use cases. Don't upgrade just because you can.

**Hexa → Nova ($70 more):** Yes, when you're ready. The Nova is clearly better technically. If the Hexa has you wanting more bass extension and more treble refinement, the Nova is the right call. If you're happy with the Hexa's neutrality, stay there.

**Pure → Nova ($50 more):** The Nova is the better all-rounder, but the Pure is more vocal-specific. If vocals are why you bought the Pure, you won't automatically be happier with the Nova. Audition or demo if you can.

## The bottom line

Truthear's lineup is unusually coherent for a brand this young. There's no obvious skip in the five models — each one has a defined audience and delivers on its promise at the price. That said, most people only need one.

If you're new to the brand: start with the Gate if you want something musical and approachable, the Hexa if you want neutral reference, or the Nova if you want the best they make.

If you already own one: resist the urge to collect the lineup. The differences are meaningful but not dramatic enough to justify owning multiples unless you have distinct use cases (analytical work vs. casual listening vs. vocal focus). The Nova is the endgame for this lineup for most people — get there and stay there.
