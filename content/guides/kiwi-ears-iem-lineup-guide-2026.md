Kiwi Ears spent the last couple of years quietly turning into one of the most talked-about names in ChiFi, and in 2026 the "quiet" part stopped applying. The brand showed up everywhere — SoundGuys, Android Authority, Unbox Therapy, ShortCircuit — picked up multiple honors at the VGP 2025 Summer Awards, and co-designed models with respected names in the community like Crinacle and Z Reviews. The pitch that drove the rise is simple: premium driver tech without flagship pricing, backed by a proprietary KARS tuning system and a dedicated EQ platform. The result is a catalog that now stretches from a $10 DAC dongle to a $349 ten-BA flagship.

That breadth is great for the brand and confusing for buyers. This guide cuts through it. We'll focus on the three models most people are actually choosing between in 2026 — the Cadenza II, the Aether, and the Halcyon — and point you to the right one for your budget and your ears.

---

## The Kiwi Ears lineup at a glance

| Model | Price | Driver config | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cadenza II | ~$49 | Single 10mm titanium DD + KARS 2.0 | First "real" ChiFi IEM, clean U-shaped fun |
| Aether | ~$169 | Single 15.3mm planar magnetic | Accessible, musical planar sound |
| Halcyon | ~$259 | MEMS + 1DD + 3BA tribrid | Flagship resolution and treble |

Above and around these sit other models — the budget Cadenza II shares the entry tier with cheaper single-DDs, and the lineup climbs past the Halcyon into all-BA sets like the Septet ($269) and the ten-BA Orchestra II ($349). But for most listeners, the three IEMs above are the decision.

## Kiwi Ears Cadenza II

The [Kiwi Ears Cadenza II review]({{< ref "reviews/kiwi-ears-cadenza-ii-review" >}}) covers this one in full, but the short version: at around $49 it's a single 10mm titanium-coated dynamic driver paired with Kiwi Ears' KARS 2.0 bass-tuning system, which uses an engineered labyrinth-tube network for airflow control. The tuning is a clean, U-shaped signature — boosted, robust sub-bass, a dense and lush midrange with good note weight, a slightly relaxed but clear upper midrange, and smooth, well-extended treble. It's a "larger-than-life" sound rather than a strictly neutral one.

This is the pick for someone buying their first proper ChiFi IEM, or anyone who wants clean bass impact and an easygoing top end without spending much. It's transparent and pleasing with a surprisingly big soundstage for the money. Skip it only if you specifically want flat reference tuning — the Cadenza II is tuned for enjoyment, not measurement.

## Kiwi Ears Aether

The [Kiwi Ears Aether review]({{< ref "reviews/kiwi-ears-aether-review" >}}) goes deep, but here's the gist: at roughly $169, the Aether is built around a single 15.3mm planar magnetic driver with multi-layered N52 neodymium magnets — billed as the largest planar ever put in an IEM at its release. What matters is how it sounds: smooth and musical rather than clinical, with far-reaching sub-bass extension, punchy and textured mid-bass that stays clean rather than meaty, and treble that's generally smooth and refined with minimal sibilance. Crucially, it largely avoids the metallic "planar sheen" that plagues cheaper planars, and the soundstage is unusually wide with real depth.

The Aether is for the listener who wants into planar sound without the harshness or thinness that turns people off it — someone after a smooth all-day listen with great soundstage. The main caveat is fit: the shell is on the larger side and may not suit smaller ears, and the stock cable is unremarkable. For where it sits in the category, see our [best planar IEMs under $300]({{< ref "guides/best-planar-iems-under-300-2026" >}}) guide.

## Kiwi Ears Halcyon

The Halcyon is the current flagship of this group and the most technically ambitious thing Kiwi Ears has shipped: it's billed as the world's first MEMS + 1DD + 3BA tribrid. The configuration pairs a 10mm composite-diaphragm dynamic driver for sub-bass, two custom DEK-series balanced armatures for the midrange, and a custom WBFK-series BA tweeter working alongside a MEMS driver for the very top end. Rated at 29 ohms and 109dB sensitivity, it runs roughly $259 at retail (it started lower as a Kickstarter early-bird).

Tuning is close to a neutral "studio monitor" presentation — linear but powerful bass, open and expressive mids, and treble that's genuinely special for the price thanks to the MEMS driver. Technical performance, build, and that top-end resolution put pricier IEMs on notice. The one trade-off: the mids are slightly recessed, so dedicated vocal-first listeners may want a touch more presence. Our full [Kiwi Ears Halcyon review]({{< ref "reviews/kiwi-ears-halcyon-review" >}}) has the deep dive; if tribrids are your thing in general, our [best tribrid IEMs]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-tribrid-iems" >}}) guide has the broader context.

## Which Kiwi Ears should you buy?

### On a tight budget

Get the Cadenza II. At around $49 it's the easiest recommendation in the lineup — a clean, bassy, U-shaped single DD that sounds good straight out of a phone with no EQ. It's the natural starting point and holds its own against anything in our [best ChiFi IEMs under $50]({{< ref "guides/best-chifi-iems-under-50-2026" >}}) roundup. Buy it, enjoy it, and only think about climbing the ladder once you know what you'd want to change.

### For planar sound

Go straight to the Aether. The whole point of this model is delivering planar speed and resolution in a musical, non-fatiguing package, and it does that better than most planars at its price. If you've been burned by harsh or thin budget planars before, this is the one to reset your expectations with — just confirm the larger shell will fit your ears first.

### For the flagship experience

The Halcyon. It's the most resolving Kiwi Ears most people will consider, with a near-neutral tuning and a MEMS-driven treble that's the headline feature. At ~$259 it's the move if you want the brand's technical ceiling and you're comfortable with a slightly polite midrange. If you're more vocal-centric, the warmer Aether may actually satisfy you more day to day despite costing less.

## How Kiwi Ears compares to Moondrop & Truthear

Against the two brands ChiFi Wire covers most, Kiwi Ears slots in as the more adventurous of the three on driver tech. Moondrop is the tuning-first brand — Harman-leaning, consistent, design-driven (see the [Moondrop lineup guide]({{< ref "guides/moondrop-iem-lineup-complete-guide-2026" >}})). Truthear is the value-reference specialist, with a coherent ladder from the Zero:Red to the Nova and Crinacle's fingerprints on several models (the [Truthear lineup guide]({{< ref "guides/truthear-iem-lineup-complete-guide-2026" >}}) has the full breakdown).

Kiwi Ears differentiates by chasing novel configurations — the largest-in-class planar in the Aether, the first MEMS tribrid in the Halcyon — and by tuning for musicality more than strict reference. If you want a safe, proven neutral set, Moondrop or Truthear are the obvious calls. If you want something that feels a little more cutting-edge for the money, Kiwi Ears is where to look.

## Bottom line

Kiwi Ears' 2026 lineup is broad, but the decision usually isn't. Spending around $50, the Cadenza II is the easy answer — clean, fun, and a great first ChiFi IEM. Want planar sound done right without the harshness, the Aether at ~$169 is the smart entry. Chasing the brand's technical ceiling, the Halcyon at ~$259 delivers flagship resolution and a standout MEMS treble, with a slightly polite midrange as the only real caveat.

Most people need exactly one of these. Match the price tier to your budget, match the tuning to your ears, and resist the urge to collect the lineup.
