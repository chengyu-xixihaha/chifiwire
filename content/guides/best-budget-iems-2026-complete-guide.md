Budget IEM recommendations go stale fast. A pick that was the clear winner 18 months ago might now be outclassed at the same price. This guide is current as of mid-2026, covering the tiers where most people actually buy — $20 to $150 — with honest notes on what changed and what's still holding up.

---

## At $20–$30: Three Distinct Characters

The sub-$30 tier is the most competitive it has ever been. Three IEMs stand out for different reasons:

### Moondrop Chu 2 (~$20) — Most Versatile
Detachable cable at $20 is still unusual. The Chu 2's mild Harman-adjacent tuning works across genres without fatiguing: elevated but controlled bass, natural midrange, non-aggressive treble. If you're buying someone their first serious IEM or want a low-risk recommendation, this is it.

### Truthear Gate (~$22) — Best With Mic / Best for Vocals
The Gate is brighter and more upper-midrange forward than the Chu 2. Vocals sit closer and more present in the mix. The included cable with inline microphone is the only option in this price range worth using for calls. Detachable 0.78mm 2-pin cable is a bonus.

### 7Hz Salnotes Zero 2 (~$30) — Best Bass
Sub-bass extension and physical impact that competes with IEMs at twice the price. V-shaped tuning (bass up, midrange slightly recessed) suits EDM, hip-hop, and electronic music. Not ideal for acoustic or classical. Cable is fixed, which is the main downside.

**Who should buy what:** Default → Chu 2. Need a mic → Gate. Listen to bass-heavy music → Zero 2.

## At $30–$60: Technicalities Start to Matter

### Simgot EW200 (~$29) — Most Technical Under $40
Neutral-bright tuning with the best imaging and separation at this price. The most "audiophile" sounding pick under $50 — instruments are placed more precisely, busy passages separate more cleanly. The trade-off: lean bass, bright treble can fatigue over long sessions. Not the most immediately enjoyable, but the most technically revealing.

### Truthear Nova (~$79) — Best Vocals Under $80
Dual dynamic driver (10mm + 6mm) gives the Nova a smoothness through the upper midrange that single-DD competitors rarely achieve. Vocals are the highlight — genuine texture and body. Asks more from your source (32Ω) but any decent phone output handles it. Best for singer-songwriter, jazz, acoustic.

---

## At $80–$100: The Aria 2 Standard

### Moondrop Aria 2 (~$89) — Best All-Rounder
The Aria 2 is the standard recommendation for a reason. Neutral-bright Harman-adjacent tuning in a zinc alloy shell, 10mm dynamic driver, 0.78mm 2-pin cable that's actually usable. Versatile enough for any genre, honest enough to serve as a reference.

If you've heard the sub-$30 tier and want to know what a meaningful upgrade sounds like, the Aria 2 is where most of the gap gets closed. The jump from Chu 2 to Aria 2 is audible; the jump from Aria 2 to $200 options is smaller.

### Simgot EA500 LM (~$99) — Most Technical Under $100
The EA500 LM (Low Mass diaphragm) prioritizes resolution and imaging over warmth. Better than the Aria 2 at separating instruments and imaging precisely; slightly leaner in the bass. Best for classical, jazz, and orchestral. Can be fatiguing for treble-sensitive listeners.

---

## At $100–$150: Diminishing Returns Begin

### 7Hz Legato (~$139) — Best Bass Under $150
Beryllium-coated 12mm dynamic driver with noticeably faster transient response than conventional polymer drivers. W-shaped tuning (elevated bass, slight mid-dip, airy treble). For listeners who've tried the Aria 2 and want more energy and bass physicality rather than more neutrality. Not a reference IEM — a fun one.

### Tanchjim Origin (~$129) — Best Midrange Under $150
Splits the difference between the Aria 2's neutrality and the Nova's vocal warmth. Resolution approaches the EA500 LM without the tonal leanness. Best for vocal and acoustic music. The pick if you've tried the Nova and want more technical performance in the same character.

## What's Changed in 2026

**Dropped from recommendations:**
- Most KZ multi-driver hybrids (ZSN Pro X, ZS10 Pro, CCA CA16 Pro): outclassed by better-tuned alternatives at the same price. V-shaped tuning with sibilance issues has not aged well.
- TRN budget hybrids: similar situation. Single DD alternatives at $20-$30 now measure better and sound more coherent.

**Still worth buying:**
- Truthear Zero Red (~$55): warm V-shape with elevated bass, good for casual listening, but the Nova outperforms it for most use cases at similar money.
