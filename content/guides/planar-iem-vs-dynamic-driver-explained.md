If you've spent any time reading audiophile reviews, you've seen the terms **"planar magnetic"** and **"dynamic driver"** thrown around. Reviewers will say things like "the planar driver delivers fast transients" or "the dynamic driver's natural timbre" — but rarely explain **what these actually mean** or **how they audibly differ**.

This guide is for the buyer who's about to spend $80-$200 on an IEM and wants to understand **which driver type fits their listening style** before clicking buy.

## How each driver works (the simple version)

### Dynamic driver (DD)

A **dynamic driver** is essentially a tiny speaker. You have:
- A circular **diaphragm** (the moving part)
- A **voice coil** glued to the back of the diaphragm
- A **magnet** surrounding the voice coil
- When current flows through the coil, the magnetic field pushes/pulls the coil, which moves the diaphragm, which moves air, which makes sound

**Used in**: every speaker you've ever owned, most headphones, most IEMs, your phone speaker, every car stereo.

**Typical IEM DD size**: 6-12mm diameter

### Planar magnetic driver

A **planar magnetic driver** flips the design. Instead of a circular diaphragm with a coil in the center, you have:
- A **flat, thin film diaphragm** (usually 14-15mm for IEMs)
- **Voice coil traces printed directly onto the diaphragm** (not glued separately)
- **Two arrays of magnets** sandwiching the diaphragm front and back
- Current through the traces creates magnetic force across the **entire surface** of the diaphragm, moving it as one flat sheet

**Used in**: HiFiMan, Audeze headphones; Letshuoer S12 Pro / S15, Tin HiFi P2 Plus, 7Hz Timeless IEMs.

**Typical IEM planar size**: 12-15mm (much larger surface area than DD)

## The audible differences

### Speed (transient response)

**Planar wins decisively**. The diaphragm has very low mass (it's a thin film) and is driven across its entire surface uniformly — so it starts and stops moving very fast. Result:

- **Fast drum hits stay distinct** (matters for death metal, fast EDM, complex orchestral)
- **No "smearing"** of bass into mids on busy passages
- **Cleaner attack** on guitar strings, piano notes

DD speed depends on diaphragm material (beryllium / DLC / ceramic-coated dynamics are faster than basic polymer DDs).

### Bass

**Planar has more sub-bass extension** but **less mid-bass slam**. The large surface area moves a lot of air for deep frequencies but doesn't punch the way a focused DD does.

- **Planar bass**: deep, textured, controlled — feels "speaker-like" or "subwoofer-like"
- **DD bass**: punchy, slammy, focused — feels "alive" or "energetic"

For **EDM with sub-bass drops**, planar shines. For **rock kick drums**, DD often feels better.

### Midrange timbre

**DD typically wins on naturalness**. Single-DD IEMs have a "coherent" midrange because one driver covers it all. Planar mids are also clean but some listeners find them slightly "synthetic" or "thin" by comparison.

For **vocal music, acoustic, jazz**, single-DD is usually preferred over planar.

### Treble

**Planar wins on extension and detail**. The flat diaphragm reproduces upper frequencies more accurately than a circular DD. Cymbals, hi-hats, brushed snares — all sound cleaner on planar.

The trade-off: **planar treble can be aggressive** if not carefully tuned. Letshuoer S12 Pro and Tin HiFi P2 (original) both had upper-treble peaks that some found fatiguing.

### Soundstage and imaging

Roughly **tie**. Both can deliver wide stages — depends more on tuning and shell design than driver type.

### Source requirements

**Planar usually needs more power**. Lower sensitivity (often 95-105 dB/Vrms) means:
- Phone headphone jack = underpowered, planar will sound thin and lifeless
- Dongle DAC ($30+) = minimum acceptable
- DAP or desktop amp = where planar shines

**DD typically drives easily from anything**. Phone, dongle, basic DAP — all work.

If you only listen from a phone, **a single-DD IEM will outperform a planar IEM at the same price**, simply because the planar isn't getting the power it needs.

## When to pick which

### Pick **dynamic driver (DD)** if:

- You **only have a phone or basic dongle** as source
- You listen primarily to **vocals, jazz, acoustic, rock**
- You want **natural timbre** over technical performance
- You're new to ChiFi (DD is more forgiving overall)
- You like **mid-bass slam** over sub-bass extension

### Pick **planar magnetic** if:

- You have a **real source** (DAP or dongle DAC with 100+ mW output)
- You listen primarily to **modern classical, electronic, complex prog, dense metal**
- You want **maximum detail and speed**
- You're upgrading from a single-DD IEM and want a meaningful step up
- You enjoy **deep, textured sub-bass** over mid-bass punch

### Hybrid (DD + BA) is a third option

Some IEMs combine **a dynamic driver for bass + balanced armatures for mids and treble** — this gives you DD bass quality + BA treble extension. Examples:

- [Truthear Hexa]({{< ref "vs/truthear-hexa-vs-moondrop-aria-2" >}}) (1DD + 3BA, $79)
- [Truthear Nova]({{< ref "reviews/truthear-nova-review" >}}) (1DD + 4BA, $150)
- [Letshuoer Cadenza 12]({{< ref "reviews/letshuoer-cadenza-12-review" >}}) (1DD + 11BA, $1,999)

Hybrids try to **get the best of both worlds**. Trade-off: BAs sometimes have a "plasticky" character that doesn't fully match the DD's natural timbre.

## Common myths

**"Planar is always better."** False. Planar wins on speed and detail; DD often wins on timbre naturalness and source-friendliness.

**"Hybrid is always best."** False. Hybrids can have BA timbre issues; well-tuned single-DD or planar often sound more coherent.

**"More drivers = better sound."** False. Driver count is marketing. A well-tuned single 10mm DD beats a poorly-tuned 12-driver IEM.

**"Burn-in matters for planars."** Mostly false. Planar drivers may settle slightly in the first 20-50 hours but the change is small. Most "burn-in" is your brain adapting to the sound.

**"Planar needs $500+ amps."** False. A $9-30 dongle DAC is usually enough for sub-$300 planars like the S12 Pro. Bigger amps add headroom for full-size planar headphones, not IEMs.
