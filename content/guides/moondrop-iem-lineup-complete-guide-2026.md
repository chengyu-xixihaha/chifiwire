Moondrop is one of the few ChiFi brands that actually means something. Not because of hype cycles or influencer deals, but because they've been making consistent, well-tuned IEMs since the Spaceship era and haven't stopped. Their house tuning target — VDSF (Virtual Diffuse Sound Field) and more recently a refined variant of it — anchors the single DD lineup in a warm-to-neutral signature that sounds natural and non-fatiguing. The result is a brand where almost any pick from the lineup is a safe one, and the upgrade path from $20 to $319 is coherent rather than chaotic.

By 2026, Moondrop has five IEMs worth serious consideration: the Chu 2, Aria 2, VOID, Kato, and Blessing 3. They span $20 to $319 and cover everything from budget-first to tribrid flagship. This guide is about figuring out which one to buy — and just as importantly, which ones to skip.

| Budget | What you want | Pick |

If you don't know where to start: Chu 2 if you're on a hard budget, VOID if you want to stop thinking about single DDs, Blessing 3 if you want the best Moondrop makes and don't mind paying for it.

## Moondrop Chu 2 — $20

{{< ref "reviews/moondrop-chu-2-review" >}}

The Chu 2 is what happens when a brand decides to actually try at $20. Single dynamic driver, VDSF-tuned, build quality that embarrasses most IEMs in the $40–$60 range. The sound is neutral with a mild warmth — there's a gentle low-end lift that adds body without becoming a bass shelf, and the treble is extended enough to pick up detail without any sharpness. Vocals sit naturally in the mix. At this price, there's essentially nothing that competes with it on tuning quality.

This one is for anyone who needs an IEM under $25 and doesn't want to compromise. It's also the correct answer for anyone buying their first "serious" IEM to understand what well-tuned sounds like before spending more. The only real limitations are what you'd expect at $20: staging is intimate, resolution tops out at good-for-price, and the cable is basic. If you know you want warmth and musicality rather than strict neutrality, step up to the Aria 2 — but if budget is the constraint, the Chu 2 won't embarrass you.

## Moondrop Aria 2 — $89

{{< ref "reviews/moondrop-aria-2-review" >}}

The Aria 2 is the Moondrop for listeners who want to enjoy music without overthinking it. Single dynamic driver with a warm-neutral tuning that sits between the Chu 2's strict neutrality and a full-blown musical V-shape. Bass has genuine weight — more than the Chu 2, and more than most Harman-tuned IEMs at this price — while the midrange stays clear and the treble is smooth enough for long sessions. The modular cable is a legitimately useful feature, not a checkbox: 3.5mm and 4.4mm swaps mean it works with your current source and any future balanced DAC/amp setup.

This is the pick for daily driver listeners who know warm sounds better to them than flat, but don't want to go full bass-head. It pairs well with vocal music, R&B, indie, and anything where you want the music to feel warm and present rather than analysed and dissected. The Aria 2 is also the upgrade that makes sense if you've lived with the Chu 2 and want more bass body and better physical package without jumping to $149 and above.

## Moondrop VOID — $149

{{< ref "reviews/moondrop-void-review" >}}

The VOID uses Moondrop's LCP (Liquid Crystal Polymer) diaphragm — a material choice that gives the driver faster transient response and tighter bass control than conventional DD configurations at this price. The sound lands warm-neutral, closer to the Aria 2 than to anything analytical, but with noticeably better detail retrieval and a more controlled low-end. Staging opens up compared to both the Chu 2 and Aria 2. Like the Aria 2, it ships with a modular cable that terminates in both 3.5mm and 4.4mm. At $149, it's the single DD endgame for most listeners who don't want to go further up the price stack.

Buy the VOID if you want to stop thinking about single DD IEMs below $200. It resolves better than the Aria 2, it's more technically capable than anything else Moondrop makes under $150, and the tuning is warm enough to stay musical rather than clinical. The competition from Truthear at this price (specifically the [Moondrop VOID vs Truthear Nova]({{< ref "vs/moondrop-void-vs-truthear-nova" >}})) is real — the Nova is more neutral and arguably technically comparable — so if you're choosing between those two, it comes down to tuning preference: VOID for warm and musical, Nova for neutral and precise.

## Moondrop Kato — $189

{{< ref "reviews/moondrop-kato-review" >}}

The Kato is where Moondrop's single DD engineering peaks. The STEC (Super-Linear Transducer with Enhanced Compliance) driver is the most technically refined dynamic driver the brand has built — it has a wider dynamic range, more precise transient attack, and cleaner distortion characteristics than the LCP driver in the VOID. The tuning is warm-neutral as expected from the lineup, but with a transparency and resolution that gets genuinely close to hybrid and tribrid territory. It's $40 more than the VOID, and the gap is audible.

Who should buy the Kato over the VOID: listeners who consider single DD coherence and timbre non-negotiable, have heard the VOID and want the next level of resolution while staying in a pure DD configuration, and aren't ready to jump to the Blessing 3's price or multi-driver complexity. The Kato is also the right call if you're using it with a proper DAC/amp setup — it scales with source quality in ways that the VOID only partially does. If you're plugging straight into a phone, the VOID's value-per-dollar is harder to argue against.

## Moondrop Blessing 3 — $319

{{< ref "reviews/moondrop-blessing-3-review" >}}

The Blessing 3 is a different category of IEM. One dynamic driver plus four balanced armatures in a tribrid configuration, tuned to Moondrop's Harman-adjacent target — this is the analytical, technically-focused flagship, not a warm musical expansion of the single DD lineup. Bass is tight and controlled rather than full and organic. The midrange has a precision that the single DDs can't match. Treble extends cleanly with detail that reveals things in recordings you won't catch on anything cheaper. Imaging and staging are in a different league from the $20–$189 models below it.

The Blessing 3 is for listeners who have been through the single DD lineup and want the ceiling pushed higher. It's also for anyone coming from the Blessing 2 or Blessing 2: Dusk who wants a modern Harman target rather than the DF-tuned predecessor. One honest caveat: the character change is significant. If you love the warm musicality of the Aria 2 or VOID, the Blessing 3 will feel more analytical and demanding. That's the intended direction — but know what you're buying before you do it.

## The Blessing 2: Dusk — what happened to it?

The Blessing 2: Dusk was the crinacle-tuned predecessor to the Blessing 3, tuned to his personal DF (Diffuse Field) target rather than Harman. It's no longer in current production but remains widely available used at $100–$130. If you want an analytical, reference-class IEM at a sharp used price, it's still one of the best sub-$150 options in ChiFi — reviewed on site at {{< ref "reviews/moondrop-blessing-2-dusk-review" >}}. The Blessing 3 supersedes it technically, but the Dusk has its own character that some listeners prefer for critical listening and mixing work.

## How Moondrop scales

The single DD lineup — Chu 2, Aria 2, VOID, Kato — follows a consistent path: each step up adds technical performance (resolution, staging, dynamics) while keeping the warm-neutral tuning character largely intact. You don't change sound signatures as you move up; you get more of the same done better.

The jump to the Blessing 3 is different. This isn't just "more VOID" — it's a different architectural approach (multi-driver tribrid vs. single DD), a different tuning philosophy (Harman vs. VDSF-warm), and a different listening experience (analytical clarity vs. musical warmth). Think of it as a second branch of the lineup rather than a continuation.

What you gain at each tier:
- **Chu 2 → Aria 2:** Bass body, better build, modular cable, more organic presentation
- **Aria 2 → VOID:** LCP driver speed, tighter bass control, better detail retrieval, equivalent modular cable
- **VOID → Kato:** STEC driver precision, more resolution, closer to tribrid technicalities in a pure DD
- **Kato → Blessing 3:** Multi-driver architecture, analytical Harman tuning, significantly better staging and imaging, tribrid complexity

## Which Moondrop should you buy?

**Strict budget limit of $20–$25:** Chu 2. Nothing competes at the price.

**Want warm and musical for under $100:** Aria 2. The modular cable alone justifies the step over the Chu 2, and the tuning is exactly right for long-session listening.

**Want single DD endgame without breaking $200:** VOID at $149. Clear pick.

**Want the absolute best single DD and can stretch to $189:** Kato. Worth the $40 premium over the VOID if source quality and DD purist preferences are part of the picture.

**Want Moondrop's flagship regardless of price:** Blessing 3. Know that you're trading warmth for analytical precision.

**Working with a used budget of $100–$130 and want analytical tuning:** Blessing 2: Dusk. Still excellent, still worth finding.
